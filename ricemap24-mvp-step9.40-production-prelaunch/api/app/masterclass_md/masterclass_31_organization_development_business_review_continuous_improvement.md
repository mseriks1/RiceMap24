## Big idea (general business first): your business is a system

Most entrepreneurs think growth is:
- more marketing
- more posting
- more hours
- more menu items

Sometimes that helps short-term.
But long-term, most growth comes from one thing:

**a better system.**

A system is simply:
- how you get customers
- how you produce the product
- how you deliver it
- how you get repeat orders
- how you manage money and time

When the system improves, results improve automatically.
When the system stays messy, you can work twice as hard and still feel stuck.

So organizational development (even for a one-person home kitchen) is:
- making the business more stable
- making quality more consistent
- making profit more predictable
- making your life easier

This masterclass is your “business review engine”.

# Part 2 — The evaluation mindset: results are signals, not emotions

Most small businesses evaluate like this:
- “This week felt hard.”
- “I posted a lot.”
- “I think people didn’t care.”

That’s emotional data, not business data.

A better approach:
**Results are signals.**

If you had fewer orders, something changed:
- visibility
- offer clarity
- schedule
- value perception
- trust
- operational capacity

The point of evaluation is not to blame yourself.
The point is to learn:

**What worked? What didn’t? What will we change next week?**

This is how businesses compound.

# Part 5 — Weekly review (15 minutes): “Stop / Start / Continue”

This is the simplest evaluation routine that works.

Every week, ask:

## Continue (what worked)
- Which post converted?
- Which dish sold best?
- Which pickup window worked?
- Which packaging system prevented leaks?

## Stop (what didn’t work)
- Which dish created stress or complaints?
- Which content got attention but no orders?
- Which process created mistakes?

## Start (one new improvement)
- one new checklist
- one packaging upgrade
- one clearer menu description
- one better cutoff policy

**Rule:** only choose one “Start” improvement per week.
Small improvements compound. Too many changes create chaos.

# Part 6 — Monthly review (60 minutes): “the 6-area scan”

Once per month, do a deeper scan across the 6 areas.

## 1) Offer & positioning
- Do we have a clear identity line?
- Do we have 1–2 banker dishes that sell repeatedly?
- Is the menu too long?
- Are bundles increasing AOV?

## 2) Marketing & distribution
- What channel brought orders (Instagram, Facebook, local partner, QR)?
- Are we posting consistently (weekly rhythm)?
- Is our ordering method clear?
- Did we try one new distribution move?

## 3) Operations & quality
- Where did mistakes happen?
- Are we using SOPs and checklists consistently?
- Any food safety risks in summer?
- Are we improving speed?

## 4) Customer experience
- Is pickup smooth?
- Are instructions clear?
- Are we communicating warmly but clearly?

## 5) Finance & pricing
- Is profit per hour improving?
- Are ingredient costs rising?
- Are we pricing bundles correctly?
- Do we have a buffer?

## 6) Owner sustainability
- Are we burned out?
- Is the schedule realistic?
- Do we need a helper or collaboration?

Monthly reviews prevent slow drift.

# Part 8 — Root cause tools (non-corporate, practical)

When something goes wrong, don’t just say “we need to try harder.”
Find the cause.

## Tool 1 — The “5 Whys” (simple)
Problem: “We had 3 leaking orders.”

Why? Sauce cups leaked.  
Why? Lids were loose.  
Why? Wrong cup type.  
Why? We bought cheap cups.  
Why? We didn’t have a packaging standard.

Fix: standardize sauce cups + shake test + packing checklist.

## Tool 2 — The “bottleneck map”
Where is time stuck?
- prep
- cooking
- packing
- pickup communication

Fix the bottleneck first.
That gives the biggest speed improvement.

## Tool 3 — “Error log”
Write down every mistake type weekly.
After 4 weeks, you’ll see patterns.
Then you solve one pattern at a time.

Patterns are gold.

# Part 9 — The leverage map (what to improve first for profit)

Not all improvements matter equally.
Use the leverage map:

## High leverage improvements (usually)
1) Shorter menu (less complexity, fewer mistakes)
2) Better bundles (higher AOV)
3) Better repeat system (higher retention)
4) Better packaging (fewer complaints, fewer refunds)
5) Better pickup window clarity (less waiting, more trust)
6) Better prep system (time savings)

Low leverage improvements:
- redesigning logo
- endless new dishes
- random posting without CTA

If you want more profit, focus on high leverage.

# Part 17 — Customer journey evaluation (how to find hidden friction)

Customers don’t see your effort.
They experience a journey.

Evaluate your customer journey in 7 steps:

1) **Discovery**
How do they first see you? (post, flyer, friend, partner)

2) **Understanding**
Do they understand what you sell? (identity line, photo, “served cold by design”, spice)

3) **Decision**
Do they have an easy choice? (short menu, bundles)

4) **Ordering**
Is ordering simple? (DM template, link, form)

5) **Confirmation**
Do they feel safe? (clear pickup time, clear address instructions)

6) **Pickup**
Is it fast and clean?

7) **After experience**
Do they know when to reorder? (next pickup window)

### Journey audit questions
- Where do we lose people?
- What questions do people ask repeatedly?
- What causes last-minute confusion?

Every repeated question is an opportunity for:
- a pinned post
- a template message
- a clearer menu line

This is how you reduce friction and increase conversion.

# Part 18 — Menu engineering review (profit + simplicity)

Menu engineering is how you decide:
- what to keep
- what to remove
- what to highlight

For a home kitchen, use a simple method.

## Step 1: classify dishes by 2 variables
1) Popularity (sales volume)
2) Ease/profitability (time + cost)

Create 4 groups:

### Group A — Stars
High sales, easy/profitable.
These are your banker dishes.
Protect them.

### Group B — Workhorses
High sales, but stressful.
These need system improvements:
- batch cooking
- better prep
- simpler plating

### Group C — Potential
Low sales but easy/profitable.
These need better marketing:
- photos
- naming
- positioning

### Group D — Problem dishes
Low sales and stressful.
Remove them.

This is not emotional.
It’s strategy.

Every extra menu item costs you:

So part of organizational development is:

# Part 19 — Pricing audit (are you paid for your effort?)

Many home kitchens underprice because:
- they compare to takeaway
- they feel guilty charging “too much”
- they forget packaging and time

Pricing is a strategy decision.
If pricing is wrong, you can work a lot and still feel broke.

## Pricing audit questions
- Do we cover ingredient cost comfortably?
- Do we cover packaging cost?
- Do we cover our time (profit per hour)?
- Are we pricing bundles to feel smart?
- Are we giving away value without charging?

### The “confidence price” concept
A price that makes you feel resentful is too low.
A price that makes you feel proud is usually closer to correct.

If you feel you must apologize for the price, it’s often underpriced.
If you deliver premium quality and clean experience, you can price accordingly.
