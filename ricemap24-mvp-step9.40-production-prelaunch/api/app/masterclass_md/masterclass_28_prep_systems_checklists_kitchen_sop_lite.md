## What SOP means (in normal words)

SOP = Standard Operating Procedure.

In normal words:
- a fixed work routine
- a standard way you do things
- a checklist that helps you repeat quality

For a home kitchen, a Kitchen SOP can be a simple guide that says:

**Shopping → Prep → Cooking → Packing → Pickup/Delivery → After work**

…with 3–10 bullet points under each.

That’s it.

## 1) Weekly planning (15 min)

This is where stability begins.
If you plan well, everything else becomes easier.

### Your weekly planning checklist (15 minutes)

**A) Choose your sales day + pickup window**
Pick one main pickup window.
Example:
- Saturday 16:00–18:00  
or  
- Sunday 14:00–16:00

Keep it consistent.
Consistency makes customers confident.

**B) Decide max orders (protect quality)**
Choose a hard limit.
Example:
- 20 orders max  
- 30 portions max  
- 12 family trays max  

You can increase later.
But start with a limit that feels safe.

**C) Choose “menu for this week” (simple structure)**
Use this trio:

1) **Signature dish** (your hero)
2) **Banker dish** (easy, stable, high margin)
3) **Add-ons** (spring rolls, dessert, sauce, rice)

Keep it small.
Small menu = faster prep + fewer mistakes.

**D) Decide your shopping day**
Example:
- Thursday evening shopping
- Friday morning shopping

**E) Decide your prep blocks**
Example:
- Friday: sauces + marinating + chopping
- Saturday: cook + pack

**F) Write your “stress note”**
One sentence:
“What is the one thing that usually stresses me?”

Then decide one fix for this week.

### Weekly planning (simple example)
- Pickup: Sat 16–18
- Max: 25 orders
- Menu: adobo + pancit + spring rolls + dessert
- Shopping: Thu
- Prep: Fri 18–21
- Cook/Pack: Sat 10–15
- Stress note: “Sauce leaks” → fix: better cups + wipe edges

That’s a business move.

## 2) Shopping SOP

Shopping is not just “buy ingredients”.
Shopping is a quality system.

A good shopping SOP prevents:
- missing items
- last-minute panic
- cold chain issues
- recipe inconsistency

### Shopping SOP checklist

**A) Use a template**
Create a standard list with categories:
- Proteins
- Vegetables
- Dry goods
- Spices / sauces
- Packaging
- Extras (napkins, labels)
- Backup items

**B) Must-buy + Backup**
For each critical ingredient, define:
- must-buy (main choice)
- backup (second option)

Example:
- Must-buy: chicken thighs  
- Backup: chicken drumsticks or tofu option

This keeps you calm when stores are out of stock.

**C) The cold chain basics (home kitchen version)**
Cold chain simply means:
**keep cold items cold and safe.**

Simple rules:
- buy cold items last
- bring insulated bag/cooler if possible
- go home quickly
- store immediately
- keep raw meat separate from ready-to-eat items

You don’t need to be paranoid.
You just need a calm system.

**D) Packaging stock check**
Before you shop, check packaging:
- containers
- sauce cups
- bags
- labels
- tape

Packaging shortages create chaos on packing day.
This is a common failure point.

**E) “One extra” rule**
Buy one extra of your critical items when possible:
- extra sauce cups
- extra bags
- extra rice
- extra labels

This is cheap insurance.

## 3) Prep SOP

Prep is where speed is created.
If prep is clean, cooking and packing become calm.

### Prep SOP checklist

**A) Clean zone setup**
Start with a clean surface.
Then define zones:
- clean tools area
- raw area
- cooked/ready area

**B) Raw vs finished separation**
Simple home kitchen rule:
- raw food stays in one area
- cooked food stays in another
- avoid cross-contact

**C) Mise en place (the “pro secret”)**
Mise en place means:
**everything ready before cooking.**

Prep your:
- chopped vegetables
- measured spices
- sauces and marinades
- containers and sauce cups
- labels
- utensils

Put everything into small boxes.
When cooking starts, your brain is calm.

**D) Prep packaging early**
Pre-open containers.
Stack them.
Prepare sauce cups.
This reduces packing chaos later.

**E) Label system**
Even if it’s simple:
- dish name
- spicy level (if needed)
- allergen note (if needed)

Labels reduce mistakes and complaints.

---

## 4) Cook SOP

Cooking SOP is about:
- batch rounds
- quality checkpoints
- safe temperature habits (without stress)

### Cook SOP checklist

**A) Batch rounds (don’t cook everything at once)**
Break cooking into rounds.
Example:
- Round 1: rice + base sauce
- Round 2: main protein dish
- Round 3: add-ons

Rounds prevent overwhelm.

**B) Taste check checkpoints**
Your “taste check” should be scheduled, not random.

Taste checks:
- after sauce is built
- after protein is added
- before final seasoning
- before packing

If taste is consistent, reputation grows.

**C) Doneness checkpoints**
Have 2–3 clear checkpoints.
Example:
- chicken fully cooked
- noodles not mushy
- vegetables not overcooked

Simple, consistent.

**D) Hold hot / cool fast (home kitchen principle)**
The principle:
- keep hot food hot (until packing)
- if cooling, cool fast and store safely

You don’t need complex equipment.
Just a habit:
- don’t leave food sitting in “warm zone” for long
- pack or cool with a plan

---

## 5) Packing SOP

Packing is where most mistakes happen.
Because it is fast, repetitive, and detail-based.

So packing should be an assembly line.

### Packing SOP checklist

**A) Build an assembly line**
Set up stations:
1) containers open + ready
2) portioning
3) sauce cups
4) wipe edges + close
5) label
6) bag + name
7) final check

**B) Portion method**
Choose one method and stick to it:
- measuring cup
- scale
- “standard scoop”
Consistency is more important than perfection.

**C) Sauce separation**
If sauce can leak, separate it.
- sauce in cups
- main food dry

**D) Wipe edges**
A tiny detail, big impact.
Clean edges = professional impression.

**E) Name on bag**
Always.
Even for family.
Mistakes happen easily during pickup.

**F) Final check rule**
Before bagging:
- right dish?
- right add-ons?
- utensils/napkins?
- special notes?

This is your “mistake prevention gate”.

## 6) Pickup/Delivery SOP

Pickup is where customers judge your “business level”.

### Pickup/Delivery SOP checklist

**A) Message templates**
Use templates so you don’t type new messages every time.

Templates:
- order confirmation
- pickup reminder
- “we’re ready”
- “running 10 minutes late”
- “sold out”
(Templates provided below.)

**B) Late customer rule**
Set a calm rule.
Example:
- we hold food for 20 minutes
- after that, we may need to reheat or reschedule

**C) Sold out & waitlist**
Sold out is good.
It creates demand.

Simple system:
- “We are fully booked this week”
- “Want to be on the waitlist for next pickup?”

**D) Delivery basics (if you deliver)**
Keep it simple:
- set zones
- set time window
- don’t promise exact minute delivery unless you can

## 7) When things go wrong SOP

This is the SOP that protects your reputation.

Your response should be:
- calm
- respectful
- practical
- fair
- consistent

### A simple “problem ladder”
Use this 4-step ladder:

1) **Acknowledge** (“Thank you for telling me.”)
2) **Clarify** (what happened, photo if needed)
3) **Fix** (refund/freebie/replace)
4) **Improve** (internal note for next week)

---

### Scenario 1 — Missing item

**Response template:**
> I’m so sorry about that — thank you for telling me.  
> Can you confirm your name and what was missing?  
> I can either refund the missing item or add it for free next pickup — whatever you prefer.

**Internal fix note:**
- add “add-on final check” before bagging

---

### Scenario 2 — Leak

**Response template:**
> Ah no — I’m sorry. Thank you for letting me know.  
> If you can send a quick photo, I’ll fix it properly.  
> I can refund the sauce/add-on and I’ll also add a free add-on next time.

**Internal fix note:**
- better cups / double lid / wipe edges / separate bag

---

### Scenario 3 — Cold food complaint

**Response template:**
> Thank you for telling me — I’m sorry that happened.  
> Was this pickup or delivery, and about how long after pickup did you eat it?  
> I’ll make it right: partial refund or a free replacement next time.

**Internal fix note:**
- better hold-hot method / clearer pickup timing / insulated bag for delivery

---

### Scenario 4 — Sickness claim (calm, serious)

**Response template:**
> Thank you for telling me. I’m really sorry to hear that.  
> Your health is the most important thing.  
> Can you share what you ordered and when you ate it?  
> I will take this seriously and review our full preparation process immediately.

**Internal steps:**
- write down order details
- review shopping/storage/prep/cook/pack steps
- pause selling that item if needed until you understand
- stay calm and respectful

---

### Refund / freebie ladder (simple and fair)

1) Small issue → free add-on next time  
2) Medium issue → partial refund + free add-on  
3) Big issue → full refund + priority replacement next pickup

Fix: keep one signature dish stable and repeat it.

3) **No limits**

## Make it “SOP Lite” (not SOP heavy)

Many people fail with SOPs because they try to write a big manual.
That’s not the goal.

**Kitchen SOP Lite** is built on three rules:

### Rule 1 — Only write what you actually repeat
If you do it every week, it belongs in the SOP.
If you do it once per year, keep it out.

### Rule 2 — Bullets, not paragraphs
SOPs must be skimmable.
If you need to read long text, you won’t use it on a busy day.

### Rule 3 — One page per phase
If the checklist is too long, split it.
One page for Shopping. One for Prep. One for Packing. etc.

This makes it feel easy.

## The “Quality Triangle” (how SOPs protect reputation)

Quality is not only about taste.
Customers experience quality as three things:

1) **Taste** (flavor, seasoning, balance)  
2) **Portion** (fairness, consistency, “I feel satisfied”)  
3) **Delivery condition** (heat, leak-free, presentation)

Your SOP should protect all three.

### Simple question
When a customer complains, it is usually one of these three.

So your SOP Lite should include:
- taste checkpoints (taste)
- portion method (portion)
- packing rules (delivery condition)

When these three are stable, your kitchen becomes trusted.

## Prep SOP — The clean zone setup (simple but powerful)

Clean zones sound boring.
But this is where mistakes and safety issues usually start.

### Your 3-zone setup
1) **Raw zone**
- raw meat, raw cutting board, raw knife
- one towel/paper for this zone

2) **Cook zone**
- stove, pots, cooked food area

3) **Pack zone**
- clean containers, labels, bags
- no raw items here

Even in a small kitchen, you can do this.
The goal is not perfection.
The goal is avoiding cross-contact and confusion.

### Mise en place checklist (copy/paste)
- [ ] sauces made
- [ ] spices measured
- [ ] vegetables chopped
- [ ] containers stacked
- [ ] sauce cups prepared
- [ ] labels ready
- [ ] trash bag ready
- [ ] hand towel/paper ready

When this is done, cooking feels easy.

## Cook SOP — Taste and doneness checkpoints (make it consistent)

A home kitchen often tastes amazing.
But consistency is the challenge.

So you want checkpoints.

### Taste checkpoints (copy/paste)
- checkpoint 1: base sauce tastes good before protein
- checkpoint 2: after protein cooks 70%
- checkpoint 3: final seasoning before packing
- checkpoint 4: final taste of one portion before first pack

This is not overkill.
It is your “quality gate”.

### Doneness checkpoints (keep it simple)
Pick 2–3 checks that matter:
- protein cooked through
- vegetables not overcooked
- noodles not mushy
- rice not dry

Write them in your SOP.
Then you don’t guess under stress.

### “Hold hot / cool fast” principle (simple)
If you are packing hot:
- keep the batch covered
- pack in rounds
- don’t leave it sitting open

If you are cooling:
- portion into shallow containers
- cool in a safe way quickly
- store promptly

The goal is not to be technical.
The goal is good habits.

## Packing SOP — The assembly line (a real business upgrade)

Packing is where reputation is won or lost.
Because customers see the result directly.

### Assembly line stations (expanded)
Station 1: containers open + ready  
Station 2: portioning (scoop/scale)  
Station 3: add-ons + sauce cups  
Station 4: wipe edges + close lids  
Station 5: label  
Station 6: bag + name  
Station 7: final check + staging table

### The “final check” script (say it out loud)
Before you close each bag, ask:
- main dish?
- add-ons included?
- sauce included?
- utensils/napkins?
- name correct?

Saying it out loud reduces mistakes.
It sounds silly, but it works.

### Leak prevention mini-rules
- sauce cups not filled to the edge
- lids pressed fully
- sauce cups placed in separate small bag if possible
- wipe edges
- keep bag upright

This reduces stress and complaints.
