## The 80/20 photo upgrades (do these first)

These are small actions with big results:

1) **Wipe your lens** (seriously)  
2) **Use window light** (avoid harsh yellow light)  
3) **Clean background** (remove clutter)  
4) **Get closer** (show texture)  
5) **One consistent setup** (same plate/board)

Do these and you will look more professional instantly.

---

## Step 1 — Light (the biggest secret)

Light is everything.

### Best light: window light
- Stand near a window
- Turn the dish toward the window
- Use soft daylight

### Avoid harsh kitchen light if possible
Yellow ceiling light makes food look dull.
If you must use it:
- move closer to a window
- turn off some lights
- use a white wall as reflection

### Easy trick: use a white plate or paper as reflector
Put a white plate/paper on the dark side of the dish.
It reflects light and makes food look brighter.

---

## Step 2 — Background (clean = premium)

A messy background makes customers scroll away.
Clean background makes customers trust you.

### Best simple backgrounds
- clean table (wood or white)
- simple cutting board
- neutral cloth (white/gray)
- plain wall

### Remove these from the shot
- dirty dishes
- random kitchen items
- plastic bags
- clutter

Remember: you are selling an experience.
Premium is clean.

---

## Step 3 — Angles (what angle for what food)

Use 3 angles. That’s enough.

### Angle A: 45-degree (best for bowls and plates)
Great for:
- curry over rice
- noodle bowls
- pho, soups
- stir-fry plates

### Angle B: Top-down (best for boxes and trays)
Great for:
- portion proof
- packing photos
- catering trays

### Angle C: Close-up (best for texture)
Great for:
- crispy food
- sauce shine
- dumplings
- spring rolls
- meat texture

You can rotate angles and reuse the same dish.

## Step 5 — Composition (make it look “magazine”)

Simple composition rules:
- keep the dish as the star
- leave some empty space
- avoid too many objects
- use one garnish (optional) for color

### The “triangle” trick
Place:
- main dish
- side/add-on (optional)
- utensil/napkin (optional)
in a triangle shape. It looks balanced.

### The “rule of thirds” (easy)
Put the main dish slightly off center.
It looks more premium than dead-center.

---

## Step 6 — Color (make it pop naturally)

Food looks best when:
- colors are real
- greens look fresh
- sauces look rich

### Easy color upgrades
- add a little green garnish (spring onion, herbs)
- add contrast (chili, lime, sesame)
- keep the background neutral

Do not over-edit until food looks fake.
Real looks tastier.

---

## Step 7 — Phone editing (simple and safe)

Editing should be minimal.
Just make food look like it looked in real life.

Use your phone editor:
- Brightness: slightly up
- Contrast: slightly up
- Warmth: adjust if photo is too yellow
- Sharpness: slight

**Rule:** if the food looks “fake”, reduce editing.

## How to shoot food in a box (so it looks good)

Many kitchens sell in boxes.
Boxes can look boring if you shoot them wrong.

### Box photo rules
- top-down is best
- open lid fully
- clean edges (wipe sauce)
- show full portion
- put box on a clean surface

Optional:
- add a small garnish or sauce cup for color

Box photos are powerful because they show value.

## If your food is soup (pho, noodle soup) — special photo tips

Soup can be hard to photograph because it looks flat.

Soup tricks:
- show steam (hot)
- show herbs/lime for color
- shoot 45-degree angle
- show broth separately in packing (trust)

For packing:
- broth in one container
- noodles/toppings separate
This also makes soup taste better for customers.

## Editing tips that keep food real (avoid fake look)

Editing should be gentle.

Good edits:
- slightly brighten
- slightly increase contrast
- slightly correct yellow light

Bad edits:
- too much saturation (food looks plastic)
- too much sharpness (looks harsh)
- filters that change colors too much

Natural = delicious.

## Troubleshooting (quick fixes)

### “My photos look dark”
Move to window. Increase brightness slightly. Use a white reflector.

### “My photos look yellow”
Turn off strong kitchen light. Move to daylight. Adjust warmth slider.

### “My food looks flat”
Get closer. Add steam, sauce pour, or garnish.

### “My background is messy”
Use one clean board and shoot tighter.

Small fixes, big results.
