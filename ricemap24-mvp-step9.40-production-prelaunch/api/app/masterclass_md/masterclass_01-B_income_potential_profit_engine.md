# Part 1 — The Profit Engine (the model that makes income predictable)

## The 3 numbers that control your income

Everything you earn can be understood with three numbers:

1) **Orders per month**  
2) **Average order value (AOV)**  
3) **COGS % (food + packaging cost)**

Then:
- **Revenue = Orders × AOV**
- **Gross profit = Revenue × (1 − COGS%)**

This is not “finance”.  
This is a simple steering wheel.

---

## What is a realistic COGS%?

COGS here means: **ingredients + packaging** (as requested).

Typical ranges:
- Simple comfort food (rice/noodles/curry/stew): **25–35%**
- Meat-heavy / seafood / premium items: **35–45%**
- Desserts/baked goods: **20–30%**
- Mixed menu average (common): **30–40%**

**Target range:** **30–40%**  
If you can hold 35% consistently, you can earn very well.

---

## The first win: think in gross profit per order

Instead of thinking:
> “I sold 40 orders.”

Start thinking:
> “I earned $___ gross profit per order.”

Example:
- AOV = **$25**
- COGS = **35%** → cost = **$8.75**
- Gross profit per order = **$16.25**

Now every order has a clear meaning.

When your gross profit per order is strong, volume becomes exciting.

# Part 2 — The 6 Profit Multipliers (how high income is actually built)

Most people try to grow by doing one thing:
- “Get more customers.”

But the best businesses grow using multipliers that compound.

Here are the six multipliers that matter most for home kitchens.

---

## Multiplier 1 — Increase AOV (average order value)

AOV is the easiest growth lever.

If you increase AOV by **$3** and you sell 200 orders/month:
- Revenue increases by **$600/month**
- If COGS is 35%, gross profit increases by **$390/month**
- That’s **$4,680/year** from a small change.

### The calm way to increase AOV: “natural yes” add-ons
You don’t push people. You structure the menu.

Examples of AOV-friendly add-ons:
- extra rice/noodles
- sauce cup
- spring rolls / side snack
- dessert cup
- drink (if relevant)
- family upgrade

**Rule:** Add-ons must be easy to prep and fast to pack.

---

## Multiplier 2 — Control COGS (food + packaging)

COGS control is “invisible profit”.

If revenue is $6,000/month:
- COGS 40% → gross profit $3,600
- COGS 34% → gross profit $3,960

That’s **+$360/month**  
= **$4,320/year**

And most of that comes from:
- portion consistency,
- smart purchasing,
- fewer packaging types,
- less waste,
- fewer “menu experiments” with expensive ingredients.

---

## Multiplier 3 — Increase frequency (repeat customers)

Frequency means: how often the same customer orders again.

If you have 100 customers:
- if they order once/month → 100 orders
- if they order twice/month → 200 orders (same marketing effort, more predictability)

**Repeat is the salary effect.**

How to increase frequency:
- predictable weekly rhythm,
- reliable pickup window,
- consistent quality,
- a “next week teaser” (preview menu),
- and a simple VIP follow-up message.

(We’ll give message templates later.)

---

## Multiplier 4 — Increase volume per production day (batch power)

High income happens when you can produce more orders in fewer sessions.

If you cook 2 days/week:
- 15 orders/day → 120 orders/month
- 30 orders/day → 240 orders/month

Same number of cooking days.  
Different income.

This comes from:
- standardizing the menu,
- batching,
- and having a packing line.

(We won’t repeat full batch-cooking methods here — but we will show how to use them for profit.)

---

## Multiplier 5 — Occasions (sell for moments, not only hunger)

Occasions create **peaks**.

People spend more when the order is for:
- a family gathering,
- a celebration,
- a work lunch,
- a holiday,
- a weekend treat,
- guests visiting,
- or an event.

Occasions increase:
- AOV,
- volume,
- and repeat (because they remember you when the moment returns).

We’ll build an “occasion map” you can use.

---

## Multiplier 6 — Campaign weeks (controlled demand spikes)

Campaign weeks are planned “sales peaks”:
- limited menu drop,
- special theme,
- a bundle offer,
- a deadline,
- clear pickup times.

Campaigns are powerful because they:
- reduce decision stress for customers,
- create urgency,
- let you plan ingredients perfectly,
- reduce waste,
- and generate strong revenue in short bursts.

This is how you can earn more **without being always on**.

We won’t rebuild full menu architecture here.

## Keep your menu profitable by design

A profitable menu has:
- a stable base (rice/noodles/potato),
- controlled protein cost,
- and add-ons that increase AOV.

### The “profit triangle”
1) **Base** (cheap, filling, scalable)
2) **Protein** (controlled portion, consistent)
3) **Sauce & flavor** (high perceived value, low cost)

If your dish feels premium mostly because of sauce/flavor and experience, your COGS stays healthy.

## Packaging strategy (profit and presentation)

Packaging is both cost and branding.

Simple rule:
- Choose 1 main container type and 1 add-on container type.
- Buy in bulk.
- Use the right size.

You want the food to look full, clean, and premium.

This creates:
- better reviews,
- repeat orders,
- and the ability to charge more confidently.

# Part 11 — Capacity & Profit Per Hour (the “real” income picture)

Even if we ignore electricity and rent, your **time** still matters.
The goal is not only to earn more — it’s to earn well **per hour**.

You don’t need perfect tracking. Use a simple estimate.

## The simple time model

For many home kitchens, time per order becomes much faster once the system is stable.

Typical ranges (after you have a rhythm):
- Production (batch cooking): **2–6 minutes per portion** (because you cook many at once)
- Packing + labeling: **1–3 minutes per order**
- Communication/admin: **0.5–2 minutes per order** (if you set deadlines and use templates)

So a realistic “all-in” time per order can be:
- **5 minutes/order** (very efficient system)
- **8 minutes/order** (common and healthy)
- **12 minutes/order** (early stage or complex menu)

### Profit per hour example
Assume:
- AOV $28
- COGS 35% → gross profit per order $18.20

If you average **8 minutes/order**:
- Orders per hour ≈ 60 / 8 = 7.5
- Gross profit per hour ≈ 7.5 × $18.20 = **$136.50/hr**

If you average **12 minutes/order**:
- Orders per hour = 5
- Gross profit per hour ≈ 5 × $18.20 = **$91/hr**

That is why systems matter so much.
Small efficiency improvements can increase your effective “hourly income” dramatically.

> Note: This is gross profit, not final salary.  
> But it shows why home kitchens can become surprisingly profitable when organized well.
