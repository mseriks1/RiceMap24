## What “Cold-Ready” means (simple)

Cold-ready items are designed to be:
- served cold (by intention)
- stored cold
- eaten cold (or lightly warmed if you say so)

The core principle is:

**Cold-ready must be designed for cold.**

Not “hot food that became cold”.
But food that is meant to be cold.

That’s why messaging matters so much.

## The Cold-Ready Success Rules (simple model)

If you want a cold menu to feel premium and avoid complaints, follow these rules:

1) **Choose dishes that are meant to be cold**  
2) **Separate wet and dry** (dressing/sauce separate)  
3) **Label “served cold” clearly**  
4) **Use freshness signals** (clean packing, date, storage note)  
5) **Build bundles that solve a moment** (lunch, weekend treat, picnic)  
6) **Test and iterate** (4-week rollout)

These rules create trust.

## Step 1 — Choose the right cold-ready dishes

Not everything works cold.
So we choose categories that naturally win cold.

### Category A — Fresh rolls and chilled snacks
These feel premium and “clean”.

**Examples:**
- Fresh spring rolls (Vietnamese-style) with peanut/hoisin dip
- Rice paper rolls with shrimp/tofu/chicken
- Summer rolls + dipping sauce cups
- Chilled snack boxes (edamame, pickles, fruit)

**Why these work:**
- customers expect chilled freshness
- sauce is separate
- looks beautiful

### Category B — Cold noodle bowls and salad bowls
These can be very “meal prep friendly”.

**Examples:**
- Vietnamese vermicelli bowl (bun) with dressing separate
- Thai-style noodle salad with lime dressing separate
- Sesame noodle salad
- Grain bowls (served cold) with protein and sauce separate

**Why these work:**
- filling and fresh
- easy to batch components
- great for lunch

### Category C — Cultural side dish sets (banchan-style)
These are perfect for upsell and bundles.

**Examples:**
- kimchi (if you do it)
- cucumber salad
- pickled vegetables
- spicy eggplant
- potato salad (culture-dependent)
- vinegar slaw

**Why these work:**
- small portions, high value
- keeps well
- encourages repeat orders

### Category D — Dessert cups (premium and stable)
Desserts are the easiest cold-ready product.

**Examples:**
- mango float cups
- leche flan
- panna cotta cups
- tiramisu cups
- fruit and cream cups

**Why these work:**
- customers expect cold
- high perceived value
- great margins

### Category E — “Chilled kits” (assemble cold)
These feel modern and premium.

**Examples:**
- salad kit with dressing separate
- poke-style bowl (if you do it safely and responsibly)
- charcuterie-style snack boxes (simple)

## Step 2 — Avoid the “cold disappointment” dishes

Some foods can technically be eaten cold, but customers will feel disappointed.

Avoid as cold-ready main products:
- fried items that should be crispy (unless you sell them frozen as in Masterclass A)
- hot rice dishes that become dry
- noodles that become soggy without separation
- anything that relies on heat for flavor experience

If you include these, you must either:
- separate components strongly
- or position as “reheat” (Masterclass A)

Cold-ready must feel intentional.

## Step 4 — Packaging rules that protect freshness (and trust)

Cold-ready wins when it looks clean and professional.

### Rule 1 — Separate wet and dry
Always separate:
- dressing
- sauce
- wet toppings

Wet + dry together creates sogginess and “mess”.

### Rule 2 — Use the “component layout”
Think like a bento box:
- protein section
- carb/greens section
- toppings section
- sauce cup

This looks premium.

### Rule 3 — Label clearly
Every cold-ready item should have:
- dish name
- “served cold”
- date
- “keep refrigerated”
- optional: “best eaten within X days”

### Rule 4 — Cold chain basics (light and responsible)
- keep cold items cold
- store quickly
- deliver/pickup in a chilled state
- encourage customers to refrigerate

We keep it calm, but responsible.

## Cold-ready logistics (keep it chilled without stress)

Cold-ready doesn’t require fancy equipment.
But it does require a simple chilled routine.

### The goal
Keep cold items cold from packing to pickup.

### Simple tools that help
- insulated bag or cooler box for staging
- ice packs (simple and cheap)
- a separate fridge shelf for “pickup orders”
- a thermometer (optional, for confidence)

You don’t need to overthink it.
You just need a habit.

### Pickup day staging (simple)
- Pack cold-ready items last (so they stay chilled)
- Place completed orders in one chilled staging area
- If pickup window is long, use a cooler box with ice packs

### Customer instruction (one line)
“Please refrigerate when you get home.”

This reduces misunderstanding and keeps the experience good.

---

## Prep scheduling (how to batch cold-ready items)

Cold-ready becomes easy when you batch components.

### Component batching examples
- wash and cut vegetables in one block
- portion sauces/dressings in one block
- prep proteins in one block
- prep side cups in one block
- pack dessert cups in one block

### A realistic weekly schedule
**Day 1 (30–60 min):** sauces + dressings + side cups  
**Day 2 (60–120 min):** bowls and rolls assembly + labels  
**Pickup day (30–60 min):** final assembly + chilled packing + staging  

This spreads work and protects freshness.

## Sauce and dressing rules (the “separation secret”)

Cold-ready lives or dies by sauces.

### Rule 1 — Always separate
Dressing or sauce must be in a cup, not mixed in the bowl.

### Rule 2 — Portion consistency
Use one standard cup size and fill level.
Consistency builds trust.

### Rule 3 — Label sauce cups when needed
If you offer two sauces, label them:
- “peanut”
- “lime”
- “spicy”

This reduces confusion.
