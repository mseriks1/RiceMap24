## Big idea (business reality)

A home kitchen is a real business.

That means it follows the same business principles as any store:
- **positioning** (what you are known for)
- **product design** (what you sell, in what format)
- **packaging** (how customers buy)
- **conversion** (how easy it is to choose and order)
- **profitability** (can you keep doing it without burnout?)

Most kitchens fail not because the food is bad.
They fail because the menu is messy:
- too many options
- unclear offer
- low profit per order
- too much time per customer

A strong menu architecture fixes all of this.

When your menu is designed well, **everything becomes possible**:
- customers decide faster
- you get bigger baskets
- you cook more efficiently
- you feel more confident
- your business becomes stable


## What “menu architecture” means (simple)

Menu architecture is how your menu is *structured* to sell:
- which dish is the star
- which dishes are safe choices
- which items increase order value
- how customers choose size and quantity

A menu is not only food.
A menu is a decision system.

Your job is to make the decision system easy:
**“I know what to order.”**

## Step 1 — Choose your “menu identity” (positioning)

Before you list 10 dishes, choose what you want to be known for.

Examples:
- “The Adobo Kitchen”
- “Dumpling & Chili Oil Kitchen”
- “Thai Curry Kitchen”
- “Vietnamese Comfort Bowl Kitchen”
- “Home-style noodles and spring rolls”

This identity is your positioning.
It makes your menu feel focused and professional.

### The 1-sentence kitchen line (copy/paste)
Use a simple line everywhere:
- “Homemade [Cuisine] food — small batch, easy to order.”
- “Taste of home — made fresh, clean packing.”
- “Family-style [Cuisine] cooking for dinner and sharing.”

Keep it short and confident.


## Step 2 — Build the “Signature + Banker + Add-ons” structure

This is one of the best menu structures for home kitchens.

### A) Signature dish (your hero)
This is your “flag”.
It should be:
- unique
- cultural/traditional
- memorable
- the best quality you can make consistently

Signature dish has one job:
**make people want to try your kitchen.**

### B) Banker dish (your safe choice)
Bankers are the dishes that:
- people understand quickly
- feel safe for first-time buyers
- convert people who are unsure

Banker dish examples:
- fried rice
- noodles
- stir-fry
- spring rolls
- mild curry over rice

Banker dish has one job:
**remove hesitation and get the first order.**

### C) Add-ons (your profit helpers)
Add-ons increase your average order value without much extra stress.

Add-on examples:
- extra rice
- sauce cup
- spring rolls / dumplings
- dessert
- spicy upgrade (chili oil)

Add-ons have one job:
**make each order bigger and more profitable.**

### The simple ideal menu (for most kitchens)
For a strong starting menu:
- 1 signature dish
- 1–2 banker dishes
- 2–4 add-ons/sides

That’s enough to run a real business.

You can add more later.
But start simple and win.


## Step 3 — Menu size strategy (too many dishes vs too few)

Many restaurants (especially some international ones) list 20+ dishes.
Home kitchens don’t need that.

### Why too many dishes hurts
- customers get confused (“decision stress”)
- your prep becomes chaotic
- quality becomes inconsistent
- you waste ingredients
- you spend more time per order

### Why too few dishes can hurt (sometimes)
- customers feel no choice
- some customers have dietary preferences
- they might want variation over time

### The balanced strategy (recommended)
- Keep the menu small **per cooking cycle**
- Rotate variations across weeks/months if you want variety

This is how stores do it:
They don’t sell everything every day.
They run “drops” and “campaign weeks”.


## Step 4 — Package sizes (Single / Family / Group) as business strategy

This is where your menu becomes a *business*.

Package sizes are not only UI.
They are:
- pricing strategy
- workload strategy
- profit strategy
- customer segmentation

### Why package sizes are powerful
They help customers buy for different situations:
- solo meal
- dinner for family
- guests / office / party

And they help you:
- get bigger orders
- reduce messaging per portion
- batch cooking more efficiently


## Single: when it is smart (and when it is not)

### Single works best when:
- you use **pre-order + pickup/delivery window**
- you cook in batches (not random on-demand)
- you can deliver 10–30 portions at once

In that scenario, single can be very profitable because:
- many singles arrive in the same time window
- packing is assembly line
- messaging can be standardized

### Single is often not smart when:
- you get 1–3 orders spread across the day
- you must do many micro pickups
- you have no clear pickup window

Then single becomes:
- too much time per order
- low profit per hour
- stress

### Single “fixes” if you still want it
If you want single but also profit, add one boundary:
- **minimum order** (e.g., 2 portions / minimum amount)
or
- **limited pickup window** only
or
- **single only on certain days**

This protects you.


## Family (3–4): the most powerful size for many home kitchens

Family size often becomes your best product because:
- bigger basket
- fewer customers to manage
- people buy for dinner (habit)
- easier for customers to justify ordering

It also fits your “home cooking” positioning:
family food.

**If you want profitability without stress, Family is a strong default.**

## Group (6–8): the “event” and “office” bridge

Group size is powerful because it creates:
- party orders
- office lunch orders
- gatherings
- repeat large orders

Group also positions your kitchen as:
“this is serious and scalable”.

Even if group orders are not daily, they can create big boosts.

## The best “package strategy” options (choose one)

Different kitchens have different capacity.
Here are 4 common strategies:

### Strategy A: Family-first (recommended for many)
- Family (3–4) is the main offer
- Group is available
- Single is optional or removed

Great for: part-time kitchens, efficiency, higher profit per order.

### Strategy B: Single + window (pre-order model)
- Single is available
- but only within pre-order + pickup window
- Family and Group available too

Great for: kitchens with strong demand and batch cooking.

### Strategy C: Minimum order
- Single exists
- but minimum order (2 portions or minimum amount)

Great for: preventing low-profit micro orders.

### Strategy D: Catering-lite
- Family and Group only
- no single

Great for: kitchens that want fewer customers and bigger baskets.

Pick one strategy and commit for a month.
Then evaluate.

## Step 5 — Dish descriptions that sell on small space (micro-copy)

You often only have 6–12 words.
So your description must do three things:

1) **Taste / feel** (savory, tangy, creamy, crispy)
2) **Clarity** (what it actually is, for unfamiliar dishes)
3) **Trust** (classic, comfort, family-style, made fresh)

### The micro-copy formula (pick one)
- Taste profile: “Garlic-soy comfort with a gentle vinegar tang.”
- Texture hook: “Crispy outside, juicy inside, sweet chili dip.”
- Culture + clarity: “Filipino classic — family-style comfort, made fresh.”
- Familiar bridge: “Like a savory stew: rich, tangy, comforting.”

Keep it short.
Keep it appetizing.
Avoid long explanations.

## Rotation strategy: variety without chaos

You can have variety without a big menu.

Use rotation.

### Rotation model A: Keep signature, rotate banker
- Signature stays the same (brand consistency)
- Banker rotates every few weeks (variety)
- Add-ons mostly stable

This keeps the kitchen known for something, but still feels fresh.

### Rotation model B: Seasonal or monthly special
- Signature stays
- Add a “special dish” once per month (campaign week)
- Remove it after

This creates excitement without permanent complexity.

### Rotation model C: Variation inside the same dish (smart)
Instead of adding new dishes, vary:
- protein (chicken / pork / beef / tofu)
- spice level
- add-on style

This gives variety while keeping prep simple.

## The “profit per order” mindset (simple and powerful)

You don’t need complicated accounting.
You need one question:

“Is this order worth my time?”

Single orders can be worth it **if**:
- they happen in the same window
- you have many at once

Single orders are often not worth it **if**:
- they are one by one across time
- you have many micro pickups
- you spend lots of messaging time

This is why windows and minimum orders matter.
They protect profit per hour.


## How to write pro descriptions (2–4 lines) without being boring

Micro-copy is for small space.
Pro description is for the click-in detail.

A strong pro description has this structure:

1) **What it is** (clear)
2) **What it tastes like** (appetizing)
3) **Why it’s special** (tradition/signature)
4) Optional: spice or serving note

### Pro description template (copy/paste)
“[Dish] is a [Cuisine] classic: [what it is].  
Expect [taste profile] with [texture].  
Made fresh in small batches, family-style.”

Short, professional, appetizing.

### Example: Adobo (pro)
“Chicken Adobo is a Filipino comfort classic: slow-cooked chicken in soy, garlic and vinegar.  
Deep savory flavor with a gentle tang, cooked family-style.  
Made fresh in small batches.”

This is clear even for customers who don’t know the dish.


## Ingredients: how to list them like a pro

Ingredients list is a trust signal, but it should be readable.

### Best format
- main ingredients first (protein, base)
- then sauces
- then spices/aromatics
- optional toppings last

Example:
Chicken, soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, onion, bay leaf, peppercorns, spring onion.

Simple.


## “Bridge phrases” for unfamiliar dishes (high conversion)

- “comfort classic”

- “savory and tangy”

- “coconut curry comfort”

Bridge phrases reduce fear.
Reduced fear increases conversion.

## Package strategy playbook (very practical)

Package sizes should not be random.
They should match how people buy food in real life.

People buy in 3 common situations:
1) “Me” (treat myself)
2) “Us” (dinner solved)
3) “Them” (guests, office, event)

Single / Family / Group maps perfectly to this.
But the *default* you push should match your business reality.

### If you are part-time (most kitchens)
Your best default is usually **Family**.
Because one family order = multiple portions in one message.

So the menu should visually feel like:
- Family is the main option
- Group is the “upgrade”
- Single is optional

### If you have strong demand and a strict window
You can include Single confidently.
But your window must be clear and consistent.

### If you are new and want profit safety
Use **minimum order** from day one.
It protects your time.


## How to write size labels so customers choose faster

If your UI allows a short subline, use it.
If not, you can still include it in the detail view.

Examples:

- **Single (1):** “Perfect for one meal.”
- **Family (3–4):** “Dinner for 3–4 (best value).”
- **Group (6–8):** “Great for guests / office / party.”

These lines reduce decision stress.
They also gently push customers toward bigger packs.


## “Single is not profitable” — practical fixes

If singles are not profitable, you have 4 simple fixes:

1) **Window-only singles**
Singles only available inside your pickup/delivery window.

2) **Minimum order**
Example: “Minimum 2 portions” or “Minimum 250 NOK”.
This is common in delivery businesses.

3) **Singles only for VIP customers**
VIP customers tend to be easier and repeat.
So singles become less stressful.

4) **Remove single and push family packs**
This is often the best option for part-time kitchens.

A business is allowed to choose what is sustainable.
Sustainable kitchens last.
Unsustainable kitchens quit.
