## Big idea (general business first): don’t compete on their strengths — compete on your unfair advantages

In business, the fastest way to lose is to imitate a competitor who has:
- better location
- higher volume capacity
- longer opening hours
- trained staff
- established customer flow

Takeaway restaurants and food trucks often win on:
- **availability** (open many days/hours)
- **speed** (they are built for quick service)
- **location** (visibility, foot traffic)
- **habit** (people already know them)

If you try to beat them at those exact strengths, you will burn out.

So the smarter business move is:

**Choose a different game.**

You win by leaning into what they can’t easily copy:
- real home-style flavor
- authenticity and cultural depth
- small-batch freshness
- personal care and consistency
- excitement (limited drops)
- community and belonging
- smart bundles and value perception
- predictable pickup rhythm that feels easy

This masterclass is about designing that game.

# Part 3 — Timing: when do people buy takeaway?

People mostly buy takeaway at moments of:
- hunger + tiredness
- time pressure
- weekend treating
- social gatherings
- office lunch routines

For delivery platforms (a useful proxy for takeaway ordering behavior), peak ordering often clusters around:
- **lunch** (roughly 11:00–13:00)
- **dinner** (roughly late afternoon to evening)
- **weekends** being consistently busy, with Sunday often among the busiest days  
These peak patterns are documented for couriers in Norway (Wolt materials mention lunch 11:00–13:00 and dinner windows roughly 15:30/16:00–20:00+; weekends can be busy all day and Sundays often peak). citeturn0search2turn0search6

### What this means for home kitchens
You don’t need to be open daily to compete.
You need to be available at the right moments.

A smart home kitchen schedule often aligns with:
- **Thursday/Friday**: dinner relief  
- **Saturday**: family dinner + social gatherings  
- **Sunday**: comfort food + “I don’t want to cook” moment  

If your customers are families, weekend windows are powerful.
If your customers are workers, a **lunch window** (weekly) can also work.

# Part 4 — The menu strategy that beats takeaway

## The core rule: short menu, strong identity
Many takeaway menus are too long.
A long menu often means:
- more prep complexity
- lower consistency
- more average items
- more decision fatigue for customers

Your advantage is focus.

### The 3-layer home kitchen menu
1) **Signature dish** (the hero)  
2) **Banker dish** (reliable, high repeat)  
3) **Add-ons** (increase value, not complexity)

That’s enough.

---

## “Known ingredients” wins (don’t be too exotic too fast)
People often buy what feels familiar.

You can be culturally authentic while still using:
- known proteins (chicken, beef, salmon, tofu)
- known carbs (rice, noodles, potatoes)
- known flavors (garlic, ginger, chili, coconut)

Then your cultural uniqueness becomes:
- sauces
- spice balance
- technique
- sides
- story

### The best bridge strategy
Offer “familiar base + cultural twist”:
- chicken + rice + signature sauce
- noodle bowl with recognizable vegetables
- wraps with a cultural filling

This reduces fear and increases first orders.

---

## Bundles that takeaway can’t compete with
Takeaway often sells “one meal”.
You can sell “a solved moment”.

Bundle ideas:
- **Dinner Solved (2–4 people)**: main + rice + 2 sides
- **Weekend Treat Bundle**: main + add-on + dessert cups
- **Office Lunch Pack**: 10–20 portions with simple labeling
- **Family Tray**: larger format, better value perception

Bundling increases:
- average order value
- perceived value vs takeaway
- repeat orders

---

## Pricing: competitive without a race to the bottom
You can be competitive with takeaway without being cheapest.

### The value equation customers actually feel
**Value = portion + taste + reliability + convenience + experience**

Takeaway often competes on:
- convenience and habit

You compete on:
- taste + freshness + personal care + bundles

So pricing strategy is:
- keep individual portions in the “reasonable zone”
- make bundles feel smart (better value per person)
- avoid constant discounts

A small “new customer” benefit is okay:
- priority slot
- small sauce cup
But avoid heavy discounting that attracts price-only customers.

# Part 5 — Availability vs rhythm: how to compete without being open daily

Takeaway wins on “always available”.
You can’t win there without burning out.

So you win on:
**predictable rhythm**.

### The rhythm promise
- “Weekly pickup every Saturday 16–18”
- “Thursday dinner drop 17–19”
- “Lunch packs every Wednesday 11–13”

This makes you easy to remember.
And memory is how you compete against a restaurant location.

### The consistency rule (important)
Customers lose trust when you are:
- active one week
- gone the next
- back again randomly

If you want to compete long-term, you must protect your rhythm.
This is where capacity honesty matters.

# Part 6 — Capacity honesty and long-term stamina

The biggest hidden advantage of takeaway is:
they can handle volume spikes.

Home kitchens must design around capacity.

### Your capacity protection system
- max orders per window
- clear cutoff time
- sold-out confidence
- simple menu

This is not limiting you.
This is protecting quality and endurance.

### The long-game rule
**Consistency beats intensity.**
Better to run:
- 25 orders every Saturday for 6 months  
than  
- 60 orders once, then disappear for 3 weeks.

Takeaway wins because it’s always there.
You must become “always there” in your chosen rhythm.

# Part 7 — Delivery outsourcing vs pickup (smart choices)

Takeaway often has:
- in-house pickup flow
- delivery partnerships

Home kitchens can choose:

## Option A — Pickup-only (strong for margin + quality)
- simplest logistics
- highest control
- strongest reliability

## Option B — Outsourced delivery (careful)
- more convenience
- more complexity and risk
- more packaging requirements

A smart approach:
- start pickup-only
- add delivery later for limited windows or premium orders

If you add delivery, design it like a system:
- packaging upgraded
- clear time windows
- limited radius (if possible)
- clear expectations

# Part 12 — Menu design: why shorter menus often win

A long menu looks impressive, but it has hidden problems:
- it spreads quality thin
- it increases mistakes
- it increases prep and waste
- it slows down service
- it makes customers confused

A short menu signals:
- “we know what we’re good at”
- “this is our signature”
- “this is why people come here”

### The best home-kitchen menu architecture
- 1 signature dish (hero)
- 1 banker dish (safe repeat)
- 1 seasonal rotation (optional)
- 2–4 add-ons (high margin, low complexity)
- 1 dessert cup (optional but powerful)

That’s enough to compete.

---

## The “banker dish” concept (very important)
A banker dish is:
- easy to produce consistently
- reheats well (if needed)
- has wide appeal
- uses familiar ingredients
- supports stable weekly rhythm

Most successful kitchens build their business around 1–2 banker dishes.
Takeaway competitors often have many dishes but no clear “banker”.

Your banker dish becomes your identity.

---

## The “familiar bridge” menu method (first-time conversion)
To compete with takeaway, you must reduce fear.

Fear reducers:
- recognizable ingredients
- clear photos
- short descriptions
- clear “how it tastes” lines (“mild”, “comfort”, “rich sauce”)

You can be authentic and still easy to try.

Examples (format, not recipes):
- “Chicken + rice + signature sauce”
- “Beef stew style + rice”
- “Noodle bowl with vegetables + dressing”
- “Wrap with grilled chicken + cultural sauce”

This makes first-time orders feel safe.

# Part 13 — Pricing strategy: compete on value, not desperation

Takeaway pricing is shaped by:
- rent
- staff wages
- long opening hours
- platform fees (if used)

Home kitchens have different cost structures.
That gives you flexibility.

But you still need to price sustainably:
- ingredients
- packaging
- your time
- stress buffer

### Pricing positioning options (choose one)
**Option A — Competitive price, stronger quality**
You price close to takeaway, but win on taste and freshness.

**Option B — Slightly premium price, more value**
You price a little higher, but bundles and portion value make it feel smart.

**Option C — Premium drops**
You sell limited drops that feel special (weekend treat).

Most home kitchens succeed with A or B.
Premium drops are optional add-ons.

### The “no discount war” rule
Discounts attract price shoppers.
Price shoppers are not loyal.
Your goal is repeat customers, not one-time bargain hunters.

Use:
- bundles
- VIP priority
- small gratitude bonuses
Instead of constant discounts.
