---

## Big idea (positive and simple)

Most home kitchens try to grow by doing more content.

That can work.
But it can also feel exhausting.

Partnerships are different.

A partnership is when someone else gives you access to **their customers**:
- a gym with members
- a salon with loyal clients
- a shop with foot traffic
- a church with community
- an office with hungry people every week

In business, this is called **distribution**.

And distribution is powerful because it creates:
- trust (you are “approved” by a local place)
- visibility (people see you again and again)
- convenience (QR + order flow becomes normal)
- repeat orders (the best kind of growth)

Here is the mindset:

**You don’t need everyone to know you.  
You need the right places to recommend you.**

When you get “shelf space” in your city, you grow like a local brand.
Even if you cook at home.

---

## Why partnerships work (simple psychology)

Partnerships work because humans are social and local.

People trust food more when:
- it is recommended by a place they already trust
- they see it consistently (not just once on Instagram)
- it feels easy (scan → order → pickup)

And partners say yes when:
- you make their place look better (added value)
- you make their customers happier (better experience)
- you remove risk (pilot, simple terms)
- you make it easy (you bring the materials and the plan)

This is good news:
You don’t need to be “big”.
You need to be **organized** and **reliable**.

---

## The simple model: Shelf Space = Place × Offer × Proof × Ease

Use this 4-part model:

1) **Place**  
Choose a place where your ideal customers already spend time.

2) **Offer**  
Give a simple offer that fits that place (not “everything on your menu”).

3) **Proof**  
Show trust: clean brand, photos, reviews, consistency.

4) **Ease**  
Make it easy: QR, clear pickup times, quick ordering, clear payment.

If you get all four, partnerships become natural.

## Step 1 — Choose the right partners (don’t start random)

A common mistake is choosing partners based on “sounds cool”.

Instead, choose partners based on **customer fit**.

### The best partner categories for home kitchens

**1) Gyms / fitness studios**  
Customers already think about food.
Great for: protein bowls, meal prep, healthy plates.

**2) Salons / barbers**  
People sit and wait. They talk. They share recommendations.
Great for: comfort food, treats, “weekend pickup”.

**3) Local shops (grocery, Asian store, butcher, mini market)**  
Foot traffic + community.
Great for: weekly specials, “preorder for weekend”.

**4) Churches / community groups / associations**  
Strong trust networks.
Great for: trays, family packs, event food.

**5) Offices / coworking spaces**  
Consistent routine.
Great for: lunch delivery, weekly pickup.

**6) Schools / after-school activities / sports teams**  
Parents + convenience.
Great for: family packs, fundraiser collabs.

### Quick “partner score” (1–5)
Rate each place on:
- Customer fit (do they match your food?)
- Foot traffic / member base
- Trust level (do people respect this place?)
- Access (can you talk to the owner/manager?)
- Simplicity (can you do a pilot without chaos?)

Start with the places that score highest in **customer fit + access**.
Not the biggest.

## A quick note: partnerships are “boring growth” (and that’s a compliment)

Social media is exciting. Partnerships are quieter.

But partnerships are powerful because they **compound**:
- The first week you place a QR poster, a few people scan.
- The second week, they scan again (familiarity).
- The third week, someone mentions you to a friend.
- The fourth week, the partner owner starts recommending you naturally.

This is how local brands are built.
Not in one viral moment, but through consistent “presence”.

If you are a home kitchen, this is perfect:
You can grow **without becoming a content machine**.
You can grow like a real business: distribution + routine + reputation.

And you can do it step-by-step.
No stress. No big risk.


## Step 2 — Pick ONE partnership model (keep it simple)

Here are 6 models. Choose one to start.

### Model A — QR placement (the easiest)
You place a small QR poster or table card in the partner location.

**Partner gets:** added value (nice recommendation for their customers)  
**You get:** visibility + orders  
**Best for:** gyms, salons, shops, community boards  
**Risk:** very low

### Model B — Member perk (exclusive offer)
The partner’s members get something special:
- a small bonus add-on
- early access to drops
- a “member menu” pickup time

**Partner gets:** better membership value  
**You get:** loyal repeat customers  
**Risk:** low

### Model C — Commission per order (simple affiliate)
The partner gets a small commission for orders that come through their QR code.

**Partner gets:** clear incentive  
**You get:** a motivated promoter  
**Risk:** medium (tracking + paying on time)

### Model D — Fixed monthly placement fee
You pay a fixed amount for placement (like local advertising).
This is clean and predictable.

**Partner gets:** guaranteed money  
**You get:** stable distribution  
**Risk:** medium (you must make it worth it)

### Model E — Sampling day / tasting table (high conversion)
You do a small tasting day (1–2 hours).
People try your food and scan your QR.

**Partner gets:** event energy + happy customers  
**You get:** fast trust  
**Risk:** medium (prep + time)

### Model F — Recurring pickup (weekly routine)
You create a weekly pickup point:
“Every Thursday 17:00–18:00 pickup here.”

**Partner gets:** foot traffic  
**You get:** predictable repeat orders  
**Risk:** higher (requires consistency)

**Start suggestion (realistic):** Model A (QR placement) + a 30‑day pilot.
Then upgrade later.

## Step 3 — Build the “partner-friendly” offer (one sentence)

Your partner does not want to learn your whole business.
They want a clear offer that benefits their customers.

Use this structure:

**“My customers love [RESULT]. I can offer your customers [OFFER] with [EASY PROCESS].”**

Examples:

- “I make clean, homemade Filipino bowls. Your members can scan a QR and pre-order for Saturday pickup.”
- “I do family trays for weekends. Your clients can scan and order a tray for Sunday pickup.”
- “I do office lunch sets. Your team can pre-order every Wednesday morning.”

### Keep your partner offer narrow
Don’t offer 20 menu items.
Offer 1–3 clear options that fit that partner.

**Gym offer ideas (simple):**
- Protein bowl + rice
- Healthy plate + salad
- Meal prep pack (2–4 portions)

**Salon offer ideas:**
- Weekend comfort meal
- Treat box / dessert
- “Dinner solved” family pack

**Church/community offer ideas:**
- Tray for small group
- Family pack
- Event catering menu (simple)

You don’t need to invent a new strategy for every place.
Use a simple playbook.

**Best offer (simple):**
- “Protein Bowl” (one hero option)
- “Meal Prep Pack” (2–4 portions)

**Best model to start:**

## How to run a tasting day (simple and professional)

A tasting day is one of the fastest ways to convert new customers.
But keep it small and clean.

**The simple tasting setup:**
- 1–2 items only (signature flavor)
- Small cups with lids (clean and easy)
- One QR sign: “Order for weekend pickup”
- A simple script: “If you like it, scan the QR and message ‘ORDER’.”

**Your goal is not to feed everyone.**
Your goal is to create trust.

If 30 people taste and 6 order, that is a win.
Those 6 can become repeat customers.


## Step 4 — Prepare “proof” (trust package) in 15 minutes

Before you pitch, prepare a simple trust package.

You need:
- 3 great food photos
- 1 clean “about” paragraph
- 3 reviews or testimonials (screenshots or quotes)
- Clear pickup times and order method
- A QR poster (simple)

You don’t need a perfect brand.
You need **clarity**.

### Your one-paragraph “about” (template)
> I run a small home kitchen focused on [STYLE OF FOOD].  
> Customers love it because it’s [3 TRUST WORDS: clean, consistent, generous, authentic].  
> Ordering is simple: scan the QR, send your order, and pick up on [DAYS/TIMES].  
> I keep it limited so quality stays high.

This is enough.

## Step 5 — The 30‑day pilot (this is your secret weapon)

A pilot makes it easy to say yes.

Instead of asking for a “forever partnership”, say:

**“Can we test this for 30 days and see if it helps your customers?”**

### A simple pilot plan (what you propose)
- You place one QR poster + one table card (or one small sign)
- You run one small “member offer” (optional)
- You do 1 reminder post/story for the partner (you can write it)
- You track results for 30 days
- At the end, you show results and propose the next step

This removes fear.

### What you promise during the pilot
- You handle all customer questions
- You keep ordering simple
- You keep pickup times predictable
- You keep quality consistent
- You do not create chaos at their place

When partners feel safe, they say yes.

## Step 6 — Deal terms (simple and fair)

Let’s keep it home-kitchen friendly.

### Option 1 — “Free placement” (start here)
You give value, partner gives shelf space.
No money changes hands.

Use when:
- you’re early
- you want your first partnerships
- the partner is small but trusted

### Option 2 — Commission per order (5–15%)
This works if you can track it simply.

Rules:
- Only pay on completed orders
- Pay monthly
- Keep it clean and transparent

Example:
- 10% commission on orders that used the partner QR code
- Paid at month end

### Option 3 — Fixed monthly fee
Use when the place has strong traffic or members.

Example:
- $50–$200/month for placement + 1 monthly shout-out
(Your city will decide the level.)

### Option 4 — “Member perk” instead of money
Sometimes this is the best deal.

Example:
- Gym members get a free sauce cup, extra protein option, or small discount
- Partner gets to say: “We take care of our members”

### Simple rule: pick what is easiest to manage
The best deal is the one you can deliver consistently.

## Step 6.5 — How to talk about money (without feeling awkward)

Money is where many partnerships die.
Not because the partner is “greedy”.
But because the deal is unclear.

Here is a warm, simple way to frame it:

1) Start with value  
   “This helps your customers and makes your place look good.”

2) Offer a low-risk start  
   “Let’s do a 30-day pilot first.”

3) Give a clean deal menu  
   “After the pilot, we can choose what feels fair and simple.”

### Your deal menu (3 options)
Use this exact structure when you talk to a partner:

- **Option A (simple):** Free placement (no money, just value)  
- **Option B (fair):** Commission per order (paid monthly)  
- **Option C (clean):** Fixed monthly placement fee

This feels professional and calm.
Partners love clarity.

### A simple commission method (no complicated tech)
If you don’t want complicated tracking, do this:

- Use a unique **QR link** per partner (or a simple partner code)
- At month end, count orders from that code
- Pay commission once per month

Keep it transparent:
- “Here are the orders from your code.”
- “Here is your payout.”

When you pay on time, partners trust you.
When partners trust you, they recommend you more.

## Step 7.5 — Objection handling (the friendly way)

Partners will often say “no” because they are busy, not because they dislike you.

Here are common objections and warm responses:

### “We don’t want to bother customers.”
> Totally understand.  
> This is designed to be light and optional — just a small QR sign.  
> No one has to talk about it. It’s simply there if customers want it.

### “We can’t promote food here.”
> No problem. We can do the simplest version:  
> one small card near the counter, no big signage, no announcements.  
> If it doesn’t fit, we stop after 30 days.

### “We already have partners.”
> That’s great — it means your customers like partnerships.  
> My offer is different because it’s [your unique angle].  
> We can test for 30 days and see if customers respond.

### “How do we handle complaints?”
> You don’t need to.  
> I handle all customer messages and issues directly.  
> Your team stays focused on your business.

### “What’s in it for us?”
> Two simple wins:  
> 1) Your customers get a trusted local food option (added value).  
> 2) If you want, we can add commission or a member perk.  
> I’m flexible — the goal is to make you look good.

## Step 8.5 — Partnership quality rules (protect your reputation)

Partnerships bring customers.
But you must protect quality.

Use these rules:
- **Limit quantity** until you know your capacity  
- Keep a consistent pickup window  
- Don’t promise delivery everywhere  
- Don’t expand the menu during a pilot  
- Keep packaging clean and reliable  
- Always communicate early if something changes

A small kitchen that is consistent beats a big kitchen that is chaotic.
Every time.

## Common mistakes (and quick fixes)

1) **Pitching too many ideas at once**  
Fix: start with one model (QR placement + pilot).

2) **No clear pickup time**  
Fix: one weekly window. Predictable.

3) **Trying to track commission in a complicated way**  
Fix: keep it monthly, simple, transparent.

4) **Partner location gets “chaos” from customers**  
Fix: you handle all customer messages, always.

5) **Partner doesn’t post or promote**  
Fix: write the post for them. Make it easy.

6) **Your offer doesn’t match the partner’s customers**  
Fix: adjust the offer (gym → protein/healthy; salon → comfort/treat).

7) **No follow-up**  
Fix: send a short monthly update with results.

8) **Quality drops when you get more orders**  
Fix: limit quantity and scale slowly.

## A realistic 12-week partnership plan (from “one pilot” to stable distribution)

This is a calm, realistic plan.
You are not trying to sign 20 partnerships.
You are trying to build **one stable distribution channel**, then add another.

### Weeks 1–2: Prepare + outreach
- Build your trust package (photos, about, reviews)
- Print or design your QR materials
- Contact 5 partners (Message 1)
- Book 1–2 short visits

**Goal:** get 1 pilot yes.

### Weeks 3–4: Run your first 30-day pilot
- Place materials
- Keep ordering simple
- Do one reminder post (you write it for the partner)
- Track basic results (orders, new customers, best sellers)

**Goal:** prove that it works without chaos.

### Weeks 5–6: Improve the offer (small upgrade)
- Adjust the offer based on what sold best
- Improve packaging or pickup time if needed
- Ask for 1–2 customer reviews (social proof)

**Goal:** make the system smoother.

### Weeks 7–8: Close the “real” deal
- Share pilot results with the partner
- Offer your deal menu (Option A/B/C)
- Lock a weekly rhythm (pickup window + reminder)

**Goal:** turn pilot into stable distribution.

### Weeks 9–10: Add the second partner (same system)
- Use your first results as proof
- Pitch 5 more partners
- Start a second pilot

**Goal:** build a small network.

### Weeks 11–12: Stabilize (quality + routines)
- Review capacity and quality
- Limit orders if needed (protect your reputation)
- Create a simple monthly partner update
- Decide your next expansion (third partner or recurring pickup)

**Goal:** stable income comes from stable routines.

## Simple metrics to track (no spreadsheets required)

Track these 5 numbers monthly:
1) Orders from partners
2) Repeat customers from partners
3) Average order size from partners
4) Best-selling offer (for each partner type)
5) Time cost (how many hours it took)

If one partnership gives you good orders but steals too much time:
- simplify the offer
- limit quantity
- change pickup windows
- or shift to fixed fee + fewer orders, higher value

The best partnership is the one that supports your life, not just your revenue.
