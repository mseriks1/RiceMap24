## 11C — Deliverability basics (SPF / DKIM / DMARC in plain language)

Deliverability means: emails actually land in inbox, not spam.

Three common settings help:
- **SPF**: tells inbox providers which servers can send for your domain
- **DKIM**: adds a signature to prove the email is authentic
- **DMARC**: a policy that tells providers how to treat suspicious mail

Most email platforms provide step-by-step DNS records.
You typically copy/paste them into your domain DNS settings.

If you do nothing, emails can still send — but deliverability is weaker.
If you set SPF/DKIM, you look more trustworthy to inbox providers.

Keep it simple:
Use the platform’s recommended setup.

# Part 21 — Technical deliverability (deeper but still plain language)

Deliverability is the difference between:
“email marketing works” and “email marketing feels dead.”

Inbox providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo) protect users from spam.
They look for signals that you are legitimate.

## The 5 deliverability levers that matter most
1) **SPF + DKIM configured**
2) **Send from a real domain**
3) **Warm up the list gradually** (don’t blast 5,000 emails on day 1)
4) **Low bounce rate** (don’t add random emails)
5) **Good engagement** (people open/click/reply)

If you do these, you’re already ahead of most small businesses.

---

## SPF in one paragraph
SPF is a DNS record that tells inbox providers:
“These services are allowed to send email using my domain.”

Your email platform will give you a TXT record like:
v=spf1 include:someprovider.com ~all

You paste it into your domain DNS.

If you already have an SPF record, you typically add the provider’s include.
(Only one SPF record per domain is recommended—merge includes instead of creating duplicates.)

---

## DKIM in one paragraph
DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to emails.
It proves the email wasn’t altered and that it truly comes from your domain.

Your platform gives you:
- a selector name
- a TXT record value
You paste it into DNS.

---

## DMARC (optional but helpful)
DMARC tells providers what to do if SPF/DKIM fail.
Many small businesses start with a “monitor” policy.

If you’re not comfortable, you can skip DMARC early.
But SPF + DKIM are worth doing.

# Part 23 — Double opt-in vs single opt-in (what to choose)

Some platforms offer:
- **Single opt-in**: email goes straight onto list
- **Double opt-in**: user confirms via a confirmation email

Double opt-in can improve list quality and deliverability.
Single opt-in can grow faster.

For a home kitchen, double opt-in is often a safe choice because:
- fewer fake emails
- better engagement
- fewer spam complaints

But if your list is small and you capture emails in person, single opt-in can be fine.

Choose the option that matches your risk tolerance and local norms.

# Part 32 — Troubleshooting deliverability (when emails land in spam)

If you notice low opens, or customers say they didn’t receive emails, check:

1) **SPF/DKIM** configured correctly  
2) **From address** consistent and on your domain  
3) **Too many images** and too little text  
4) **Spammy words** (“FREE!!!”, “BUY NOW”, excessive caps)  
5) **Broken links** or too many links  
6) **Low engagement** (send more consistently to rebuild)  

### Simple deliverability rescue plan (2 weeks)
- send one helpful email (menu + clarity) to VIP first
- ask one question to encourage replies
- keep design simple
- then send to the rest

Replies are a strong positive signal for inbox providers.
