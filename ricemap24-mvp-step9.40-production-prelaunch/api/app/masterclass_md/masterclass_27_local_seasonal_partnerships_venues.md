## Big idea (general business first): borrowed foot traffic is gold

In local business, growth is often limited by one thing: **access to people**.

Venues already have:
- traffic (people physically present)
- rhythm (events, weekends, seasons)
- trust (members and communities)
- a real need (food solves a problem)

So a venue partnership is basically a distribution deal:
**you bring the product, the venue brings the audience.**

This is the same model you see everywhere:
- cafés selling a local bakery’s pastries
- gyms selling meal prep packs
- event locations partnering with caterers
- sports clubs running kiosks

The win is simple:
You avoid “posting harder” or running ads for every sale.
Instead, you plug into a place where customers already are.

But you only win if the partnership is designed well:
- clear format
- clear timing
- clear responsibilities
- clear deal terms
- quality control

That’s what this masterclass gives you.

## Why venue partners can beat delivery platforms

Delivery platforms can work, but they often come with:
- heavy fees
- less control
- reputation risk tied to delivery variance
- customer relationship owned by the platform

Venue partnerships often give:
- better margin (fixed fee, lower rev share)
- more predictability (preorders, fixed days)
- easier logistics (one delivery, one handover)
- long-term relationships (season contracts)

They are often the calmest route to stable income.

# Part 2 — The partner scoring system (choose the right venues)

Before you invest time, score the venue quickly (1–5 for each):

1) **Traffic:** do they actually have people consistently?  
2) **Need:** do they have a real food need (not “maybe someday”)?  
3) **Decision maker:** can you reach the person who can say yes?  
4) **Fit:** does your format fit their setting (time, storage, service)?  
5) **Logistics:** can delivery/handover be simple and consistent?  
6) **Trust:** do they seem organized and respectful (low drama)?

Score total out of 30:
- 24+ = strong partner candidate  
- 18–23 = test only with a small pilot  
- <18 = likely not worth it right now  

This prevents you from chasing “cool ideas” that won’t convert.

# Part 3 — Choose a format (don’t start with “random food”)

Venues do not want a complicated menu.
They want an easy solution.

Your best strategy is to sell a **format**, not a big menu.

Examples of strong venue formats:
- lunch boxes (fast, portable)
- trays for groups (events)
- snack packs (kiosk speed)
- cold‑ready lunch (clean and stable)
- dessert cups (high margin)
- finish‑at‑home family packs (weekend preorder)

A good partnership offer is:
**one venue + one format + one day/time.**

# Part 4 — The 6 partnership deal models (pick the right one)

## Model 1 — Fixed fee per drop (simple)
The venue buys X units (or pays a fixed fee for supply).
You deliver at a fixed time.

Best for:
- camps, conferences, planned events
- clubhouses that want predictability

## Model 2 — Revenue share / commission
Venue sells your products and keeps a percentage.

Best for:
- kiosks that actively sell
- venues with staff handling sales

## Model 3 — Preorder system (best for zero waste)
Customers preorder via QR (or venue collects orders).
You deliver what is ordered.

Best for:
- golf clubs
- sports events
- weekend venues

## Model 4 — Minimum order protection
You only deliver if minimum volume is reached.
This protects your time and cost.

Best for:
- uncertain partners
- early pilots

## Model 5 — Shelf space / monthly fee
Your product sits in their fridge/display, sold on-site.
Either you pay for shelf space or they buy wholesale.

Best for:
- dessert cups
- snack boxes
- chilled items with labels

## Model 6 — 30‑day pilot (the “yes” machine)
A pilot reduces risk and increases yes-rate.
You set a small test:
- 2–4 products
- one day per week
- preorder or minimum
- evaluate after 4 weeks

Pilots turn “maybe” into “yes”.

# Part 5 — The 30‑day pilot framework (how to make it safe)

A good pilot has four features:
1) **Small menu**
2) **Clear timing**
3) **Low waste risk (preorder/minimum)**
4) **Clear evaluation metrics**

### The simplest pilot structure
- 2 products (hero + alternative)
- 1 add-on (dessert or snack)
- 1 day per week
- preorder cutoff or minimum volume
- evaluate after 4 weeks

This is easy for the venue and easy for you.

# Part 6 — Preorder vs minimum order (choose the right safety tool)

## Preorder is best when…
- audience is predictable and willing to plan
- you can print QR + simple instructions
- you want near-zero waste

## Minimum order is best when…
- the venue wants “walk-in” sales
- the audience is uncertain
- you need protection from low volume

You can also combine them:
- preorder encouraged
- but a small minimum volume guaranteed by venue

That’s a very strong structure.

# Part 8 — Handover SOP (quality protection when they “host” your food)

Venue partnerships fail when quality responsibility is unclear.
So you create a simple handover SOP.

## Handover SOP (copy/paste)
**Delivery window:** [day/time]  
**Drop location:** [exact place]  
**Receiver:** [name/role]  
**Storage rule:**  
- cold items → refrigerated  
- hot items → served within [time] or held warm safely  
**2-minute receiving check:**  
- count items  
- check labels  
- check sauce cups (if relevant)  
**Customer questions:**  
- who answers? [venue/you]  
**Complaints:**  
- first contact: [venue]  
- escalation to you: [phone/message]  
**Resolution ladder:**  
- small issue → credit next time  
- medium issue → partial refund/credit  
- big issue → full refund/replace  
**Internal fix note:**  
- one improvement for next week

This keeps trust intact and prevents blame games.

4) Complaints (count + type)

6) Stress score (1–10)  
7) One improvement for next week

### Weekly venue report (template)
- Sold: __  
- Best seller: __  
- Leftovers/waste: __  
- Complaints: __ (type: __)  
- Profit estimate: __  
- Stress score: __  
- One improvement: __  

If stress is high and profit is low, redesign or stop.
If stress is low and profit is good, make it recurring.

# Part 13 — Negotiation (how to structure a fair deal)

A lot of partnerships fail because the deal is vague.
The venue asks:
“Can you just bring some food and we’ll see?”

That sounds friendly, but it often turns into:
- low volume
- high waste
- unclear responsibility
- frustration

So your job is to offer **clarity** in a warm way.

## The three fairness anchors
When negotiating, anchor the conversation around:

1) **Workload**  
Your time has value. Production + packaging + delivery is real work.

2) **Waste risk**  
If nobody guarantees volume, you carry the waste. That’s not sustainable.

3) **Distribution value**  
A venue with strong traffic and strong trust is valuable. A venue with weak traffic is not.

A fair deal balances these three.

---

## Choosing the right deal model (quick guide)

### If the venue has strong traffic and staff that sells
Revenue share can work.

### If the venue is uncertain or “testing”
Use preorder or minimum order.

### If it’s a camp or conference with known volume
Fixed fee / fixed volume is best.

### If they want fridge items (desserts/snacks)
Shelf space or wholesale is often best.

The trick is: don’t force one model everywhere.
Pick what fits the venue.

---

## The calm negotiation sentences (copy/paste)

- “To keep this sustainable, we need either preorder or a minimum volume.”
- “If you can guarantee X units, I can keep the price stable and quality high.”
- “We can start with a 30-day pilot so it feels low risk for both sides.”
- “If the pilot works, we can lock it in as a weekly rhythm for the season.”

These are professional, not pushy.

# Part 14 — Pricing (how to avoid a ‘bad deal’)

Venues often ask for low prices.
But your business must stay healthy.

## The pricing truth
You are not competing with supermarket sandwiches.
You are offering:
- convenience
- quality
- local identity
- a better venue experience

So you price as a service + product.

### The three cost buckets to include
- ingredients
- packaging
- time + delivery

Then choose a model:
- wholesale price (venue resells)
- retail price with commission (venue keeps a %)
- fixed fee for supply

### A simple safety rule
If a deal makes you feel stressed and underpaid during the pilot,
it will feel worse when scaled.
Fix the deal early.

# Part 15 — Preorder system (QR) done right

Preorder is often the best model because it:
- protects you from waste
- gives predictable volume
- makes production calmer

## The simplest preorder flow
1) Venue displays a small QR poster/card  
2) Customers preorder by deadline  
3) You deliver exact quantity  
4) Venue hands out or sells preordered items  

You can keep it simple:
- one weekly cutoff time
- one delivery window

## Two QR variants that work
- **Variant A (menu + preorder):** “Order by Thursday 20:00. Pickup Saturday 12–14.”
- **Variant B (event preorder):** “Order by Friday 12:00 for Saturday game day.”

Short, clear, predictable.

# Part 16 — When you should use minimum order instead

Minimum order is better when:
- the venue wants walk-in sales
- they don’t want preorder friction
- audience is unpredictable

Example:
- sports kiosk with random crowds

In that case:
- venue commits to buying X units (minimum)
- they sell walk-in
- any extra is on them, not on you

That is fair because they control the traffic.

# Part 17 — Quality control when the venue stores/serves your product

This is where reputation can break.

You must define:
- storage rules
- shelf life rules
- hot vs cold instructions
- “what happens if something goes wrong”

## The 3 quality protection layers
1) **Label clarity** (hot/cold/reheat, date)
2) **Handover check** (count + labels + sauces)
3) **Complaint routing** (who handles what)

If you do only these, quality complaints drop.

# Part 25 — Shelf space, wholesale, and “reseller” models (when the venue sells for you)

Some venues don’t want a weekly drop event.
They want product they can sell any day.

This is common for:
- café counters
- small shops with a fridge
- gym cafés
- clubhouses with staff

In that situation, you can use two models:

## Model A — Wholesale (venue buys, then resells)
- Venue buys units at a wholesale price
- Venue sets retail price and keeps the margin

**Pros**
- you get paid immediately
- zero waste risk (for you)
- simple relationship

**Cons**
- venue may push for lower price
- you lose control of retail pricing

This works best for:
- dessert cups
- snack packs
- cold-ready items with clear labels

## Model B — Consignment (you supply, venue pays after sale)
- You place product
- Venue pays you only for sold units

**Pros**
- low barrier for venue to say yes
- easy to test

**Cons**
- higher risk for you
- you need clear tracking and pickup of unsold items

If you do consignment, protect yourself with:
- clear shelf-life rules
- a weekly settle day
- a simple inventory sheet

# Part 26 — Food safety and responsibility (keep it clear and calm)

Venue partners often worry about:
- storage responsibility
- allergens
- complaints
- “who is responsible if something goes wrong”

You don’t need legal language.
You need clarity.

## The responsibility split (simple)
- **You are responsible for production quality and correct labeling**
- **Venue is responsible for storage and handling after handover**
- **Complaints are routed through the agreed process**

Add two simple safety habits:
- label with production date
- label hot/cold/reheat clearly

For group events, keep allergen notes simple but visible.

# Part 28 — Scaling to two venues (how to avoid chaos)

One venue can be stable.
Two venues can be chaos if you don’t systemize.

Use these rules:

1) Standardize packaging and labels  
2) Keep each venue menu small (2–4 products)  
3) Keep delivery windows consistent  
4) Avoid overlapping peak times  
5) Track KPIs weekly for each venue separately  

### The best scaling order
- build one venue stable for 4–6 weeks
- then add a second venue
- never add two at the same time

This prevents burnout.
