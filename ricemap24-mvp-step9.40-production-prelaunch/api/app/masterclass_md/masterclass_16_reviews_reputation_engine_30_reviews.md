## Big idea (positive and powerful)

In almost every local business, reviews work like this:

1) People don’t know you  
2) They feel uncertain  
3) They look for proof  
4) Reviews reduce fear  
5) They buy

This is true for:
- restaurants
- hair salons
- gyms
- home repair
- clinics
- local services

Reviews are not only “nice”.
They are a **trust shortcut**.

And trust shortcuts matter because customers rarely have time to research deeply.
They want a quick “yes”.

So your goal is not to beg for reviews.
Your goal is to build a system that makes reviews happen naturally.

That system is what we call:
**the Reviews & Reputation Engine.**

## How reviews actually happen (the truth)

Most customers do not leave reviews, even if they loved it.
Not because they don’t care.
But because:
- they forget
- they are busy
- they don’t want to write long text
- they don’t know where to do it
- the process feels annoying

So the review engine is basically:
**remove friction + ask at the right moment.**

That’s it.

We build a system with 5 parts:

## Step 1 — Choose one “main review location”

This masterclass is about system, not platforms.
But you still need one main place.

Pick one main review location and keep it consistent.

Good options for local businesses:
- Google reviews (often strongest)
- Facebook page reviews (if your audience is there)
- Another consistent place you already use

You can still accept reviews elsewhere.
But your “engine” should push toward one main place.

Why?
Because concentration matters.
30 reviews in one place is stronger than 30 spread everywhere.

## Step 3 — The review pipeline (from happy customer → published review)

This is the core.

### Pipeline steps
1) Customer orders  
2) Customer picks up / receives food  
3) Customer eats and feels satisfied  
4) You ask (at the right moment)  
5) You make it easy (one link, short ask)  
6) Customer leaves review  
7) You thank them and reuse proof (optional)

The pipeline is simple.
The difference is doing it every week.

---

## Step 4 — Timing (the 3 best review moments)

Most kitchens ask at the wrong time.
They ask too early or too late.

Here are the three best moments:

### Moment 1 — “Right after the first good experience”
For new customers:
- after they confirm they enjoyed it
- same day or next morning

### Moment 2 — “After the second order” (best conversion)
Repeat customers are the easiest review source.
They already trust you.

Ask after:
- their second order
- or third order

This feels natural:
“They’re clearly a fan.”

### Moment 3 — “After a problem was handled well”
If something went wrong and you fixed it warmly,
customers often become more loyal.

If they say “thank you” after you fixed it,
that is a review moment.

## Step 5 — The “not pushy” ask language

Your ask should feel like:
- a small favor
- helpful for small business
- optional
- short

Avoid:
- guilt
- pressure
- long paragraphs

A perfect review ask is 2–3 lines.

## Step 6 — The QR mini-system (2–3 QR variants)

QR is powerful because it removes friction.
But it can feel spammy if you plaster it everywhere.

So we do QR with purpose.

### QR Variant A — New customer QR
Text vibe:
“New here? If you enjoyed it, a quick review helps a lot ❤️”

### QR Variant B — Repeat customer QR
Text vibe:
“Love being part of the community? Leave a quick review ❤️”

### QR Variant C — Office/B2B QR
Text vibe:
“Team lunch feedback? Quick review helps us grow ❤️”

You don’t need long text.
One line is enough.

### Where to place QR (without spam)
- inside the bag (small card)
- on a simple thank-you card
- on pickup table sign (optional)
- on invoice/receipt page (optional)

Avoid:
- huge posters screaming “review us”
That feels aggressive.

---

## Step 7 — The “two-click rule” (friction control)

Your rule:
**customer should be able to leave a review in 2 clicks.**

If your review process requires:
- searching your name
- choosing location
- logging in
- scrolling

…many customers will drop off.

So:
- use one direct link (or QR to direct link)
- keep instructions minimal
- avoid extra steps

## Damage control routine (negative reviews playbook)

Negative reviews happen.
The goal is not perfection.
The goal is professional response.

Your response should:
- protect trust
- show calm
- show responsibility
- offer a solution
- avoid arguing

### The 5-step damage control routine
1) **Pause** (do not reply emotionally)  
2) **Acknowledge** (thank them for feedback)  
3) **Clarify** (ask for details in DM)  
4) **Fix** (refund/credit/replace ladder)  
5) **Improve** (internal note for next week)  

### The “one sentence you can always use”
“Thank you for telling us — I’m sorry that happened. We take it seriously and we’ll make it right.”

That line alone reduces conflict.

### When to move to DM
Move to DM when:
- you need order details
- it’s a sensitive issue
- it’s becoming a long argument

Public comment should be short.
DM can be longer.

## Output 3 — Damage control scripts (ready to use)

### Script A — Short public reply
> Thank you for the feedback — I’m sorry that happened.  
> Please message me your order details so I can make it right ❤️

### Script B — DM clarification
> Thanks for reaching out ❤️ Can you tell me what you ordered and when you picked up?

> I’m sorry again ❤️ Here are the options I can offer:

> 2) partial refund

> Thank you for telling me — I’m really sorry to hear that.  
> Your health is most important. Please share what you ordered and when you ate it.

## The “ask segmentation” (who to ask first)

Don’t ask everyone equally.
Ask the people most likely to say yes.

### Segment A — Repeat customers (highest yes-rate)
Ask these first.
They already trust you.

### Segment B — New customers who sent a compliment
If they wrote “so good”, ask immediately.

### Segment C — Office / group orders
Ask the contact person, and invite 2–3 quick reviews from team members.

### Segment D — Customers you helped during a problem
If you fixed it professionally, they may become your strongest advocates.

### Segment E — Friends and family (use carefully)
This can help you start, but don’t rely on it long-term.
Your engine should be customer-based.

If you ask Segment A + B consistently, you can reach 30 quickly.

## QR design that doesn’t feel spammy (simple guidelines)

QR works best when it feels like a thank-you, not an advertisement.

### QR card size
Small card inside the bag is perfect.
It feels personal.

### QR text (one line)
Keep it to one line, like:
- “If you enjoyed it, a quick review helps ❤️”
- “Thank you — feedback helps us grow ❤️”

Avoid:
- “REVIEW US NOW”
- “5 STAR ONLY”
That feels manipulative and harms trust.

### QR placement
Best placements:
- inside bag
- on thank-you card
- on pickup table sign (optional)
- on office tray label (optional)

Worst placements:
- huge aggressive posters in public spaces
- repeated messages everywhere

A review engine should feel warm.

## Damage control: decision tree (what to do when a bad review happens)

Not every bad review is the same.
So we use a decision tree.

### Case A — Legit complaint (you probably made a mistake)
Response:
- acknowledge
- apologize
- move to DM
- offer fix
- improve system

### Case B — Misunderstanding (expectation issue)
Response:
- acknowledge
- clarify politely
- move to DM
- improve messaging (so it doesn’t repeat)

### Case C — Unreasonable / aggressive
Response:
- stay calm
- short reply
- invite DM
- do not argue
Then stop.

### Case D — Health claim
Response:
- serious
- calm
- gather details
- review process
- document internally

You protect trust by being calm and responsible, not defensive.

your goal is a warm system, not pressure.

A good review engine is honest.
Don’t destroy trust.

- ask happy customers
- respond calmly to negative feedback
- fix real problems
- treat reviews as community proof

Honesty is a long-term advantage.

## Handling “not bad, not great” reviews (3‑star situations)

Not every review will be 5 stars.
That’s okay.

The worst thing you can do is argue.
The best thing you can do is learn.

### If someone leaves 3 stars with feedback
Public reply:
> Thank you for the honest feedback ❤️ We appreciate it and will improve.  
> If you’re open to sharing details in DM, I’d love to understand what we can do better.

Then DM:
- ask what they wanted
- offer a small solution if fair
- write one internal improvement note

This shows professionalism.

### If someone leaves 3 stars with no text
You can still reply:
> Thank you for trying us ❤️ If you ever have feedback, I’d love to hear it.

Then move on.

## Negative review response templates (4 common types)

### Type 1 — Missing item / wrong order
Public reply:
> I’m sorry that happened — thank you for telling us.

### Type 2 — Leak / messy packaging
Public reply:
> Thank you for the feedback — I’m sorry about the mess.  
> Please DM me so I can make it right and improve our packing ❤️

### Type 3 — Cold food complaint (hot menu)
Public reply:
> I’m sorry it wasn’t as expected.  
> Please DM me your pickup time and order details so I can review what happened and fix it ❤️

### Type 4 — “Got sick” claim
Public reply:
> I’m really sorry to hear that. Thank you for telling us.  
> Please DM your order details and timing so I can take this seriously and review our full process.

Short.
Serious.
No arguing.
