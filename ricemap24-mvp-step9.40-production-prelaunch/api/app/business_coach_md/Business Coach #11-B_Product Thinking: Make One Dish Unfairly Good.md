# Business Coach #11-B — Product Thinking: Make One Dish Unfairly Good
## The “anchor dish” that customers crave, remember, and repeat

There is a moment when a home kitchen stops being “a place that sells food” and becomes something stronger:

A place that sells a craving.

Cravings build repeat customers.
Repeat customers build stability.
Stability builds freedom.

And cravings are often created by one thing:

**One dish that is unfairly good.**

This month is about making one dish so strong—so memorable—that it becomes your anchor.

Not because you need a huge menu.
Because one anchor dish can carry an entire business.

---

## The anchor dish principle

An anchor dish does three things at once:

1) It attracts first-time customers (“I have to try that.”)  
2) It creates repeat behavior (“I want that again.”)  
3) It builds your identity (“That kitchen with *that dish*.”)

In Asian kitchens, this is especially powerful because flavor memory is strong:
- aroma
- sauce balance
- texture
- heat level
- umami depth

When you get the balance right, customers remember you.

---

## “Unfairly good” is built from a few small choices

You don’t need 20 upgrades.
You need 3 strong decisions.

### 1) One dominant flavor identity
Make it clear:
- sweet-spicy
- garlic-ginger umami
- coconut-lime comfort
- smoky chili depth
- sesame-soy richness

If the flavor identity is clear, customers remember it.

### 2) Texture that survives real life
Your dish must survive:
- packaging
- transport
- waiting a few minutes

That means product design:
- separate crunchy topping
- sauce on the side
- vented container for crispy items
- double-layer method for heat retention

This is not overthinking.
This is protecting the eating moment.

### 3) A “signature detail”
A small detail that makes it yours:
- a house sauce
- a finishing oil
- a pickled side
- a herb crunch
- an intentional spice level option

This detail becomes your identity.

---

## Make it repeatable (the real secret)

A dish becomes “unfairly good” when it is repeatable.

Repeatable means:
- the taste is consistent
- the portion is consistent
- the sauce balance is consistent
- the customer knows what they will get

If you want repeat customers, you must become repeatable.

This is not boring.
This is mastery.

---

## Packaging for the “moment of eating” (a product owner advantage)

If you want your anchor dish to become a repeat craving, you must protect the moment of eating.

Ask:
- “Will this still taste great after 15–20 minutes?”
- “Will the texture survive?”
- “Will it still look appetizing?”
- “Will the customer feel confident opening it?”

Small packaging choices can protect big flavor:
- separate sauce cups for crisp items
- vented lids when steam would ruin crunch
- insulated bags for heat retention on cold days
- a clean, stable container that prevents spills

This is not luxury.
This is product strategy.

---

## The proud cultural advantage

If your kitchen has a cultural foundation, lean into it.

Customers are tired of generic food.
They want real flavor, real logic, real comfort.

A culturally anchored dish has:
- story
- authenticity
- confidence

It does not need a long explanation.
It needs consistency.

Consistency is what makes authenticity feel trustworthy.

---

## Your “cultural signature” can be your product moat

Many customers want what chains cannot offer:
- real aroma
- bold spice logic
- authentic balance
- dishes that taste like a real home or street kitchen

When you treat cultural authenticity as a product advantage, you stop trying to imitate restaurants.

You build something more valuable:
a dish people can’t replace easily.

That is your moat.

---

## Build one quality rule for the anchor dish

Choose one rule that protects your signature dish:

Examples:
- “Sauce is always measured, not guessed.”
- “Crunchy element stays separate until eating.”
- “Finish with the same topping every time.”
- “Spice level options are consistent and intentional.”

One rule is enough.
One rule builds trust.

---

## The owner rule that keeps it simple

**Make the anchor dish easier to repeat than to change.**

When it is easier to repeat, you build mastery.
When you build mastery, customers trust you.
When customers trust you, they return.
