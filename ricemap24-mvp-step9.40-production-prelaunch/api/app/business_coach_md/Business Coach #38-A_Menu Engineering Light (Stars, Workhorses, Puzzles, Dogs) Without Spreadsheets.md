# Business Coach #38-A — Menu Engineering Light (Stars, Workhorses, Puzzles, Dogs) Without Spreadsheets
## The simplest way to see what to protect, redesign, or remove—using four dish types

Menu decisions get easier when you stop thinking:
“Do I like this dish?”

…and start thinking:
**“What role does this dish play in the business?”**

This month is a light, practical version of menu engineering—no spreadsheets required.

It helps you classify every dish into one of four types:
- **Stars**
- **Workhorses**
- **Puzzles**
- **Dogs**

Then your actions become obvious:
- protect
- redesign
- reposition
- remove

This model works perfectly with RiceMap24 when you use:
- Top dishes (Last 30 days / month / quarter)
- your own knowledge of cost/time intensity
- your profit-per-dish thinking (rough, honest estimates)
- repeat behavior from customers

And remember:
**Never remove what sells well. Protect anchors.**
This model helps you protect anchors *and* improve profit.

---

## 1) What the four dish types mean (simple)

### Type 1: Stars (high demand + good profit)
Stars:
- sell often
- repeat well
- feel easy to run
- protect net profit

Stars are your business heroes.

### Type 2: Workhorses (high demand + thin profit)
Workhorses:
- sell often
- but margins are weaker
- they keep volume, but may not pay you back enough

Workhorses are common in food businesses.
They are not “bad.”
They simply need an owner move.

### Type 3: Puzzles (low demand + good profit)
Puzzles:
- could be profitable
- but they don’t sell enough
- sometimes customers don’t understand them
- sometimes the name or photo is confusing
- sometimes they need better lane positioning

Puzzles are opportunity, not failure.

### Type 4: Dogs (low demand + thin profit)
Dogs:
- don’t sell much
- are not very profitable
- often create ingredient waste or complexity

Dogs are usually the easiest improvement:
remove or restrict.

---

## 2) How to classify dishes without spreadsheets

You only need two questions:

1) **Demand:** Does it sell often and repeat?  
2) **Payback:** Does it feel like it pays you back for ingredients + packaging + time?

If demand is high and payback is good → Star  
If demand is high and payback is weak → Workhorse  
If demand is low and payback is good → Puzzle  
If demand is low and payback is weak → Dog

You can do this quickly with your top dishes list + honest owner intuition.

---

## 3) What to do with each dish type (the owner actions)

### Stars: protect and standardize
Owner actions:
- keep them always available (anchors)
- protect portion and packaging standards
- protect naming consistency
- make them the center of bundles/add-ons

Stars build stability.

---

### Workhorses: redesign to improve margin
Workhorses are your “quiet profit leak.”

Owner actions:
- standardize portions (stop drift)
- simplify ingredients (overlap system)
- redesign packaging (reduce cups/components)
- add a premium add-on option (increase order value)
- consider a small price update if signals support it

You usually don’t remove workhorses.
You fix them.

---

### Puzzles: reposition and clarify
Puzzles might be great, but customers don’t choose them.

Owner actions:
- improve naming and clarity (one flavor headline)
- reposition into the right lane (lunch, family, treat)
- simplify choice (make it one obvious option)
- consider making it a rotation item with clear availability

Puzzles are often marketing/clarity problems, not product problems.

---

### Dogs: remove, restrict, or redesign hard
Dogs cost you:
- waste
- complexity
- attention

Owner actions:
- remove them
or
- move them to special-order only
or
- redesign using overlap ingredients

Removing one dog often improves your whole month.

---

## 4) The “menu engineering” insight most owners miss: workhorses matter

Many owners think:
“My top seller must be my best dish.”

Not always.

Sometimes your top seller is a workhorse:
- it sells, but margins are thin
- it drains energy
- it increases waste

If you improve one workhorse, your net profit can change dramatically—without selling more.

That’s why this model is powerful.

---

## 5) Keep creativity safe: one rotation slot

To avoid chaos:
- keep stars stable
- keep workhorse redesign focused (one lever)
- keep puzzles as rotation tests
- remove dogs

And protect creativity with one rotation slot only.

This keeps your menu alive and controlled.

---

## 6) Use a 30-day window to learn (simple)

Pick one dish in each category and do one action:
- protect a star standard
- redesign one workhorse lever
- clarify one puzzle headline
- remove one dog

Then compare:
- last 30 days vs previous 30 days

If net improves and stress decreases, the model worked.

## 7) A fast “dish type review” template (use quarterly)

If you want a simple quarterly ritual:
- list your top 8–12 dishes
- classify them: Star / Workhorse / Puzzle / Dog
- choose one action per type

This takes minutes and prevents menu drift for months.

## 8) Examples of each dish type (so classification feels easy)

You don’t need exact numbers to understand the categories.
You need patterns.

### Star patterns
- customers reorder it without thinking
- it works for your best segment (lunch, family, office)
- it travels cleanly and tastes consistent
- it doesn’t require rare ingredients

### Workhorse patterns
- sells often but feels “too cheap for the effort”
- requires many steps or lots of protein cost
- packaging is component-heavy
- you feel busy but net doesn’t improve

### Puzzle patterns
- you love it and it’s profitable when sold
- but customers don’t choose it
- the name may be unclear
- the photo may not communicate the value
- the lane placement may be wrong (e.g., dinner dish marketed to lunch buyers)

### Dog patterns
- sells rarely
- creates waste/spoilage
- adds ingredient complexity
- causes confusion or complaints

When you recognize patterns, classification becomes fast.

---

## 9) The “puzzle fix” toolkit (why puzzles are often easy wins)

Puzzles are often the fastest wins because you don’t need to change the recipe.
You often only need to change how it is presented.

Puzzle fixes:
- simplify the name (one flavor headline)
- highlight the main benefit (portable / spicy / comfort)
- move it into a clear lane (lunch or treat)
- pair it with a natural add-on (bundle logic)
- make availability clear (rotation week)

The goal is not more items.
The goal is clarity.

---

## 10) Workhorse improvement ideas (how to turn volume into profit)

Workhorses are where many businesses lose margin.

Workhorse upgrades:
- portion standard (stop drift)
- ingredient overlap redesign (reduce waste)
- packaging simplification (reduce cups/components)
- add-on structure that increases AOV
- pricing micro update (if justified by costs and signals)

You do not need five changes.
You need one lever.

One lever turns a workhorse into a star over time.

---

## 11) The “dog removal” confidence (why removing is growth)

Removing dishes can feel emotional.

But removal is often the fastest way to:
- improve quality
- reduce waste
- reduce stress
- simplify purchasing
- increase net profit

Removal is not “giving up.”
Removal is choosing what the business will carry.

Businesses become strong by subtraction.

---

## 12) Menu engineering also protects creativity

This model frees creativity because it protects your foundation.

- Stars stay stable  
- Workhorses get upgraded  
- Puzzles become experiments  
- Dogs are removed  

Now you can create inside a system.

That is how creativity becomes sustainable.
