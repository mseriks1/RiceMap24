# Business Coach #37-A — Customer Segments And Your Best Customer Profile (Profit + Stability)
## Stop treating “customers” as one group—build around the customers who actually make your business stable

One of the biggest upgrades an experienced owner can make is this:

Stop thinking “I sell to everyone.”  
Start thinking “I build for my best customers.”

Because not all customers create the same kind of business.

Some customers:
- order often
- order smoothly
- increase order value
- become predictable habits

Other customers:
- order rarely
- create complexity
- negotiate or change a lot
- don’t repeat

This is not judgement.
This is strategy.

This month is about customer segments—the simplest way to understand:
- who drives profit
- who drives stability
- who your menu should actually be designed for
- what kind of “lane” you should lean into

And the best part:
you can do this without complex analytics.

You can do it with:
- your Customer List (tags/notes)
- Top customers over time (if you link customers)
- your own real-life observation
- order patterns you already see

---

## 1) What a “customer segment” is (simple definition)

A segment is a group of customers who behave similarly.

They share something:
- same ordering times
- similar needs
- similar order sizes
- similar sensitivity to price
- similar reasons for buying

When you know your segments, you stop guessing.

You design:
- menu
- packaging style
- pickup windows
- bundles and add-ons
- communication tone

…to fit the segment that matters most.

That is how businesses become stable.

---

## 2) Why “best customers” are a business strategy, not a preference

Best customers are the customers who produce the best combination of:
- profit
- repeat behavior
- smooth experience
- low stress

You are not “being unfair” by designing for them.
You are being smart.

Because building for best customers creates:
- stable income
- calmer operations
- stronger brand identity
- easier growth

And when the business is stable, you can serve everyone better anyway.

---

## 3) The most common segments for home kitchens (and how to spot them)

Here are common segments you will recognize quickly:

### Segment A: Weekday lunch customers
- value speed and clarity
- repeat weekly
- often order one item consistently
- can become habits fast

### Segment B: Office / small catering customers
- larger order value
- care about timing and clarity
- may request receipts
- can become a stable base if you deliver reliably

### Segment C: Family / group customers
- bigger orders
- often weekend-focused
- may buy bundles and add-ons
- can create strong AOV (average order value)

### Segment D: “Treat” customers
- order occasionally
- want something special and memorable
- less frequent, but can pay premium for delight

### Segment E: Price-sensitive bargain hunters
- choose based on cheapest option
- negotiate or compare constantly
- low loyalty unless discounts continue

All segments can exist.
But one or two segments usually drive the majority of stability and profit.

Your job is to identify those.

---

## 4) How to find your “best customer profile” (fast method)

You can do this in a simple way:

### Step 1: Identify your top repeat names in your memory
Who orders again and again?

### Step 2: Identify who creates the smoothest orders
Who is easy to serve?
Clear communication, predictable timing.

### Step 3: Identify who increases value
Who tends to buy add-ons, bundles, bigger orders?

### Step 4: Identify who feels aligned with your kitchen
Who loves your flavors and returns for them?

Your best customer profile is usually where these overlap.

---

## 5) Turn the best customer profile into a “menu design” decision

Once you know your best customers, you can design for them.

Examples:

### If your best customers are lunch customers:
- focus on portable, repeatable items (wraps/baguettes/bowls)
- make pickup windows lunch-friendly
- keep 1–2 lunch anchors stable
- keep ordering simple and clear

### If your best customers are families:
- bundles and add-ons become a core strategy
- “family packs” or multi-portion logic becomes valuable
- dessert/drink add-ons can increase AOV

### If your best customers are offices:
- reliability and clarity is premium
- receipts available when needed
- consistent labeling
- predictable timing and packaging

Design follows customer profile.
That is business maturity.

## 6) Use tags and notes to make segments visible (light, not heavy)

Customer List becomes powerful when you use a small tag set.

Example small tag set:
- Lunch
- Office
- Family
- VIP
- Allergy-aware

And one-line notes:
- “prefers mild”
- “orders Fridays”
- “office—needs receipt sometimes”
- “likes extra sauce”

This makes service smoother and segmentation clearer.

Even if you don’t do it perfectly, it improves quickly.

## 9) Segment-based “upgrade ideas” (fresh and practical)

Once you identify your best segment, your next upgrade becomes obvious.

### If Lunch is the best segment:
- create 1–2 “always available” lunch anchors
- keep ordering and pickup ultra simple
- keep one signature add-on (drink or crunchy side)

### If Office is the best segment:
- create a simple office bundle structure
- offer clear pickup/delivery timing
- keep labeling and receipts ready
- make the experience feel premium and predictable

### If Families are the best segment:
- create family bundles and add-ons
- make portion value and clarity obvious
- reduce menu confusion (fewer strong winners)

### If Treat customers are best:
- create 1 edge item that feels extraordinary
- use premium signals (clean packaging, signature finish)
- keep rotation exciting but controlled

Segments help you build the business intentionally.

## 10) Segment “pain points” (what each segment secretly cares about)

Segments are not only about timing.
They are about what customers worry about.

### Lunch customers care about:
- speed of decision
- clean eating experience
- consistency (same lunch every week)

Your advantage:
- clear lunch anchors + simple ordering

### Office customers care about:
- reliability and timing
- clarity for multiple people
- feeling professional (labels/receipts if needed)

Your advantage:
- predictable bundles + “no drama” experience

### Family customers care about:
- value feeling
- enough food and clarity
- easy add-ons and bundles

Your advantage:
- family packs + obvious add-ons

### Treat customers care about:
- memorable flavor
- something “special”
- premium feel

Your advantage:
- one extraordinary edge item + signature system

When you match the pain point, customers become loyal faster.

## 11) The “segment upgrade” that changes your whole month

If you want one high-impact move, it’s this:

**Pick one best segment for the next 30 days and design around it.**

That does not mean you reject other customers.
It means:
- menu choices prioritize the segment
- rotation choices fit the segment
- bundles/add-ons support the segment

Results become clearer because you stop being random.

---

## 12) Segment mapping: what you should keep stable vs rotate

A clean segment mapping looks like:

- Anchors (stable): designed for best segment  
- Rotation (one slot): used to keep interest inside the lane  
- Add-ons (few): chosen to increase value for the segment  

This makes the business feel like a product, not a list of dishes.

A lane becomes real when customers can describe you in one sentence.
