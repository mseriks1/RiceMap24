# Business Coach #29-A — Bundles And Add-Ons Strategy (Increase Order Value Without More Chaos)
## The calm way to raise order value: build “more value per pickup,” not “more stress per week”

One of the smartest ways to grow a home kitchen business is not only selling more orders.

It’s increasing value per order.

Because if you can increase the average order value (AOV), you often get:
- stronger net profit
- fewer total pickups needed for the same income
- less transport and messaging
- calmer operations

Bundles and add-ons are the owner tools for this—when done correctly.

Done incorrectly, they create:
- menu chaos
- too many ingredients
- packaging complexity
- waste

So this month is about building bundles and add-ons with a simple rule:

**Increase order value without increasing chaos.**

And remember:
**Never remove what sells well. Protect the anchors.**

---

## 1) Bundles and add-ons are product strategy, not “sales tricks”

A bundle is simply:
a package that makes it easier for customers to order more.

An add-on is:
a small extra item that pairs naturally with an anchor dish.

This is not manipulation.
This is good menu design.

Good menu design helps customers decide faster and feel more satisfied.

Satisfied customers repeat.

---

## 2) The “bundle ladder” (start small, then grow)

A calm way to introduce bundles is in levels:

### Level 1: Add-ons only (simplest)
- one side
- one sauce cup option
- one snack
- one dessert or drink

Add-ons should be:
- repeatable
- low prep stress
- easy packaging

### Level 2: Simple meal bundle
Example concept:
- main dish + side
or
- main dish + drink

This increases AOV while staying simple.

### Level 3: Family / office bundle
Example concept:
- 2–4 mains + sides + sauces
This is higher value, but only works if you have strong packaging and rhythm.

Owners climb the ladder slowly.

---

## 3) Add-ons should have ingredient overlap (profit and calm)

Add-ons are best when they reuse what you already buy:
- ingredients
- sauces
- toppings
- packaging types

Overlap reduces:
- waste
- purchasing complexity
- storage stress

This is why bundles can increase net:
they increase value using the same base system.

---

## 4) Bundles should be built around anchors (not around random items)

Your bundle should center on what already sells:
your anchor dish.

Anchor-based bundles:
- keep demand stable
- feel natural to customers
- reduce risk

Examples of bundle logic:
- “Anchor dish + crunchy side”
- “Anchor dish + dessert cup”
- “Anchor dish + signature drink”

The key is natural pairing.

Natural pairing makes ordering easier.

---

## 5) Packaging and assembly must stay simple

Bundles fail when packaging becomes chaos.

Use this owner rule:
**A bundle is only valid if packaging stays repeatable.**

That means:
- one packaging standard per dish type
- clear assembly order
- minimal extra steps

A bundle should reduce decisions, not add them.

---

## 6) Measure bundle success with your system

Bundles are not guesses.
You can measure them.

If you log income entries with dish lines, you can see:
- whether add-ons are selling
- whether bundle items are appearing more often
- whether top dishes list shifts
- whether net improves over time

Use:
- Last 30 days vs previous 30 days

Then ask:
- did income rise?
- did expenses rise?
- did net improve?

If net improves, keep it.
If chaos increases, simplify.

---

## 7) The “bundle win” mindset

A bundle win is not:
“we sold one big order.”

A bundle win is:
**customers start ordering the same combination repeatedly.**

Repeat combinations create:
- predictable prep
- predictable purchasing
- less waste
- stronger profits

Repeatability is the real goal.

---

## Your Owner Standard for this month

**“I increase order value with anchor-based add-ons and bundles that stay simple and repeatable.”**

That’s how you grow profit without growing chaos.


---

## A practical “add-on menu” that stays clean

To avoid chaos, keep add-ons limited and structured.

A clean add-on structure:
- 1 crunchy side
- 1 sauce option
- 1 dessert cup
- 1 signature drink

That’s enough.

The secret is not having many add-ons.
The secret is having add-ons that repeat.

---

## Add-ons also protect pricing without feeling expensive

If you want to increase value without raising the base price too much:
add-ons and bundles are powerful.

Customers feel:
“I chose more value.”

You feel:
“I protected net profit.”

That is a win-win.

---

## The “bundle assembly” rule

If your bundle requires three extra steps, it will create stress.

So the rule is:
**Bundles must assemble with your normal rhythm.**

Normal rhythm is how you keep quality high.

---

## Final reminder

Bundles are not a marketing trick.
They are menu architecture.

Menu architecture is business strategy.

---

## 8) Bundle ideas that match real customer behavior (without overcomplication)

Customers usually buy “combinations,” not random items.

So bundle design should match natural combinations:

### Main + Crunch
If your anchor dish is saucy or warm, a crunchy element adds contrast.
This can be:
- a simple crunchy side
- a topping cup (kept separate)
- a small crispy bite that survives packaging

### Main + Bright
A bright element increases perceived freshness:
- pickled side
- citrus note
- a small fresh garnish system (protected)

### Main + Sweet
Dessert is a strong emotional add-on:
- small, clean dessert cup
- travel-safe sweet bite
- something culturally aligned that feels memorable

### Main + Drink
A drink makes the order feel complete:
- one signature drink
- sealed, batchable, consistent

The owner trick is not adding many options.
It’s making the combinations feel obvious and easy.

---

## 9) The “add-on pricing” mindset (simple and profitable)

Add-ons are easiest when pricing is consistent:
- one price tier for small add-ons
- one price tier for premium add-ons

This reduces decision fatigue.
It also protects profit.

Customers accept add-ons when:
- they feel natural
- they feel clean and professional
- they feel worth it

---

## 10) Bundles should reduce your workload per kr/$ earned

An owner does not only ask:
“Will this sell?”

An owner asks:
**“Will this reduce friction per income?”**

Bundles win when they:
- increase value per pickup
- reduce total order count needed
- reduce messaging
- reduce transport stress

This is how AOV becomes calm growth.

---

## 11) Bundle systems that protect ingredients and waste

Add-ons can reduce waste when designed well.

Examples:
- use the same pickled element across multiple dishes
- use one dessert base with small variations
- use one sauce system across multiple formats
- use one crunchy topping system that is kept separate

Overlap makes everything easier:
- purchasing
- prep
- storage
- consistency

Overlap is profit.

---

## 12) How to test bundles without tasks

This Business Coach avoids “homework.”
So here is the mindset version:

- introduce one bundle concept as a stable option
- keep it consistent for a period
- watch whether customers repeat it naturally

Repeat behavior is the proof.
If it repeats, it becomes part of your system.

---

## 13) The “bundle discipline” rule

If bundles create chaos, it’s usually because there are too many.

A strong rule is:
- 1–2 bundles total
- 3–4 add-ons total

That keeps your menu clear and your business calm.

---

**“I increase order value by making it easy to order more—without adding complexity.”**
