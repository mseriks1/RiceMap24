## Big idea (general business first): “attention” and “intent” are different

Most small businesses live on social media.
That’s not wrong — but it’s incomplete.

There are two fundamentally different customer modes:

### Mode A — Scrolling (attention)
People are bored, curious, or killing time.
You can win:
- likes
- views
- follows

But they might not buy today.

### Mode B — Searching (intent)
People are trying to solve a problem:
- “food near me”
- “takeaway [area]”
- “gluten free [city]”
- “catering [city]”
- “lunch boxes [city]”
- “picnic pack [city]”

Search is not entertainment.
Search is a decision moment.

That’s why **findability** is one of the most underrated growth levers:
it captures customers when they are already motivated.

Your goal with local search is not to “go viral”.
Your goal is to become the easy answer when someone asks Google:
“Where can I get this?”


# Part 1 — The local search funnel (how people decide)

Local search is a funnel, even if it looks simple.

1) **Query**  
“Thai food near me” / “gluten free food [city]”

2) **Results**  
Maps pins, profiles, photos, reviews, snippets

3) **Trust scan (3 seconds)**  
- Does it look real?
- Do photos look clean and delicious?
- Are reviews good?
- Is it close enough?
- Is it open / pickup day clear?

4) **Action**  
- call / message
- click link
- order / reserve
- save for later

For home kitchens, the trust scan is everything.
Because people don’t know you.

So local SEO is mainly:
**trust engineering + clarity.**


# Part 2 — Findability for home kitchens (the special challenge)

# Part 4 — Local keyword strategy (without being spammy)

Local keywords are just the phrases people use.
Your job is to speak their language.

### Keyword groups that matter
1) **Cuisine + location**
“Filipino food [city]”
“Thai food [area]”

2) **Problem/format + location**
“takeaway [area]”
“catering [city]”
“lunch packs [city]”
“party trays [city]”
“picnic packs [city]”

3) **Niche + location**
“gluten free [city]”
“allergen friendly [city]”
“halal [city]” (if applicable)

You don’t need 200 keywords.
You need 10–20 phrases that match your real offering.

### The anti-spam rule
Never stuff keywords unnaturally.
Instead, weave them into clear descriptions:

Bad:
“Best Thai food Oslo Thai food near me Thai takeaway Oslo”
Good:
“Small-batch Thai-inspired pickup in Oslo — weekend trays and picnic packs.”

Clarity wins.

# Part 8A — Google Maps visibility “recipe” (Google Business Profile) — step by step

Local SEO can work even without Google Maps, but **Google Maps is often the highest‑intent discovery surface**.
If someone searches:
- “takeaway near me”
- “[cuisine] near me”
- “catering [city]”
Maps is where the decision happens.

This section is a practical, technical “recipe” for creating and optimizing a **Google Business Profile (GBP)** so your kitchen can be visible in Google Maps — **without exposing your private home address**.

> Important: GBP rules vary by country and Google policy changes. Home‑based food businesses can have constraints. Always follow your local regulations and Google’s guidance. If you are unsure, choose the privacy‑safe options below.

## Step 0 — Choose your location model (privacy first)
Home kitchens must be careful with location.

Pick one model:

### Model A — Service area + “pickup near area” (often best)
- You do **preorder** and **pickup window**
- You set a **service area** (city/region) instead of publishing a home address
- You communicate: “Pickup near [Area]. Exact address after confirmation.”

### Model B — Partner pickup point (very strong)
- You arrange pickup at a partner venue (gym, shop, community space)
- You can use that venue address (with permission)
- This often increases trust because the address is public and familiar

### Model C — Separate pickup location (if you have one)
- A rented kitchen, shared space, or fixed pickup point

If none of these are possible, you can skip GBP and rely on:
RiceMap24 page + offline QR + local findability copy.
But if GBP is feasible, it can be a major growth lever.


## Step 1 — Create/claim your Google Business Profile (technical)
1) Go to Google and search: **“Google Business Profile”**
2) Choose **Manage now**
3) Sign in with a Google account you will keep long-term
4) Enter your business name (match your RiceMap24 name)
5) Choose your primary category (see next step)

If a profile already exists:
- choose “claim this business” / “own this business” (wording varies)
- follow verification steps


## Step 2 — Categories (what you should choose)
Categories affect what searches you show up for.
Choose the most accurate **primary** category you can.

Depending on what Google offers in your region, common fits may include:
- “Takeout restaurant”
- “Caterer”
- “Food delivery”
- “Restaurant”
- “Meal delivery”

Then add 1–3 secondary categories that are also true.

**Rule:** don’t pick categories you can’t deliver on.  
Trust matters more than tricks.


## Step 3 — Address vs service area (the privacy decision)
In GBP you may be asked for an address.

### If you use Model A (service area)
- If the interface allows it: choose **service area** and hide the street address
- Set service area to your city/region
- In your description and updates, say: “Pickup near [Area]. Address after confirmation.”

### If you use Model B (partner pickup)
- Use the partner address (only with permission)
- Your pickup day/time becomes part of your offer clarity

### If you use Model C (separate location)
- Use that location like a normal business address

**Do not publish your private home address if you don’t want it public.**


## Step 4 — Hours: how to handle pickup windows
Home kitchens often have limited availability.

Best practice:
- Set hours that match your real pickup window (for example, Saturday 16–18)
- Or set limited hours and clarify in the description

You can also use:
- “by preorder” language in description
- weekly GBP posts (if available) to announce pickup window

The goal is clarity:
people need to know *when they can actually get food*.

## Step 7 — Link strategy (one home base)
Use one main destination:

or

Don’t split attention across many links.  
One link improves conversion.

# Part 11 — The findability system (weekly, monthly, quarterly)

## Weekly (15 minutes)
- Update one photo (or repost best seller)
- Post one clarity story: pickup window + “DM MENU”
- Ask for 1 review (only after happy order)

## Monthly (45 minutes)

- Review your descriptions: are they clear and aligned with what you sell?
- Review your top 10 keywords: are you matching them naturally?
- Review reviews: what words do customers use? Copy those phrases into your descriptions (naturally)

## Quarterly (2 hours)
- Decide your main product line (picnic packs / trays / office packs)
