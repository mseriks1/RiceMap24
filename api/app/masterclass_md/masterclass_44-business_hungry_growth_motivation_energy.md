## Big idea (general business first): hunger is a strategy, not a personality trait

When people say “I want to be hungry,” they usually mean:
- I want more progress
- I want more consistency
- I want to feel excited again
- I want to grow my business, not just survive week to week

In business terms, hunger is:
**a sustained desire to improve, learn, and build momentum.**

It is not:
- panic
- jealousy
- unhealthy pressure
- working all the time

True business hunger feels like:
- curiosity
- pride
- ownership
- calm ambition

It’s the energy that says:
“Let’s make next week better.”

And for home kitchens, that energy matters because:
the business is often built by one person.
If your energy disappears, the business becomes “on/off.”
Customers feel that.
Repeat disappears.

So this masterclass is about building hunger that lasts.

# Part 1 — The 3 kinds of hunger (choose the healthy one)

Not all hunger is equal.

## Hunger A — Fear hunger (unstable)
“I must succeed or I’m failing.”
This creates burnout.

## Hunger B — Comparison hunger (toxic)
“I need to be better than others.”
This creates anxiety.

## Hunger C — Craft hunger (healthy)
“I want to improve because I’m building something I’m proud of.”
This creates consistency and joy.

The goal is Hunger C.
Craft hunger.

# Part 2 — Why hunger fades (common reasons)

Motivation fades for predictable reasons:

1) **No visible progress**
You work, but you don’t see results.

2) **Too much chaos**
Mistakes, stress, messy systems.

3) **No clear next step**
You don’t know what to focus on.

4) **Too much isolation**
You feel alone in the journey.

5) **No celebration**
You forget the wins.

6) **Energy drain**
Sleep, health, family stress, finances.

If hunger fades, it doesn’t mean you’re weak.
It means the system needs a reset.

---

# Part 3 — The hunger engine (a business system)

You don’t “find motivation.”
You create it.

A hunger engine is made of 5 parts:

1) **Vision**
A clear reason why you’re doing this.

2) **Goals**
Small measurable targets.

3) **Feedback**
Data and reality signals.

4) **Improvement**
One change per week.

5) **Reward**
Celebration and pride.

If one part is missing, hunger fades.

---

# Part 4 — Vision: what are you building?

Vision doesn’t need to be dramatic.
It can be simple:

- “I want stable extra income.”
- “I want to become a respected local kitchen.”
- “I want to represent my culture with pride.”
- “I want to build a business my family can be proud of.”
- “I want to grow into a full-time income.”

Write your vision in one sentence.
That sentence becomes fuel.

### Vision template
“I’m building a home kitchen that ______ so that ______.”

Example:
“I’m building a home kitchen that gives me stable income, so I can invest and create freedom.”

# Part 5 — Goals: small targets that create momentum

Hunger grows when you win regularly.

Bad goals:
- “be successful”
- “get famous”

Good goals:
- “increase orders from 10 to 14 per week”
- “increase profit per hour by 10%”
- “get 10 new reviews”
- “sell 5 family packs”
- “do one partner placement”
- “run one 30-day pilot”

Small goals create momentum.
Momentum creates hunger.

# Part 6 — Feedback: measure what matters (avoid feelings-only evaluation)

If you measure nothing, everything becomes emotional.

Use a simple weekly dashboard:
- orders

- stress score

- one improvement

Feedback turns the business into a game you can improve.
Games create motivation.

# Part 7 — Improvement: one change per week (the compounding rule)

Hunger dies when you try to change everything.
So use the compounding rule:

**One improvement per week.**

- better pickup message
- better label system
- one new bundle offer
- one better photo
- one new partner placement
- one new review request routine

Small improvements build pride.
Pride fuels hunger.

# Part 8 — Reward: celebrate wins like a pro

Most small business owners don’t celebrate.
They just move on.

But celebration is fuel.
It teaches your brain:
“This is working.”

### Simple reward system
Every week:
- write 1 win
- share it with someone (friend/family)
- reward yourself with something small:
  a good coffee, a walk, a nice meal

This is not childish.
This is leadership.
Leaders manage energy.

# Part 15 — Hunger vs discipline (the secret that keeps hunger alive)

Here’s a truth most people learn late:

**Hunger is not enough.**
Hunger gives the spark.
Discipline gives the engine.

Discipline does not mean harshness.
It means:
- a schedule you can repeat
- small routines you keep even when you don’t feel like it
- standards you protect

Why this matters:
You will not feel hungry every week.
But if you keep the rhythm, hunger returns because results return.

### The 2% rule
On low-energy weeks, do the 2% version:
- one post instead of three
- one improvement instead of three
- one small review ask
- one 15-minute planning session

A business survives on consistency, not mood.

Your brain forgets wins quickly.
Your notebook keeps them alive.

Business hunger is built on energy.

# Part 24 — Anti-burnout rules (so hunger stays sustainable)

Burnout is the biggest hunger killer.
So use these rules:

1) Cap orders before you break  
2) Keep the menu short  
3) Protect your pickup window (don’t stretch it endlessly)  
4) Use templates (stop rewriting messages)  
5) Improve one thing per week  
6) Take one rest block after pickup day  
7) Don’t chase every idea

A calm business is easier to love.
A business you love is easier to grow.

# Part 28 — Daily micro-routines (the easiest way to stay hungry)

You don’t need a big routine.
You need tiny routines that keep the engine warm.

## 5-minute morning check (on business days)
- What is today’s one priority?
- What is today’s one “small win” I want?
- What is the next pickup day/time?

This keeps your mind oriented toward progress.

## 10-minute weekly planning ritual
Once per week (same day), write:
- next pickup window
- menu plan (short)
- max orders
- one improvement for the week
- one learning task (15–30 minutes)

This simple ritual prevents drifting.

## 2-minute end-of-day reflection (only on busy days)
- One thing I did well today
- One thing I will improve next time

That’s it.
This builds identity-based hunger.
