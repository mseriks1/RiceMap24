# Masterclass — Productization & Recurring Revenue (Weekly Plans, Subscriptions, B2B Repeat)

![Masterclass hero image](images/mc_recurring_revenue_hero_16x9.jpg)

**What you will learn (simple):**
- The general business truth: one-time sales create stress; recurring revenue creates calm
- How home kitchens can build predictable income **without becoming a restaurant**
- 3 proven recurring models you can use (weekly meal plan, office repeat packs, tray subscriptions/prepay)
- How to price and position subscriptions so they feel like a benefit (not “commitment pressure”)

## Big idea (positive and powerful)

One-time orders are exciting.
But they are unpredictable.

Unpredictable income creates:
- stress
- rushed prep
- weak planning
- inconsistent weeks
- “I don’t know what next month looks like”

Recurring revenue changes the game.

Recurring revenue means:
- you know how many meals you need to make
- you can plan ingredients smarter
- you can batch prep
- you can reduce waste
- you can relax

This is not only for big companies.
It’s for small local businesses too.

The key is productization:
**turning your kitchen into repeatable offers.**

Instead of “order anything anytime”, you offer:
- weekly plans
- recurring office lunch packs
- tray subscriptions
- prepay bundles

This masterclass gives you 3 models + a 14-day launch plan.

## The general business lesson (productization = stability)

Productization means turning your service into a product:
- clear package
- clear price
- clear schedule
- clear limits
- easy repeat ordering

In many industries, productization is how small businesses scale:
- gyms sell memberships
- salons sell packages
- coffee shops sell loyalty cards
- meal kit brands sell subscriptions

Home kitchens can do the same.

You’re not “locking customers in”.
You’re making it easier for them to buy again.

Convenience is value.

## The 3 recurring revenue models (choose one first)

You can do all three eventually.
But start with one model.

### Model 1 — Weekly Meal Plan (B2C subscription)
Customers subscribe to a weekly pickup:
- 2 meals
- 3 meals
- or 5 meals
They pick up weekly.
You plan weekly.

**Best for:** meal prep, busy families, lunch bowls, reheat meals, cold-ready.

### Model 2 — Office Repeat Packs (B2B recurring)
One office orders every week:
- team lunch packs
- trays for meetings
- recurring delivery/pickup

**Best for:** reliable kitchens with clear timing, trays, office bundles.

### Model 3 — Tray Subscription / Prepay Bundles (B2C or B2B)
Customers prepay for:
- 4 weekend trays over 6 weeks
- 8 dessert boxes over 3 months
- “10 lunch boxes prepaid”
You deliver/pickup on a schedule.

**Best for:** family trays, premium weekend treat drops, desserts, cultural event foods.

## How to choose the best model for you

Ask:
1) Which product do I repeat easily?
2) Which customer type do I already have?
3) Which model matches my weekly rhythm?

Quick mapping:
- If you have individual customers → Model 1
- If you have one strong office lead → Model 2
- If you sell trays/desserts → Model 3

Start with the model that feels most natural.

## The 14-day launch plan (pilot → recurring customers)

This plan is designed to be calm and realistic.

### Days 1–2: Choose one model and design the offer
- pick Model 1, 2, or 3
- define 2–3 tiers
- define pickup window and cutoff rules
- set max slots (scarcity protects quality)

### Days 3–4: Build the “offer page copy”
- one-line identity
- what’s included
- how it works
- pricing
- how to join
(Templates below.)

### Days 5–6: Recruit 5 pilot customers
- invite your best repeat customers first
- offer a small pilot benefit (priority slots)
- keep it limited (5–10 slots)

### Days 7–8: Run the first recurring week
- deliver the plan
- collect feedback
- note what’s confusing

### Days 9–10: Improve one thing
- simplify menu choices
- simplify pickup timing
- simplify messaging

### Days 11–12: Expand to 10–20 subscribers (or 2 offices)
- repeat what worked
- keep slots limited

### Days 13–14: Set your renewal rhythm
- weekly auto-renew (if possible) or
- monthly renewal messages
- track churn reasons and fix one thing

In 14 days, you can go from random orders to predictable base volume.

## The “predictability triangle” (why recurring works)

Recurring revenue is powerful because it creates predictability in three places:

1) **Customer behavior**
Customers stop thinking every week. They just follow the routine.

2) **Your production**
You stop guessing volume and can plan prep and shopping.

3) **Your finances**
You can forecast income, build buffer, and pay yourself more consistently.

When these three become predictable, your business feels calmer.
That’s the real goal.

---

## Productization rules (how to make subscriptions feel easy)

Recurring offers fail when they feel complicated or risky.
So use these rules:

### Rule 1 — One clear moment
Tie the offer to one clear moment:
- “weekly pickup”
- “Friday office lunch”
- “weekend family tray”

### Rule 2 — Clear limits
Limits are not negative.
Limits protect quality.

Example:
- “Only 15 subscriber slots”
- “Only 2 offices per week”

### Rule 3 — Simple choices
Too many menu choices creates admin stress.
Give:
- one main weekly menu
- optional add-ons

### Rule 4 — Predictable messaging
Use the same message flow every week.
Customers learn it fast.

### Rule 5 — Easy cancel/pause
If customers fear commitment, they won’t join.
Offer:
- pause week
- cancel with cutoff

Warm, fair, consistent.

## The “subscription objection” (and the best solution)

Some people dislike subscriptions.
So you give them a different option:

- weekly subscription (Model 1)
- prepaid punch card (Model 3)

This is why Model 3 is powerful.
Prepay feels like:
- commitment with control
- not endless billing

So you can offer both:
- Subscription for routine lovers
- Prepay for commitment shy customers

# MODEL 1 (B2C) — Weekly Meal Plan (Deep version)

## How to design the weekly meal plan (keep it simple)

A great weekly plan is built on:
- repeatable menu items
- clear portion sizes
- predictable pickup
- limited slots

### The best plan structure (for most kitchens)
- 2-meal plan (entry)
- 3-meal plan (core)
- 5-meal plan (premium)

If that feels too much, start with 2 tiers only:
- 3 meals
- 5 meals

### What counts as one “meal”?
Define it clearly:
- one portion + rice
or
- one bowl/box

This prevents confusion.

### Include add-ons, but keep them optional
Add-ons increase average order.
But the plan should feel complete without them.

---

## Weekly meal plan examples (realistic)

### Example 1 — Reheat meal plan (very stable)
- Meal = curry/adobo/stew portion + rice
- Reheats well
- Low complaint risk
- Great for busy weeks

### Example 2 — Cold-ready meal plan (fresh lunch)
- Meal = chilled bowl + dressing separate
- Great for office workers and meal prep
- Add rolls as optional add-on

### Example 3 — Mixed plan (two categories)
- 2 meals are reheat
- 1 meal is cold-ready
This feels premium and interesting, but still manageable.

---

## Meal plan menu choice (avoid admin overload)

Offer one of these systems:

### System A — Chef’s weekly menu (simplest)
- customers get the menu of the week
- no custom choice
- optional “spicy/no spicy” only

### System B — Two-choice menu
- choose A or B for the week
Still manageable.

### System C — “Swap one item” rule
- one swap allowed before cutoff
This keeps it fair.

Start with System A.
It is easiest.

---

## Pricing approach (subscription without heavy discounts)

Subscriptions don’t need big discounts.
They need value.

Value levers:
- priority slots
- predictable weekly pickup
- occasional subscriber bonus (small)
- convenience

### Subscriber bonus examples (small and safe)
- free sauce cup
- free dessert once per month
- early access to limited drops
- priority pickup lane (if you have it)

Keep it small.
Consistency is more valuable than discounts.

---

## Operations SOP (what you must do weekly)

To keep subscriptions calm, use this weekly flow:

1) **Monday:** announce weekly menu + confirm pickup time
2) **Wednesday:** reminder of cutoff
3) **Friday:** final confirmation for subscribers
4) **Pickup day:** deliver consistently
5) **After pickup:** renewal message (or auto-renew)

This flow becomes a habit for customers.
That’s how recurring becomes easy.
