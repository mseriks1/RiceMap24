## Why no-shows happen (general business, not just food)

Before we create policies, let’s be honest:
most no-shows are not evil.
They are normal human behavior.

Common reasons:
- they forgot
- they got stuck at work
- kids got sick
- traffic happened
- they changed their mind
- they assumed it “doesn’t matter”
- they feel awkward cancelling

So your system must do two things at once:
- reduce forgetting and confusion
- make “cancelling or rescheduling” easy and not embarrassing

A warm system doesn’t shame customers.
It guides them.

## The “Warm Policy System” (simple model)

Use this 5-part system:

1) **Cutoff time** (when ordering closes)  
2) **Pickup window** (clear time range)  
3) **Hold rule** (how long you hold the order)  
4) **Deposit/booking fee** (optional, for certain cases)  
5) **Waitlist** (a calm overflow system)  

When these five parts exist, you stop improvising every weekend.

---

## Step 1 — Choose your pickup format (make it easy to be on time)

Your pickup system should be:
- predictable
- simple
- repeatable

### The best pickup format (for most home kitchens)
- One pickup window per week (example: Sat 16:00–18:00)
- One pickup location (clear address)
- One reminder message on pickup day

Consistency reduces late arrivals.
Because customers learn your rhythm.

---

## Step 2 — Set the cutoff time (the “order closing” line)

Cutoff time is not strict.
It’s practical.

You need a cutoff because you must:
- shop
- prep
- cook
- pack

### Simple cutoff options
- Friday 20:00 for Saturday pickup
- Thursday 20:00 for Saturday pickup (if you need more time)
- 24 hours before pickup (simple rule)

### The cutoff mindset
Cutoff is not “no”.
Cutoff is:
“so we can keep quality high.”

That is warm and professional.

---

## Step 3 — Create a hold rule (the “late customer” rule)

This is the rule that saves your weekend.

A hold rule is simply:
“How long do we hold the order if someone is late?”

### A warm hold rule example
- We hold orders for **20 minutes** after the pickup window starts  
- If you’re late, just message us — we will find a simple solution  
- After the window ends, we may not be able to hold hot food in perfect condition  

This is fair and calm.

### Why a hold rule matters
Without a rule, you will:
- wait too long
- feel angry
- lose quality
- lose energy

With a rule, you feel:
- protected
- calm
- professional

---

## Step 4 — Decide if you need a deposit / booking fee (optional)

Deposits are not for everyone.
But they are very useful in certain situations.

### Deposits are good for:
- large orders
- office packs
- family trays
- custom requests
- new customers in high-demand weeks

### Deposits are not always needed for:
- small repeat customers
- low-demand weeks

### The warm deposit mindset
A deposit is not punishment.
A deposit is:
“we are reserving ingredients and time for you.”

That is normal in business.

### Deposit options (simple)
- small booking fee (fixed amount)
- percentage deposit (20–30%)
- full prepayment for large orders

Keep it simple.
Too many options create confusion.

---

## Step 5 — Build a waitlist (so “sold out” becomes calm)

Waitlists turn chaos into calm.
Because you stop negotiating and improvising.

A waitlist system says:
- “We are fully booked”
- “We can add you to the waitlist”
- “If a spot opens, we confirm by [time]”

### Why waitlists reduce no-shows
When customers feel slots are limited, they take the booking more seriously.
Also, you can replace cancellations quickly.

Short. Warm. Clear.

## Friendly enforcement (scripts for hard moments)

This is the part many kitchens struggle with.
Because you want to be kind.
But you also need boundaries.

The secret is:
**you can be warm and still be clear.**

### Script 1 — Customer asks to order after cutoff
> I’d love to help ❤️ We close orders at **[time]** so we can prep fresh and keep quality high.  
> I can add you to next pickup, or if we get a cancellation I can put you on the waitlist.

### Script 2 — Customer keeps arriving late
> Thank you for letting me know ❤️  
> Just a reminder: pickup window is **[time–time]**, and we hold orders for 20 minutes after the start.  
> If you think you’ll be late, message me early so we can plan it calmly.

### Script 3 — Customer wants you to “wait longer”
> I understand ❤️ I’ll do my best.  
> After the pickup window ends, it’s hard to keep food in perfect condition.  
> Can you give me your ETA so I can choose the best solution?

### Script 4 — No-show with payment already done
> Hi ❤️ I’m sorry we missed each other.  
> Because ingredients and time were reserved for your order, we normally don’t refund full amount for no-shows.  
> But I can offer **[credit for next pickup / partial refund / reschedule]** — what works best for you?

### Script 5 — No-show without payment (future prevention)
> No problem ❤️ For future orders, especially trays/large orders, we take a small booking fee to reserve ingredients and time.  
> It keeps everything fair and helps us stay consistent.

Warm, calm, clear.

## Refund / credit ladder (keep it fair and consistent)

This removes emotional decisions.

### Level 1 — Small late issue
- customer late but within window  
Fix: normal pickup, no penalty

### Level 2 — Late beyond hold rule
Fix options:
- partial credit (small)
- reschedule if food quality allows
- clear reminder for next time

### Level 3 — No-show
Fix options:
- offer credit (limited) instead of full refund
- or partial refund depending on situation
- require deposit next time for large orders

The goal is to be fair — and also protect your time.

## Why policies feel “strict” (and how to make them feel warm)

Many small business owners fear policies because they think:
“If I set rules, customers will leave.”

But in reality, most customers love rules when they are:
- clear
- fair
- explained with care

The “strict feeling” usually comes from two things:
1) **cold language**
2) **surprise enforcement**

So we fix both.

### Warm policy language
Warm language includes:
- the reason (“so we can keep quality high”)
- the human tone (“thank you for understanding”)
- the solution (“waitlist / next pickup / reschedule”)

### No surprise enforcement
If customers know the rule early, it feels normal.
If the rule appears only when something goes wrong, it feels harsh.

So the key is:
**state policies before the problem happens.**

That is professionalism.

## Deposit system (how to make it feel normal)

Deposits can feel scary to introduce.
So you introduce them gently.

### The warm deposit script (short)
“Because we reserve ingredients and time for trays/large orders, we take a small booking fee. It helps us keep everything fair and consistent.”

That’s it.
No drama.

### When to introduce deposit (best timing)
- after one or two no-shows
- when you start selling trays/office packs
- during high demand weeks

### Deposit options (simple and clean)

**Option A: Fixed booking fee**
- “Booking fee: 50–100 NOK” (or your currency)
Good for: small trays, new customers.

**Option B: Percentage deposit**
- “20% deposit for large orders”
Good for: big office orders.

**Option C: Full prepayment**
Good for: very large orders and custom requests.

Pick one.
Keep it consistent.

## Waitlist system (make it feel like a benefit)

Waitlists work when they feel like:
“you get access to a spot if something opens.”

### Waitlist rule (simple)
- You join waitlist
- If spot opens, you must confirm within X minutes
- If not, it goes to next person

This keeps it fair and avoids back-and-forth.

### Waitlist confirmation time (good options)
- “We confirm by 12:00 on pickup day”
- “We confirm 24 hours before pickup”

Choose one and repeat.

## How to handle the 5 most common real-life scenarios

This section makes the system feel usable.

> I understand ❤️

> But I can offer **[credit / partial refund / reschedule]** — what works best for you?

Warm response:
> Thank you for your support ❤️

> It helps us reserve ingredients and time safely.

Warm. Clear. Professional.

## Deposit implementation (how to introduce it without losing customers)

If you decide to add deposits, use this 3-step approach:

### Step 1 — Start only for large orders
Don’t apply to everyone.
Start with:
- trays
- office packs
- custom orders

### Step 2 — Explain the reason once
Use one sentence:
“We reserve ingredients and time.”

No long explanation.

### Step 3 — Keep it consistent
If you sometimes require deposit and sometimes not,
customers will argue.

So create a simple rule:
- “Trays and large orders require booking fee.”

That feels fair.

## FAQ (simple answers)

**Will policies scare customers away?**  
Most good customers like clarity. Price-shoppers may leave — and that’s okay.

**What if someone has a real emergency?**  
Be human. You can be flexible sometimes. The key is having a standard rule, then making exceptions intentionally.

**Should I charge deposits for everyone?**  
Usually no. Start with trays/large orders or high-demand weeks.

**What if I feel uncomfortable enforcing rules?**  
Use templates. Warm scripts help. And remember: clarity is kind.

**How do I avoid sounding strict?**  
Explain the “why”, offer a solution, keep messages short and warm.
