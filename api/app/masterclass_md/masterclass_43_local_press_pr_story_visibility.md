## Big idea (general business first): “earned media” is a trust shortcut

There are three ways to get attention in business:

1) **Paid media** (ads, boosts, sponsored posts)  
2) **Owned media** (your own social media, website, email list)  
3) **Earned media** (press coverage, interviews, local articles, community mentions)

Local newspaper coverage is earned media.
It gives you something ads struggle to buy:

**Borrowed trust.**

People think:
“If the newspaper wrote about them, they must be real.”

That’s why one good local article can outperform months of posting.
It also creates a “proof asset” you can reuse for years:
- share the article on social media
- put it on your RiceMap24 page (“Featured in…”)
- send it to venues and offices as credibility

But to get it, you must understand one thing:

Newspapers don’t want ads.  
They want stories.

## Why local newspapers say yes (what they actually want)

Editors are looking for:
- community interest
- human stories
- local uniqueness
- culture and food
- “new in town” stories
- entrepreneurship and small business
- seasonal angles (summer food, holidays, festivals)

A home kitchen has several strong angles:
- food + culture
- personal journey (moving countries, starting new life)
- community connection (bringing flavors to the neighborhood)
- entrepreneurship (side hustle → business)
- local integration (how long you’ve lived here, what you love about the place)

Your job is to package this as a *feature story*, not a commercial.

# Part 1 — Two routes to get press

## Route A — Tip the newspaper and offer an interview (best)
You pitch a journalist:
- why your story is interesting
- what the human angle is
- why it matters locally
- what visuals they can get

If they say yes, they interview you and write the article.

**Pros**
- more authentic
- higher credibility
- less “marketing vibe”

**Cons**
- you must be available
- you can’t control every line

## Route B — Write a “ready draft” article they can use (also works)
Some local outlets appreciate a well-written draft, especially if it’s:
- not ad-like
- well structured
- includes good photos
- includes a local angle

They may edit it heavily, but it gives them material.

**Pros**
- faster
- you control the structure
- easier for small outlets to publish

**Cons**
- must be written like journalism (not promo)
- less powerful than a journalist interview (sometimes)

A smart approach:
Try Route A first (tip + interview).  
If no response, send Route B as a helpful option.

# Part 2 — What makes a “non-ad” story (the editorial formula)

A great local feature has:

1) **A hook** (why this matters now)  
2) **A person** (human story, not just product)  
3) **A place** (local community connection)  
4) **A culture** (food as identity)  
5) **A small business angle** (entrepreneurship, motivation)  
6) **A taste moment** (a dish that makes it real)  
7) **A local invitation** (subtle, not salesy)

The invitation is usually one line at the end like:
“They take orders for weekend pickup through RiceMap24.”

That’s enough.

---

## The best “hooks” for a home kitchen story (choose one)

- “From [country] to [city]: bringing real home cooking to the neighborhood”
- “A new home kitchen is quietly building a following in [area]”
- “Cultural food as community building”
- “From homesickness to business: cooking as connection”
- “A side hustle turning into a local food movement”
- Seasonal hook: “Summer flavors / holiday food / festival season”

Pick one hook and build around it.

# Part 3 — What the article should include (your content checklist)

## 1) The opening (strong first paragraph)
The opening should:
- set the scene
- name the person
- hint at the local angle
- introduce the cuisine

Example style:
“A scent of garlic and ginger fills the small kitchen in [area]. After moving from [country], [name] has begun sharing the flavors of home with neighbors through a home kitchen concept.”

Short, vivid, human.

---

## 2) The person (the human angle)
This is where newspapers lean in.

Include:
- where you’re from (home country)
- what food means to you
- why you started cooking here
- how long you’ve lived in Norway/Sweden/etc.
- what surprised you about local life
- what you miss
- what you love about your new home

Keep it honest. Not dramatic. Just real.

---

## 3) The culture and the food (make it vivid)
Food is the “visual” part of the story.

Include:
- 1–2 signature dishes (with a short description)
- a family story or tradition connected to the dish
- why the cuisine is unique (spices, technique, comfort)

This is where the journalist can write something memorable.

---

## 4) The concept (RiceMap24 / home kitchen) — without sounding like an ad
Explain it simply:
- it’s a home kitchen
- orders are planned (pickup windows)
- limited batches for quality
- local community and convenience

Avoid heavy brand talk.
Keep it factual and calm.

---

## 5) The local community angle
This is your strongest “non-ad” section.

Include:
- why local people respond (taste, authenticity, warmth)
- repeat customers
- office lunches / events (if relevant)
- cultural exchange: “food as bridge”
- any local involvement (sports club, church, community)

Newspapers love “community glue”.

---

## 6) Practical info (one line, not sales)
End with a simple line:
“They take orders for weekend pickup through RiceMap24 and share weekly menus on [platform].”

That’s enough.
No prices. No “buy now”.

# Part 4 — Photo playbook (what editors want)

Editors love stories with good images.
A strong photo set increases your chance of being published.

Aim for 8–12 photos.
Here’s the recommended set:

1) Portrait of you in the kitchen (warm, natural light)  
2) Close-up of signature dish (clean plating, simple background)  
3) “Hands cooking” shot (stirring, seasoning, plating)  
4) Ingredients/spices shot (cultural detail)  
5) Packaging shot (clean, professional)  
6) “Kitchen vibe” wide shot (not messy)  
7) Family tradition element (optional: recipe book, family photo blurred)  
8) A “local” shot (outside: neighborhood, market, local landmark)  
9) Group tray / office pack (if you do it)  
10) A “pickup moment” (optional, if privacy allows)

**Photo rule:** make it feel like a documentary, not a commercial.

---

## Quick phone photo tips (simple)
- use natural light by a window
- avoid overhead yellow light
- clean background
- shoot close for food
- wipe edges on containers
- don’t over-edit (no heavy filters)

If you can do only one upgrade:
use a window and a clean surface.

# Part 5 — The press kit (what to prepare before you pitch)

A press kit makes you look professional and saves the journalist time.

Keep it simple:

### Press kit checklist
- 1 short bio (5–6 lines)
- 1 longer bio (12–15 lines)
- 6–10 photos (as above)
- list of 3 signature dishes + short descriptions
- your location area (city/region, not full address)
- availability (when you cook / pickup windows)
- one link to your kitchen page (RiceMap24)
- phone and email

That’s it.

# Part 7 — Interview prep (so the journalist gets great quotes)

Journalists love:
- short, vivid quotes
- feelings connected to food
- local contrast and learning

Prepare answers to these:

1) Why did you start cooking here?  
2) What dish represents “home” for you?  
3) What do locals react to most?  
4) What surprised you about food culture in Norway/Sweden?  
5) What do you hope people feel when they eat your food?  
6) What’s your weekly rhythm (pickup)?  
7) What’s next (without sounding like a business plan)?

Keep it human.

# Part 9 — “Strike while it’s hot” (7-day momentum plan)

A press feature is a spike of attention.
If you do nothing, it fades.

So here is the 7-day plan to convert attention into repeat orders.

## Day 0 (publication day)
- share the article on Facebook + Instagram stories
- keep caption simple: “Thank you for the feature ❤️”
- pin the post for 7 days
- update RiceMap24 page: “Featured in [Newspaper]” (one line)

## Day 1
- post one food photo + quote from the article
- invite people to follow for weekly menu (no hard selling)

## Day 2
- drop your weekly pickup schedule clearly (time window)
- “limited slots” only if true

## Day 3
- share behind-the-scenes cooking (short video)
- show cleanliness and care

## Day 4
- post a “best seller” dish with simple description
- add ordering clarity (how to order)

## Day 5
- ask existing customers to share the article (soft)
“Share if you want to support local food ❤️”

## Day 6
- open a small VIP list (optional)
“Early menu + first access to slots.”

## Day 7
- thank the community again and recap next pickup window

The goal is not to spam.
The goal is to create a *weekly habit* while attention is high.

1) **Pitch sounds like an ad**

2) **No photos**

# Part 13 — Angle selection (how to choose a story journalists actually want)

A journalist’s first question is not:
“Is this business good?”

It’s:
“Is this story interesting to our readers?”

So you need a strong angle. Here are the best angles for home kitchens:

## Angle A — Culture meets local community
- “Flavors from [country] are finding a home in [town].”
- Focus: food as bridge, local curiosity, cultural exchange.

## Angle B — Entrepreneurship and courage
- “From kitchen table to local following.”
- Focus: small business, resilience, side hustle.

## Angle C — The “new neighbor” story
- “New in town: a home kitchen that’s become a weekly ritual.”
- Focus: local identity, belonging.

## Angle D — Seasonal hook
- summer: “easy summer food / picnic trays”
- autumn: “comfort food season”
- holidays: “traditional dishes for celebrations”
- school season: “lunch packs for busy families”

## Angle E — Community demand
- “Sold out weekends” (only if true)
- Focus: why people are drawn to it.

### The rule
Pick one primary angle and one secondary angle.
Too many angles makes the story feel like a pitch.

# Part 14 — Outlet mapping (who to pitch and why)

Local press includes more than “the newspaper”.

Make a list of 10 targets:

1) local newspaper (print + online)
2) local online news site
3) community Facebook group admins (sometimes they share stories)
4) local radio station
5) local culture magazine
6) municipal newsletter / local bulletin
7) food bloggers / local reviewers (careful, choose quality)
8) immigrant community outlets
9) student newspapers (if nearby)
10) local “what’s happening” event pages

Pitch the top 5 first.
Then expand.

### Why this matters
If one editor is busy, another outlet might say yes quickly.

# Part 15 — Journalist psychology (how to make it easy to say yes)

Journalists have limited time.
They are attracted to stories that are:

- **clear** (one strong angle)
- **visual** (photos available)
- **local** (relevance to the community)
- **human** (quotes and personal voice)
- **timely** (season, new concept, community trend)

Your goal is to remove friction for them.

That’s why a press kit matters:
it turns “work” into “easy content”.

# Part 17 — What to avoid (ad energy and credibility killers)

Avoid phrases like:
- “best in town”
- “cheap and amazing”
- “order now”
- “limited offer, buy fast”

Avoid listing prices.
Avoid long CTA paragraphs.

Instead, use:
- facts (planned pickups, small batches)
- human quotes
- community context

One simple line at the end is enough:
“They take orders through RiceMap24.”

That’s it.

# Part 20 — The “after press” funnel (how to convert attention into regulars)

Press creates attention.
But attention becomes business only if you have a simple path.

A simple funnel for home kitchens:

1) **Article** → trust spike  
2) **RiceMap24 page** → clarity + ordering  
3) **First order** → great experience  
4) **Retention loop** → second order + VIP list  
5) **Reviews engine** → proof compounding

So when press hits, you want these ready:

### Before press goes live
- clear pickup day/time
- one best seller highlighted
- one bundle (“dinner solved”)
- simple ordering instructions
- max orders cap (protect quality)
- VIP list option (optional)

### After press goes live (7 days)
- share article
- pin it
- post one behind-the-scenes
- open 5–15 VIP spots
- invite repeat orders gently
- ask for reviews after great experiences

Press is the spark.
Systems make it a fire.

# Part 25 — Follow-up schedule (what to do if they don’t reply)

Editors are busy.
No reply often means “not now”, not “never”.

A professional follow-up schedule:

- **Follow-up #1:** 4–5 days after first pitch  
- **Follow-up #2:** 10–14 days after first pitch  
Then stop for a while and move to other outlets.

### Follow-up #1 template (short)
Hi [Name], just checking in 😊  
Happy to share photos and do a short interview if the story fits.  
No worries if it’s not a match right now.

### Follow-up #2 template (offer the ready draft)
Hi [Name], last quick note 😊  
If helpful, I can send a ready draft feature (not ad-like) with photos you can edit freely.  
Would that be useful?

Short. Respectful. No pressure.

If they offer a fact-check, great.
If not, be calm.
