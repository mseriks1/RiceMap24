## The “Batch Cooking Pyramid” (simple framework)

Think of batch cooking like a pyramid:

### Level 1 — Standardize
- same containers
- same portion size
- same menu structure
This reduces thinking and mistakes.

### Level 2 — Prep in batches
- chop vegetables together
- pre-mix sauces
- portion spices
This saves time during cooking.

### Level 3 — Cook in batches
- cook large batch of base
- cook protein in rounds
- keep quality consistent

### Level 4 — Pack in batches
- packaging station
- assembly line
- labels (optional)

### Level 5 — Repeat and improve
- note what was slow
- improve one step next time

This pyramid is the “home kitchen scaling system.”

## Step 1 — Choose a “batch-friendly” menu (most important)

Some dishes are easy to batch.
Some dishes are stressful to batch.

Batch-friendly dishes:
- curries
- braised dishes (adobo style)
- stir-fry (if organized)
- fried rice
- noodles (if you prep sauce/ingredients)
- dumplings/spring rolls (batch prep, then fry/steam)

Time-heavy dishes can still work, but you need more prep:
- pho (broth time)
- complex multi-step dishes

### The practical rule
If you want efficiency:
- focus on 1 signature dish + 1 banker dish
- add 2–4 sides
- use variations (protein/tofu) to increase options without chaos

This is the menu foundation of batch efficiency.

---

## Step 2 — Create a shopping system (save time every week)

Shopping is a hidden time cost.

A shopping system is:
- a reusable list
- standard quantities
- fewer store trips

### The “one list” method
Create one master list for:
- your signature dish
- your banker dish
- your top add-ons

Then each cooking week, you simply adjust quantities.

### The “2-category” shopping list
Make your list in two parts:

**A) Must-have essentials**
- protein
- key vegetables
- rice/noodles
- packaging

**B) Flavor essentials**
- sauces
- spices
- herbs
- oil

This makes shopping faster and prevents forgetting key items.

### Pro tip: keep backup basics at home
Keep a small stock of:
- soy sauce
- fish sauce
- vinegar
- sugar
- oil
- salt
- chili
- rice/noodles (if possible)

This reduces emergency store runs.

---

## Step 3 — Batch prep (the magic step)

Batch prep is where you win.

Batch prep means you do “similar tasks together”:
- chop all vegetables at once
- portion garlic/ginger at once
- pre-mix sauces at once

This reduces switching costs and mental stress.

### The 30–60 minute batch prep session
Before cooking day (or morning of cooking day), do:

1) Chop vegetables (for all dishes)  
2) Portion proteins (and marinate if needed)  
3) Mix sauces in a jar (signature and banker)  
4) Prepare rice/noodle base plan  
5) Set up packaging station

Now cooking feels easy.

---

## Step 4 — The packaging station (turn packing into an assembly line)

Packing becomes stressful when it is messy.

A packaging station is:
- one clean table area
- containers ready
- sauce cups ready
- bags ready

### Simple packaging station setup
- stack containers on left
- place rice/noodle base in the middle
- place main dish on right
- place sauce cups and napkins at the top

Now packing becomes fast.

### The “packing line” method
Pack in this order:
1) all boxes get rice/noodles
2) all boxes get main dish
3) all boxes get sauce cups
4) lids closed
5) bags + names

This is how restaurants pack fast.
You can do it too.

---

## Step 5 — Cooking order (reduce chaos)

Cooking gets chaotic when you jump between tasks.

Use a cooking order:

1) Start rice/noodle base early (it takes time)  
2) Cook the main sauce/base batch  
3) Cook proteins in rounds  
4) Combine and taste check  
5) Keep warm safely (if needed)  
6) Pack in assembly line

When you repeat the same order weekly, you become faster automatically.

---

## Step 6 — Reduce mistakes (mistakes cost money and stress)

Mistakes happen from:
- multitasking
- unclear portions
- messy station
- too many menu items

### Mistake prevention system
- use one ladle/scoop size for portions
- use the same containers
- label spicy/mild if needed
- keep names and orders in one list

Even a simple handwritten order list can prevent mistakes.

## A realistic weekly workflow (part-time friendly)

Here is a realistic weekly workflow you can use.

### Option A: Weekend cooking (most common)
**Thursday/Friday (30–45 min)**
- shop
- quick prep (chop/marinate)
- prepare packaging

**Cooking day (2–5 hours depending on volume)**
- cook in batches
- pack assembly line
- pickup window
- thank you messages

### Option B: Two cooking days (advanced)
**Day 1:** signature dish  
**Day 2:** banker dish + sides

This works when you have demand.

### The rule
Start with one cooking day per week.
Add a second day only when your system feels stable.

## The “portion control” system (fast and professional)

Portion control is not about being cheap.
Portion control is about being consistent.

Consistency creates trust.
Trust creates repeat orders.

### Easy portion control methods
Choose one method:

**Method A: One scoop/ladle rule**
- Use the same ladle for sauce/protein
- Use the same rice scoop for rice
Repeat every portion.

**Method B: Weigh once, then eyeball**
- Weigh one “perfect portion” one time
- Then use it as your visual reference
After that you can eyeball with confidence.

**Method C: Container size decides portion**
- Use one container size for one portion
- Fill it to the same level each time

Simple portion rules reduce complaints and save time.

## How to handle peak demand (sell out without chaos)

Selling out is good.
But it can create chaos if you accept unlimited orders.

A simple rule:
- Decide a max number of portions you can handle (example: 20)
- Stop accepting new orders when you hit the max
- Offer waitlist for next week

This protects quality and keeps you calm.

Waitlist message:
“Hi 😊 Today we are fully booked.  
Want me to reserve for next pickup? DM ORDER ❤️”
