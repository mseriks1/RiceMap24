## Big idea (positive and simple)

A good menu is not “many dishes”.  
A good menu is **clarity**.

When customers feel confused, they don’t order.  
When customers feel clear, they order fast.

Your goal is to make ordering feel like:
> “Oh, I know what to choose. This is easy.”

If you do menu strategy the right way, **everything becomes possible**:
- more orders with less back-and-forth
- higher profit with add-ons and bundles
- more repeat customers because they remember you
- less stress for you because your cooking becomes simpler

---

## Why menu strategy matters for home kitchens

Restaurants can have long menus because they have staff and systems.  
Home kitchens are different:

- you cook in small batches
- you have limited time
- you want fewer mistakes
- you want consistency and quality

So your best advantage is:
- **focus**
- **a signature dish**
- **simple options**

Customers love simple choices.

---

## The RiceMap24 menu goal

A strong home kitchen menu does 4 jobs:

1) **Makes people hungry** (photo + clear dish name)  
2) **Builds trust** (clean, consistent, looks professional)  
3) **Makes ordering easy** (few clear options + DM “ORDER”)  
4) **Increases profit** (add-ons, bundles, smart portioning)

If your menu does these 4 jobs, your kitchen will grow.

---

## The simple framework: Menu in 3 layers

Think of your menu like this:

### Layer 1 — Hero Dish (your “main star”)
- 1 dish you promote most of the time
- the dish people remember
- the dish that makes your kitchen famous

### Layer 2 — Support dishes (optional)
- 1–2 extra dishes that fit your hero dish
- helps people choose if they don’t like the hero dish
- still simple

### Layer 3 — Add-ons (profit and convenience)
- 1–3 add-ons that make the meal complete
- easy “yes” items
- high margin, low effort

**That’s it.**  
You don’t need 20 dishes.

---

## Signature + Bankers (global strategy, works anywhere)

A strong menu is not “only one dish” and it’s not “20 dishes”.

The best growth structure for home kitchens in any country (Scandinavia, EU, USA, Asia, tourists anywhere) is:

- **1 Signature / Hero dish** (your identity, your story, your best craft)  
- **1–2 Bankers / Best sellers** (easy-to-understand dishes that convert new customers)  
- **2–4 Side orders** (small “yes” items that increase order size)  
- Optional: **1 vegetarian option** and **1 mild/kid-friendly option** (often just a variation)

**Signature = differentiation.** It makes you stand out from typical take-away.  
**Bankers = conversion.** They make it easy for new customers to say “yes” the first time.

Then, once customers trust you, they are much more open to your signature dish and special items.

## Step 1: Choose your Hero Dish (the most important decision)

Your hero dish should be:
- delicious and consistent
- something you can cook well repeatedly
- something customers love to reorder
- something that looks good on camera

### Good hero dish examples
**Filipino:** Adobo, Kare-kare, Sisig, Lumpia (as a hero item)  
**Thai:** Pad Thai, Green Curry, Basil Chicken, Massaman  
**Vietnamese:** Pho, Bun bowls, Spring rolls + main  
**Chinese:** Dumplings, Fried rice, Noodle bowls, Mapo tofu

### Your hero dish test (quick)
Answer yes/no:

1) Can I cook this dish consistently without stress?  
2) Can I make it in a bigger batch without losing quality?  
3) Does it look great in photos/videos?  
4) Would I be happy selling this every week?

If most answers are yes, it’s a great hero dish.

---

## Step 2: Decide your “menu style” (part-time friendly)

Choose one menu style that matches your life.

### Style A — One hero dish all the time (simplest)
- same hero dish every week
- customers learn it
- easiest to manage

Great for: busy families, beginners, stable repeat orders.

### Style B — Hero dish + one rotating “guest dish”
- hero dish is stable
- guest dish changes sometimes (once per month or every 2–4 weeks)

Great for: variety without confusion.

### Style C — Weekend menu (only certain days)
- you cook only Fri/Sat/Sun
- menu is the same each weekend

Great for: consistent routine and less messaging.

Pick one. Simplicity wins.

---

## Step 3: Build your add-ons (your profit engine)

Add-ons are powerful because they are “small yes” purchases.

Customers think:
> “I’m already ordering. Why not add rice or rolls?”

### Best add-ons for home kitchens
- extra rice (very profitable)
- sauce cup (very profitable)
- spring rolls / lumpia
- dessert (simple)
- drink (optional)
- extra meat (higher price)

### Add-on rules (keep it simple)
1) Offer **1–3 add-ons only**  
2) Add-ons should be easy to prepare  
3) Show add-ons sometimes in photos  
4) Write add-ons as simple lines (no long descriptions)

### Easy add-on pricing (simple)
- small add-on: +10 to +20
- medium add-on: +25 to +45
- big add-on: +50+

Keep prices round and friendly.

---

## Smart variations (variety without chaos)

Variety is powerful — but it must be **controlled** so you don’t create stress.

A smart way to offer variety is to keep the same base dish and vary the protein:
- chicken / pork / beef / shrimp
- tofu (vegetarian)

This helps you:
- reach more customers
- create more marketing angles
- keep prep simple (one sauce, one base, many options)

You can also do:
- **mild version** of a banker dish (kid-friendly)
- **vegetarian version** (to reach more buyers)

The key idea is: **one base, multiple options**.

---

## Step 4: Create one bundle (optional, but great)

Bundles increase profit and reduce stress:
- fewer messages per portion
- fewer customers to manage
- bigger orders

### Simple bundle types
**Bundle A:** “2-portion pack”  
**Bundle B:** “4-portion family pack”  
**Bundle C:** “Main + side combo”

### Bundle copy (simple)
- “Family pack available ❤️”
- “Order together (2–4 portions) ✅”
- “Perfect for family & friends”

Bundles can have small discount or no discount.
Many customers buy bundles because it’s easy.

## How to rotate dishes without confusing customers

Many kitchens want variety. That’s normal.

The problem is when the menu changes too often.
Customers think:
- “I don’t know what they sell”
- “I’ll wait”

So rotate like a professional restaurant:

### Rotation rule (simple)
- Keep hero dish stable
- Rotate guest dish monthly
- Rotate add-on occasionally

### Example rotation
Month 1:
- Hero: Pad Thai
- Guest: Green curry (optional)
- Add-on: spring roll

Month 2:
- Hero: Pad Thai
- Guest: Basil chicken
- Add-on: mango sticky rice (optional)

Customers still remember you for Pad Thai, but you keep it interesting.

- holiday trays
- party packs
- office lunch packs

Do this without changing your whole menu:

**Use a campaign add-on:**
- “Party tray add-on”
- “Office pack add-on”

So the core menu stays stable, but you create excitement.

**Fix:** take one portion photo and keep it.

### Mistake 5: Customers ask too many questions
**Fix:** make menu clearer + saved reply templates.

### Mistake 6: Cooking becomes stressful
**Fix:** simplify menu; prep in batches; focus.

## The deeper strategy: Why “less choice” often sells more

Many sellers believe:
“If I have 20 dishes, I’ll get more customers.”

But in real life, too many choices often cause:
- confusion
- hesitation
- no action

This is called “choice overload”. It happens everywhere:

### Signature is your strongest differentiator (stand out from take-away)
A signature dish is your “flag”. It tells the world:
- this kitchen has a real tradition
- this is not generic fast food
- this is special and worth trying

Bankers help new customers start. Signature makes them remember you.

- supermarkets
- streaming apps
- restaurant menus
- online shopping

For a home kitchen, a simple menu is a competitive advantage.

### What customers love
Customers love:
- one clear “popular dish”
- a clear price
- easy ordering
- consistent quality

That is why the hero dish strategy is so powerful.
It turns you from “a random home kitchen” into:
> “The kitchen known for *this dish*.”

## How to pick the right hero dish (advanced but easy)

If you have 3 possible hero dishes, choose the one that scores highest in these 5 areas:

1) **Batch-friendly**  
Can you cook 10–30 portions efficiently?

2) **Photo-friendly**  
Does it look amazing in close-up? (Sauce, shine, texture, steam)

3) **Repeat-friendly**  
Would customers reorder it often?

4) **Ingredient stability**  
Are ingredients easy to get? (Not too seasonal or too expensive)

5) **Your personal strength**  
Is this your “best dish” that you cook with confidence?

Even if another dish is “more unique,” your hero dish should be your strongest and simplest.

A signature dish does three magic things:

### 1) It becomes your marketing shortcut

### 2) It becomes your trust shortcut

This is why many restaurants are famous for one item:

You can do the same as a home kitchen.

## Add-ons: the smartest “quiet profit” system

Add-ons are one of the most powerful business tools because they:
- increase order size

### Add-on selection strategy (simple)
Pick add-ons that are:
- easy to prep
- cheap to produce
- loved by many

Examples:
- rice

Your add-ons don’t need to be complicated.

## Portion clarity: how to reduce price questions

If you hear:
- “Is the portion big?”
- “What do I get for this price?”

Your solution is visual, not words.

### The portion photo rule
Take one photo that shows:
- food in the box
- lid open
- top-down view
- clear size

Then reuse that photo often.
This reduces questions and increases trust.

### The “portion + packing” combo
Post once per week (or every two weeks):
- one portion photo
- one packing photo (boxes lined up)

These two photos can increase conversion more than fancy content.
