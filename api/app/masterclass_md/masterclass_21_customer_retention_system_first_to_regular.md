## Big idea (positive and powerful)

Getting customers is good.
Keeping customers is better.

Most small food businesses burn out because they live in “acquisition mode”:
- always chasing new people
- always posting
- always discounting

Retention flips the game.

Retention means:
- fewer new customers needed
- more predictable orders
- higher average order value over time
- stronger word-of-mouth
- less stress

This masterclass is not about “begging customers to come back”.
It’s about building a simple system that makes returning feel:
- easy
- natural
- rewarding
- part of a weekly rhythm

---

## The general business lesson (why retention beats marketing)

In general business, the easiest sale is the second sale.

Why?
Because the customer already crossed the hardest barrier:
**trust.**

The first order is mostly about fear reduction:
- “Will it be good?”
- “Will it be clean?”
- “Will it be worth it?”
- “Will pickup be smooth?”

Once the customer has a good first experience, the game changes.
Now you are not selling food.
You are selling a habit.

A repeat customer doesn’t need convincing.
They need:
- a reminder
- a clear offer
- good timing
- a smooth process

So we build a retention system that turns trust into habit.

## The 4-step retention loop (the core system)

This is the system you will repeat forever.

1) **Welcome** (first-time experience + warm follow-up)  
2) **Repeat trigger** (give a reason to order again soon)  
3) **VIP habit** (make them feel “in the circle”)  
4) **Reactivation** (bring back inactive customers calmly)

If you do only these four steps consistently, retention improves.

---

## Step 1 — Welcome (first-time buyer system)

The first-time buyer is fragile.
They are still deciding if you are “real”.

Your job is:
- make pickup smooth
- make packaging clean
- make them feel appreciated
- reduce uncertainty for next time

### The first-time welcome message (simple)

Send it:
- after pickup day
- or next morning

Keep it short and warm.

**Template (copy/paste):**
> Hi ❤️ Thank you for trying us!  
> I hope you enjoyed the meal. If you ever have feedback, I’d love to hear it.  
> Next pickup is [day/time] if you want a slot.

Why this works:
- appreciation
- feedback invitation
- next pickup reminder (soft)

### The “first-time bonus” (optional, small)
Do not discount heavily.
But you can offer a small “thank you”:
- free sauce cup next time
- priority slot
- small add-on for early repeat

Keep it small. Keep it sustainable.

---

## Step 2 — Repeat trigger (make the second order easy)

The second order is where habits begin.

Most kitchens lose customers because they never create a trigger.
They just hope people remember.

A trigger can be:
- “Next pickup is Saturday”
- “Family tray this week”
- “Limited slots”
- “New menu drop”
- “Payday week”
- “Weekend comfort food”

Your job is to give one clear reason each week to return.

### The best repeat trigger (simple)
**One weekly offer + one weekly pickup window.**

People love routines.

---

## Step 3 — VIP habit (build a small inner circle)

VIP is not about being fancy.
VIP is about belonging.

A VIP list is:
- a list of people who want to hear from you first
- a small group that gets early menu and early slots

VIP works because:
- it rewards loyalty
- it creates predictable demand
- it makes customers feel valued

### How to invite without being salesy
Invite only after:
- second or third order
- or when they send compliments

**VIP invite template:**
> Hi ❤️ You’ve ordered a couple times now — thank you!  
> Want to be on the VIP list? I send the weekly menu early and VIPs get first access to slots. No spam.

This feels respectful.

---

## Step 4 — Reactivation (bring back “sleeping customers”)

Some customers disappear for normal reasons:
- travel
- busy life
- diet changes
- budget cycles
- they forgot

Reactivation is not “pressure”.
It’s a warm reminder.

The key is to:
- segment customers
- send relevant messages
- keep it short
- offer a simple reason to return

---

## The segment list (who you message and how)

Segmentation is what prevents spam.

Here are the segments that matter:

### Segment A — First-time customers (within 7 days)
Goal: second order.

### Segment B — Recent repeats (2–4 orders, last 30 days)
Goal: VIP invite + routine.

### Segment C — VIP regulars (ordered in last 14 days)
Goal: early menu + priority slots.

### Segment D — Sleeping customers (no order in 30–60 days)
Goal: warm reactivation.

### Segment E — Past heavy buyers (big orders, trays, office)
Goal: special “we miss you” + easy bundle.

### Segment F — “Engaged but not buying” (likes/comments/DMs)
Goal: remove friction and clarify ordering.

If you send the same message to all segments, you look spammy.
If you send the right message to the right segment, you look professional.

## Payday and weekend rhythms (timing triggers)

People often reorder on rhythms.

Common rhythms:
- payday weeks
- weekends (comfort food)
- Sunday meal prep
- office lunch days

You don’t need to guess perfectly.
You just need to test.

### Simple rhythm strategy
- Weekly menu drop on Monday or Tuesday
- Reminder on Thursday (before cutoff)
- Pickup day updates on Saturday
- Reactivation message on payday week (once per month)

This rhythm creates habit.

Then you repeat with small variations.

## The weekly retention routine (10 minutes)

Retention works best when it becomes a weekly habit.

Every week, do this:
1) identify 5 first-time customers → send welcome
2) identify 5 repeat customers → VIP invite
3) identify 5 sleeping customers → reactivation message
4) send VIP early menu once per week
5) track what worked (one note)

That’s 15 messages per week.
Small effort, big results.

Think of your customer journey in four stages:

1) **Try** (first order)  
2) **Trust** (second order)  
3) **Routine** (regular habit)  
4) **Dormant** (inactive)

So your first goal is:
**increase second orders.**

Second orders create habit potential.

## The “second order barrier” (why people don’t come back)

Even if the food was great, customers may not reorder because:
- they don’t remember you
- they don’t know when you’re available
- they assume you’re not consistent
- they don’t want to bother you by messaging
- they feel uncertain about ordering steps
- they wanted something different that week

Retention fixes this by:
- creating a weekly rhythm
- reducing friction
- reminding them gently
- offering clear bundles

## The “friction killers” (small changes with big retention impact)

Retention improves fast when these are true:

- ordering method is simple (DM format, link, etc.)
- pickup window is consistent
- menu is not overwhelming
- packaging is clean and reliable
- your communication tone is warm and calm

If retention is low, don’t assume it’s “no demand”.
Often it’s friction.

## VIP system (deep version, without spam)

VIP should feel like a benefit, not a mailing list.

### VIP rules (keep it clean)
- VIP gets the menu early (once per week)
- VIP gets first access to limited slots
- VIP messages are short
- VIP can opt out easily

### How to build VIP without awkwardness
Invite customers when:
- they order the second time
- they compliment you
- they ask about “next week”

### VIP onboarding message
> Welcome to VIP ❤️  
> I’ll send the weekly menu early, and you can reserve a slot first.  
> If you ever want to leave the list, just tell me — no worries.

This prevents spam feelings because it’s respectful.

## The retention triggers (what makes people reorder)

Customers reorder for reasons, not logic.
Here are the strongest triggers:

1) **Convenience trigger**
“Dinner solved.”
Bundles are powerful here.

2) **Consistency trigger**
“Same pickup every week.”
Routine builds habit.

3) **Scarcity trigger**
“Limited slots.”
Works only if true.

4) **Identity trigger**
“Taste of home.”
Cultural food + story creates loyalty.

5) **Reward trigger**
Small gratitude (not heavy discounts).
A tiny bonus works.

6) **Timing trigger**
Payday week, weekend comfort, meal prep Sunday.

You don’t need all triggers.
Use one main trigger per week.

## Reactivation without spam (rules)

Reactivation is where many businesses become annoying.
So follow these rules:

- message only sleeping customers (30–60+ days)
- keep it 2–3 lines
- no guilt
- offer one clear reason to return
- stop after 2–3 attempts if they ignore
- always allow opt-out

Warm and respectful wins.
