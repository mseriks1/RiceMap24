# Part 2 — Shorts vs Long‑Form (what each is best for)

## Shorts (10–30 seconds)
- fast reach
- discovery
- “I want that” cravings
- easy to film
- consistent posting rhythm

Shorts are your weekly local visibility engine.

## Long‑form (5–15 minutes)
- deep trust
- story
- education

Long-form is your credibility engine.

### The best strategy

# Part 3 — The “YouTube funnel” for home kitchens

A realistic YouTube funnel looks like this:

1) **Shorts discovery**
Someone sees a bowl reveal or packing proof.

2) **Profile visit**
They click your channel and watch another short.

3) **Long‑form trust**
They watch one longer video:
“How my home kitchen works” or “Pickup day behind the scenes.”

4) **Action**
They order, follow, or join your VIP list.

5) **Community**
They comment, share, and become a fan.

YouTube is a funnel because it is a library.
You can guide people from short to long.

# Part 4 — “Document, don’t perform” (the easiest creator mindset)

The biggest reason people fail on YouTube is thinking:
“I need to be a YouTuber.”

No.
You need to be a kitchen owner who documents reality.

Documenting is easier because:
- you already have daily activities
- you don’t need scripts for everything
- people love authenticity

A simple document style:
- “Here’s what I’m cooking this week”
- “Here’s how pickup day works”
- “Here’s how I test new dishes”
- “Here’s what I learned about pricing”

This builds trust and also creates content for other RiceMap24 kitchens.

# Part 10 — Simple production workflow (no burnout)

YouTube becomes heavy if you overproduce.
So use a simple workflow:

### Filming (30 minutes per week)
- film short clips while you cook
- film packing line on pickup day
- film one “talking clip” (60–90 sec) about a lesson

### Editing (45–60 minutes per week)
- cut into 3 shorts
- assemble 1 long video using the same clips
- add simple text overlays

### Publishing (10 minutes)
- upload
- add title and description
- pin a comment with pickup window

That’s enough.
