## Big idea (positive and powerful)

Online marketing is great.
But the fastest local growth often happens **offline**.

Because offline gives you something social media cannot guarantee:
**stable local distribution**.

A flyer in the right place is like a small shelf in your city.
It quietly works for you every day.

If you build a simple offline system:
- you become visible to people who are already nearby
- you reach people who are not actively searching online
- you feel “real” and trusted (especially for food)
- you can grow even when your posting is inconsistent

This masterclass is not about spamming the city.
It is about being **strategic, polite, and useful**.

The goal is simple:

**Place a few clean QR posters in the right locations, with the right message, and repeat what works.**

---

## Why this works (simple psychology)

Offline placements work because of three human behaviors:

1) **Convenience**  
People buy what feels close and easy. A QR poster in a gym or shop says: “This is nearby. This is simple.”

2) **Trust by association**  
If a respected place allows your poster, customers think: “This kitchen is probably legit.”

3) **Repeated exposure**  
A person may not order today. But if they see you three times in two weeks, you become familiar. Familiar feels safe.

4) **Micro-moments**  
People have tiny windows where they are open to ordering:
- after the gym (hungry + planning meals)
- while waiting at a salon (time + boredom = attention)
- during lunch breaks at work (food decisions)
- while shopping (food mindset)

Your offline strategy places you inside those moments.

So your system should focus on:
- trusted locations
- repeated exposure
- a simple action (scan QR)
- a clear “why now” (pickup window, limited slots, weekly rhythm)

## The Offline Growth System (simple model)

Use this 5-part model:

1) **Place** — where you show up (high-fit locations)  
2) **Poster** — what you show (one clear message)  
3) **Pitch** — how you get “yes” (polite and easy)  
4) **Proof** — why people trust (reviews, photos, clarity)  
5) **Process** — track results and improve (small iterations)

If you build these five pieces, you can scale offline growth without stress.

## Step 1 — Choose locations (where to hang up, and why)

Not every wall is equal.
The best placements are places where people:
- are already thinking about food
- have time to notice
- are likely to be repeat customers
- live or work nearby

### The best location types (ranked)

**Tier 1 — High-fit “repeat customer” places**  
These places create repeated exposure and stable demand:
- Gyms and fitness studios (people think about meals)
- Hair salons and barbers (people wait, they look around)
- Local grocery stores / ethnic shops (food mindset)
- Cafés that allow community boards
- Community centers and local libraries (notice boards)
- Churches/faith communities and local groups (strong networks)
- Coworking spaces (lunch demand)

**Tier 2 — Convenience spots (good volume)**  
- Laundromats
- Small kiosks
- Local markets
- Student housing boards / dorms (check rules first)

**Tier 3 — High traffic but low attention (be careful)**  
- Random street poles
- Busy downtown walls  
These often feel spammy and get removed quickly.

### A good location is “quietly powerful”
The best placements are not the loudest.
They are the places where people **return weekly**.

### The “10-minute rule”
A placement is much stronger if:
- it is within 10–15 minutes travel for most customers
- it is on a repeated route (gym, shop, school pickup)

### The “fit score” (fast decision tool)
Give each location a score from 1–5:

- **Repeat visits** (do people come weekly?)
- **Attention time** (do people wait or look around?)
- **Food mindset** (are they thinking about meals?)
- **Trust level** (is the business respected?)
- **Local match** (near your pickup/delivery area?)

If total score is **18+**, it’s a top target.

### Your first 10 placements (simple list)
Start with a list of 10:
- 3 gyms/fitness studios
- 2 salons/barbers
- 2 grocery/ethnic shops
- 2 community boards (library/community center/café)
- 1 local group/community (church/community hall/coworking)

This gives you a balanced system.

## Step 2 — Your mini pitch (how to get “yes”)

Most owners are not against flyers.
They are against:
- messy posters
- too many posters
- spam energy
- complaints from customers

So your pitch must feel:
- polite
- simple
- low-risk
- respectful of their space

### The one-sentence pitch (works almost everywhere)
“Hi — I run a small local home kitchen and I’m trying to reach nearby customers. Would it be okay to place one clean QR poster on your community board? I can remove it anytime, and it’s designed to look nice.”

### What to bring (makes you look professional)
- 2–3 printed posters (clean, good paper)
- small tape or magnets (depending on board rules)
- a pen (some boards want you to write the date)
- a friendly smile and quick exit

### The “no pressure” promise
Owners relax when you say:
- “Only one poster”
- “I’ll keep it clean”
- “I can remove it anytime”
- “If it bothers anyone, just tell me”

### Where to ask (timing matters)
Avoid asking when they are busy.
Best times:
- mid-morning
- early afternoon
- calm weekdays

### If they say “we don’t allow flyers”
Respond warmly:
“No problem at all — I understand. Is there any place nearby that does allow a community board?”

This keeps it positive and sometimes they still help you.

### Style B — “Value to your members”

Pick one and repeat it. Consistency makes you calm.

## Objection handling (friendly and respectful)

Owners may say no for many reasons. Here are the best warm answers.

### Objection 1: “We don’t want advertising”
> Totally understand. I’m not trying to advertise loudly — just one clean community-style poster. If it doesn’t fit, no problem at all.

### Objection 2: “It becomes messy”
> That makes sense. I can place one small poster, and I’ll come back to check it weekly. If it ever looks messy, I’ll remove it immediately.

### Objection 3: “We only allow approved posters”
> Of course. What’s your process? I’m happy to follow your rules.

### Objection 4: “Do you have a license / is it safe?”
> Yes, food safety matters. We focus on cleanliness and consistency, and we can share details on request. If it’s not a fit for your space, I fully understand.

### Objection 5: “We charge for placement”
> I understand. Could we do a 30-day pilot first to see if it provides value to your customers? Then we can choose a fair structure.

The goal is not to win every conversation.
The goal is to stay polite and keep doors open.

## Step 3 — Poster design that feels premium (not spam)

A good poster does two jobs:
1) It makes your food feel trustworthy.
2) It makes scanning feel easy.

### The 6 design rules (simple)

1) **One main photo**
Food photos should be clean, bright, and simple.
One strong hero dish is better than five small photos.

2) **One clear message**
A poster is not a website.
One line is enough:
- cuisine + vibe
- or “weekly pickup”
- or “family trays”

3) **Big QR code**
The QR must be easy to scan.
If people struggle, they won’t try again.

4) **Space, not clutter**
White space looks premium.
Clutter looks desperate.

5) **A tiny backup**
Add a small handle or contact option (tiny).
QR is the main action.

6) **Match the location**
Gym poster should be clean and minimal.
Community board can have slightly more info.
Salon should look aesthetic.

### Paper and size (simple recommendation)
- A4 is usually perfect for boards.
- Use good print quality and decent paper.
- Matte paper often looks more premium than glossy.

## Step 4 — Choose your 2–3 poster types (keep it simple)

You do not need 10 poster designs.
You need 2–3 that match different customer needs.

### Poster Type 1 — QR Intro (“What is this?”)
**Goal:** introduce you quickly and build trust.  
**Message structure:**
- One strong food photo
- One line: cuisine + vibe
- Big QR
- Tiny backup contact

**Best locations:**
- community boards
- gyms
- cafés

### Poster Type 2 — Menu / Best sellers (“Help me decide”)
**Goal:** help people decide fast.  
**Message structure:**
- 3 best sellers
- simple ordering window
- big QR

**Best locations:**
- coworking spaces
- offices (if allowed)
- gyms and studios

### Poster Type 3 — Family pack / tray (“Weekend solution”)
**Goal:** increase average order and weekend demand.  
**Message structure:**
- tray/family photo
- “family pack” or “party trays”
- big QR

**Best locations:**
- salons/barbers
- grocery/ethnic shops
- communities and groups

### Optional Poster Type 4 — New customer offer (use gently)
Discounts are not always needed.
If you use an offer, keep it calm and limited:
- “Free add-on for first-time customers (limited slots)”
- “Try us this week (limited)”

Avoid big discount language. You want trust, not bargain hunters.

## Step 5 — Make the QR experience frictionless (scan → confidence)

A QR scan should feel like a reward: “Oh nice, this is easy.”

When someone scans, they should see:
- a clean hero image
- who you are (one sentence)
- best sellers / menu
- how ordering works (simple)
- pickup/delivery details
- trust signals (reviews, photos, clarity)

### The “10-second test”
If a new customer cannot understand what to do in 10 seconds, they leave.

### The “first-time customer path”
Make first-time ordering feel safe:
- clear ordering window
- clear pickup time
- clear payment expectation
- “what happens next?” (confirmation)

The calmer the system feels, the more people order.

## Step 6 — Tracking what works (simple QR variants)

You do not need complicated analytics.
You just need to know: **Which placements bring scans and orders.**

### The simplest tracking method: QR variants
Create 10 QR versions (or start with 5) that all lead to the same menu page, but with a tiny label.

Examples:
- QR-GYM-01
- QR-GYM-02
- QR-SALON-01
- QR-SHOP-01
- QR-LIBRARY-01
- QR-CAFE-01
- QR-COWORK-01
- QR-COMMUNITY-01

Even if you track manually, this helps you learn.

### Simple tracking table (copy this into a note)
Track weekly:

- Location
- Poster type (Intro / Menu / Family)
- QR variant
- Date placed
- Scans (if you can)
- Orders (estimated)
- Notes (moved poster, changed design, etc.)

### The “Ask at checkout” method (no tech)
Ask customers:
“Where did you find us?”
Give 4–5 options:
- gym
- shop
- salon
- community board
- friend/social media

Do this for 2 weeks and patterns appear.

### What success looks like (realistic)
Offline is a slow build that becomes strong.

- Week 1: scans and curiosity
- Week 2: first orders
- Week 3: repeat exposure starts working
- Week 4: placements become predictable channels

The key is to track lightly and improve weekly.

## The 4-week offline plan (start + iterate)

This is the system you can repeat anytime.
It’s realistic and not overwhelming.

### Week 1 — Setup and first placements (start small)
**Goal:** get 8–10 placements live.

**Day 1:**
- Choose 10 locations
- Pick poster types (Intro, Menu, Family)
- Create 5 QR variants

**Day 2:**
- Print posters (at least 2 of each type)
- Prepare tape/magnets
- Prepare your pitch (one sentence)

**Day 3–4:**
- Visit 3–5 locations
- Ask for permission
- Place posters (3–5 live placements)

**Day 5:**
- Log placements in your tracking table
- Post one simple story online: “Now available locally”

**Success metric:**
- 3–5 placements live
- 15–30 scans total

### Week 2 — Expand + improve message
**Goal:** reach 12–15 placements total.

- Revisit your best 3 places (check poster is still up)
- Add 5 more locations
- Improve one thing:
  - stronger photo
  - clearer “what is this?”
  - clearer ordering window

**Success metric:**
- scans increasing
- first orders linked to offline placements

### Week 3 — Focus on winners (not quantity)
**Goal:** double down on what works.

- Identify your top 3 placements by scans or orders
- Upgrade those placements:
  - replace poster with a fresh print
  - move to a better spot on the board
  - ask permission to place a better poster type (Menu or Family)

- Stop spending energy on weak spots.
No ego. Just data.

**Success metric:**
- repeat scans from the same location
- better conversion to orders

### Week 4 — Add one partnership move (distribution upgrade)
**Goal:** turn a placement into a relationship.

Choose one location that likes you (gym, shop, salon).
Offer a small upgrade:
- “member perk” QR poster (limited)
- “monthly tasting” (if allowed)
- “pre-order day” for their members

This is how flyers become distribution.

**Success metric:**
- one partner pilot started
- a predictable weekly visibility spot

## Partnership upgrades (turn posters into real distribution)

A poster is a starting point.
A partnership is the multiplier.

### Option A — Member perk (simple and classy)
Members get a small perk (limited):
- free add-on
- priority pickup slot
- “early access” to a tray drop

Keep it limited and simple so it stays fair.

### Option B — Tasting day (high trust)
Once per month:
- small samples at the location (if allowed)
- QR sign next to it

Taste builds trust faster than any poster.

### Option C — Pre-order window for that community
Example:
- “Gym meal prep pickup every Thursday”
Predictable visibility.

### Option D — Revenue share (only if needed)
If a partner wants payment:
- a small fixed monthly fee **or**
- commission per order  

Start with free placement first. Most partners will say yes if it looks clean.

## Common mistakes (and quick fixes)

1) **Placing posters randomly**  
Fix: choose high-fit locations with repeat exposure.

2) **Too much text**  
Fix: one clear message + QR. Keep it clean.

3) **Low-quality print**  
Fix: better photo, clean paper. It matters for food trust.

4) **No tracking**  
Fix: QR variants or “where did you find us?” for 2 weeks.

5) **Too many locations too fast**  
Fix: start with 10–15. Find the best 3–5. Then scale.

6) **No relationship**  
Fix: after 2–4 weeks, upgrade one placement into a partner pilot.

7) **Spam energy**  
Fix: one poster, polite pitch, respect their space.

## The 30-day partner pilot (simple, fair, and clean)

If you want partnerships to feel professional, use a pilot.
A pilot is a safe test that makes everyone comfortable.

### The pilot promise
- **Duration:** 30 days
- **Placement:** 1 poster
- **Goal:** see if it creates value for their customers/members
- **No drama:** remove anytime

### What you offer (pick one)
**Option A: Member perk (best)**
- small add-on for first 20 orders (limited)
- or priority pickup slots (limited)

**Option B: Tasting day**
- one small sample day (if allowed)
- QR next to samples

**Option C: Pre-order window**
- “members pickup day” weekly (predictable)

### What you track (simple)
- scans from that QR variant
- number of customers mentioning that place
- number of orders in 30 days

### Pilot follow-up script (end of 30 days)
> Thanks again for letting us try this for 30 days.  
> We saw [simple result: scans/orders].  
> If you’d like, we can continue in one of two simple ways:  
> 1) Keep it free (one poster) and we refresh it monthly, or  
> 2) Add a small partner benefit and a simple commission/fixed fee.  
> What feels best for you?

This keeps it calm and business-like.

## Weekly review (10 minutes) — how to improve without stress

Offline growth becomes powerful when you do a tiny weekly review.
Not a big “analysis session”. Just a small check.

### Your weekly questions
1) Which 3 placements got the most attention/scans?
2) Which 3 placements got the most orders (or mentions)?
3) What is the one easiest upgrade for next week?
   - better poster placement spot
   - fresher print
   - different poster type
   - one more location in the same category

### The rule
**Do not change everything at once.**
Change one variable, then see what happens.

## FAQ (simple answers)

**Do I need discounts for offline marketing?**  
No. If you use an offer, keep it limited and small. Trust is more valuable than discounts.

**How many posters should I have total?**  
Start with 10–15 placements. Find your best 3–5. Then scale slowly.

**How often should I replace posters?**  
Every 3–6 weeks is a good rhythm (or sooner if it looks worn).

**What if my poster gets removed?**  
It happens. Don’t take it personally. Replace it only if the location welcomes it.

**What if I feel nervous asking?**  
Use the one-sentence pitch. Bring one clean poster. Be polite. You’re offering something helpful, not begging.

**Can offline work if I’m not great at social media?**  
Yes. Offline is one of the best growth channels for busy home kitchens.
