## Big idea (general business first): platforms are outsourced distribution

In business, there are two ways to grow:
1) **Build your own channel** (your own website, your own pickup system, your own customers)
2) **Borrow a channel** (sell through someone else’s marketplace)

Delivery platforms are the second one.

They are basically **outsourcing**:
- You outsource discovery (people browsing the app)
- You outsource last‑mile logistics (drivers)
- You outsource checkout and payments (platform infrastructure)

In return, you pay:
- fees and commissions
- some control
- reputation risk (because delivery affects perception)

This is the same pattern you see in many industries:
- Amazon for products
- Airbnb for rentals
- App stores for software
- Booking.com for hotels

It can be powerful. It can also be brutal.

The question is not “Are platforms good or bad?”
The question is:

**Can you design a platform strategy where the platform works for you, not the other way around?**

That’s what this masterclass gives you: a system.

---

## The truth: platforms are not marketing. They are a business model choice.

Many people treat delivery platforms like “extra marketing”.
But platforms are not Instagram. They are not “awareness”.

Platforms are a **sales machine** with very specific rules:
- customers expect speed and reliability
- ratings matter immediately
- visibility is often tied to performance (and sometimes promotions)
- a few negative experiences can slow your growth fast
- you don’t own the customer relationship

So you don’t “try a platform casually”.
You test it like a business model.

---

## The platform win condition (what success looks like)

A successful platform setup usually has these three outcomes:

1) **You can predict profit per order**
Not perfect, but you know you’re not working for free.

2) **You can control chaos**
You can cap orders, control windows, and protect quality.

3) **Your rating stays stable**
Because your menu and packaging are designed for delivery reality.

If you can’t hit these, platforms become stressful noise.

# Part 1 — When platforms make sense (and when they don’t)

## Platforms can work well if…

### 1) Your food is “delivery‑robust”
Meaning it still tastes good after:
- 30–45 minutes from pack → eat
- a driver who isn’t careful
- a customer who doesn’t reheat properly

Delivery‑robust categories:
- stews, curries, braises (sauce protects texture)
- reheat‑friendly mains (packed smart)
- “kits” (components separated)
- cold‑ready items (served cold by design)
- dessert cups (high stability)

Risky categories:
- anything “crispy or die” (unless you redesign it)
- fries (often loses fast)
- anything that must be eaten immediately
- items that release steam into the box and collapse

### 2) You can price for fees without shame
If you cannot price higher on the platform than direct pickup, your margin will struggle.
That’s normal. Customers pay for convenience.

### 3) You can create a platform menu (not your full menu)
Platforms punish complexity.
A curated platform menu is the difference between calm and chaos.

### 4) You can set operational brakes
Caps. Windows. Sold out. Limited items.
If you “stay open all day” without brakes, you will burn out.

---

## Platforms often become a trap if…

- Your base margin is already thin
- Your kitchen is already near capacity
- You cannot increase prices on platform
- Your packaging is weak (leaks, soggy, inconsistent)
- Your menu is complex, customizable, or fragile
- You rely on discounts to get orders
- You feel anxious about saying “sold out”

If several of these are true, consider:
- focusing on pickup + VIP + recurring offers (higher control)
- or using local venue partnerships (golf clubs, camps, conferences) instead

Platforms should never force you into panic.

# Part 2 — The 5‑question decision check (yes/no in 5 minutes)

Use this before you build anything.

**Q1 — Can I build a platform menu (8–12 items) that survives delivery?**  
If no → redesign to kits/cold‑ready/reheat/finish‑at‑home.

**Q2 — Can I charge enough to cover fees + packaging + time + risk?**  
If no → high probability of working for free.

**Q3 — Can I limit volume (caps / windows / sold out)?**  
If no → quality and stress will collapse.

**Q4 — Do I have packaging that prevents leaks and protects texture?**  
If no → fix packaging before launching.

**Q5 — Do I have a “repeat bridge” that builds brand memory politely?**  
If no → you’ll get one‑time platform customers only.

If you answer “Yes” to 4 out of 5, a pilot is worth testing.

# Part 3 — Platform menu engineering (build for rating)

## The principle: fewer items, stronger systems

Start with **8–12 items**.
Yes, that feels small.
But small menus:
- reduce mistakes
- increase speed
- stabilize quality
- improve reviews
- help you manage stock and prep

A good starter platform menu:
- 4 mains
- 2 sides
- 2 add‑ons
- 1 dessert
- 1 drink (optional)

That’s enough.

---

## Build your menu around 4 “delivery formats”

### Format 1 — Sauce‑protected mains (reheat friendly)
These are your rating anchors.
Examples:
- chicken adobo
- Thai curry
- beef stew
- braised pork
- lentil stew / dhal

**Packaging rule:** rice separate if possible.

### Format 2 — Kits (components separated)
Kits reduce complaints because the customer gets better results.
Examples:
- ramen kit (broth separate, noodles separate)
- pho kit (broth, noodles, herbs separate)
- taco/wrap kit (filling separate, toppings separate)

**Positioning line:** “Packed separately for best quality.”

### Format 3 — Cold‑ready by design
Cold is stable when it’s intentional.
Examples:
- fresh rolls + dip
- noodle salad + dressing separate
- dessert cups

**Positioning line:** “Served cold (by design). Keep refrigerated.”

### Format 4 — Finish‑at‑home add‑ons
This saves crispy and increases margin.
Examples:
- frozen spring rolls / dumplings
- frozen snacks
- “airfryer pack”

**Positioning line:** “Cook from frozen for perfect crispiness.”

---

## The “never put this on platforms” list (honest)

Avoid these unless you redesign:
- fries
- delicate crispy fish
- tempura without separation
- anything that must be eaten instantly
- complex “build your own” dishes with many steps
- items that leak easily unless you have packaging solved

Platforms reward reliability more than creativity.

---

## Menu copy that protects your rating (micro‑copy library)

These tiny lines prevent misunderstandings (and misunderstandings create complaints).

**Reheat**
- “Reheat until steaming hot.”
- “Reheats beautifully—packed for delivery.”

**Kits**
- “Packed separately for best freshness.”
- “Assemble in 3–5 minutes.”

**Cold‑ready**
- “Served cold (by design).”
- “Keep refrigerated.”

**Finish‑at‑home**
- “Cook from frozen for best crispiness.”
- “Airfryer friendly.”

You don’t need long paragraphs. Just clarity.

# Part 4 — Pricing and margin (simple, realistic, calm)

## The platform reality: your costs increase in three ways

1) **Direct platform fees**
Commission + service fees + sometimes promo pressure.

2) **Operational costs**
More packaging, more time per order, more coordination.

3) **Risk costs**
Refunds, remakes, missing items, rating impacts.

So pricing must include a “platform buffer”.

---

## A practical profit check (no advanced accounting needed)

For each item, write:

- Ingredients: ___  
- Packaging: ___  
- Time cost (rough): ___  
- Platform fee estimate: ___  
- Risk buffer (refunds/waste): ___  
= Minimum price that makes sense: ___

You don’t need perfect numbers.
You need to avoid “I’m busy but broke”.

### The most important pricing rule
**Platform price is often higher than direct pickup price.**  
That is normal. It’s convenience + delivery infrastructure.

Don’t apologize. Just deliver value.

---

## The “profit per hour” lens (what really matters)

Platforms can increase orders but decrease profit per hour if:
- you pack too slowly
- you make too many mistakes
- you have too many menu items

So measure:
- how many orders per hour you can pack calmly
- how many mistakes happen
- how stressful the window feels (1–10)

A platform strategy is only worth it if it improves your **profit per hour**, not just volume.

# Part 5 — Control systems (how you stay sane)

## You must have a brake

Platforms reward availability, but availability without control becomes burnout.

Choose at least one brake:
- **Time windows** (best)
- **Max orders per window** (necessary)
- **Menu restrictions** (necessary)

### Recommended: platform windows
Example:
- Wed: 17:00–20:00
- Thu: 17:00–20:00
- Sat: 14:00–18:00

Windows allow:
- prep batching
- stable quality
- fewer delays
- less stress

If you must be “open all day”, risk rises sharply.

---

## The sold‑out mindset (protect your brand)

Sold out is not failure.
Sold out is quality control.

If you keep selling past capacity:
- quality drops
- ratings drop
- refunds rise
- stress rises

Then growth stops anyway.

So build the courage to say:
**“Sold out today.”**

That’s professional.

---

## Staging zones (tiny operational upgrade)

Create three zones:

- **Hot hold zone** (ready and warm)
- **Cold hold zone** (cold‑ready, desserts)
- **Ready for driver zone** (final checked, bagged)

Drivers arriving early or late becomes less chaotic when zones exist.

# Part 6 — Packaging and delivery quality (where ratings are won)

## Packaging is your silent salesperson

When a customer opens the bag, they judge:
- cleanliness
- leaks
- clarity
- “this feels professional”

A small packaging upgrade can be the best ROI you make.

---

## Packaging rules (simple but powerful)

- Separate sauces when leakage is possible
- Do not overfill sauce cups (use a fill line)
- Keep wet and dry separated
- Wipe edges before closing lids
- Double‑bag wet items if necessary
- Label clearly (dish name + hot/cold/reheat note)
- Add a small instruction line (micro‑copy)

### The “three must‑deliver” rating standard
1) Correct order (nothing missing)  
2) Tight packaging (no leaks)  
3) Clear expectation (hot/cold/reheat)  

This matters more than fancy menu descriptions.

# Part 7 — The hybrid strategy (platform + your own channel)

## Why hybrid is often the best long‑term model

A pure platform strategy is risky because:
- fees can change
- visibility can change
- you don’t own the customer relationship

A pure direct strategy is slower at first because:
- you must build your own demand

Hybrid gives you both:

- Platform = mid‑week convenience sales + discovery
- Direct pickup (RiceMap24) = higher margin, bundles, VIP, recurring, better control

### Example hybrid week
- Wed/Thu: platform windows (small robust menu)
- Sat: pickup window (bundles/trays, VIP priority, repeat customers)

This protects weekend capacity and builds your own base.

You cannot aggressively pull customers off platforms.
But you *can* build brand memory politely.

Put a small card in the bag:

This is not spam. It’s clarity.

Customers often think:

Give them the answer.

# Part 8 — The 14‑day platform pilot (test like a professional)

This is a calm pilot, not a life commitment.

## Days 1–2: Build the platform menu + pricing
- choose 8–12 items (robust formats only)
- price with platform buffer
- write micro‑copy for each item (hot/cold/reheat)

## Days 3–4: Packaging stress test
- simulate a 30‑minute delivery:
  - pack the item
  - wait 30 minutes
  - open it
  - check leaks, texture, clarity
- redesign items that fail

## Days 5–6: Control system setup
- set windows
- set max orders
- create staging zones
- write a one‑page packing SOP (template below)

## Days 7–8: Soft launch
- aim for 10–20 orders total
- track issues:
  - leaks
  - missing items
  - “cold food” complaints
  - refunds

## Days 9–10: Improve one thing
- remove 1 problem item
- upgrade packaging
- simplify menu

## Days 11–14: Scale carefully
- increase cap slightly only if quality is stable
- add 1 high-margin add‑on (optional)

At day 14, decide:
- continue as hybrid
- continue on platform with strict limits
- pause platform and focus on direct channels

A pilot is a test, not a promise.

# Part 10 — Outsourcing reality (what you outsource vs what you still own)

To use platforms well, you need a clear mental model of outsourcing.

When you join a delivery platform, you outsource:
- **traffic** (people browsing)
- **checkout** (payments, order flow)
- **last‑mile delivery** (driver network)
- **some customer service load** (platform support pathways)

But you still own:
- food quality
- packaging quality
- order accuracy
- preparation timing
- your brand perception in the customer’s mind

Here is the key business lesson:

**Outsourcing does not remove responsibility. It changes where the risk lives.**

On a platform, some risks move from “marketing risk” to “operations risk”:
- you may get orders, but you can lose reputation quickly if delivery quality fails
- you may grow volume, but you can lose profit if pricing and packaging are wrong

So success on platforms is basically:
**operations excellence + product design + control.**

---

## The hidden outsourcing costs (most people forget these)

In outsourcing, costs are rarely only the fee.

Typical hidden costs on platforms:
- extra packaging layers (bags, cups, separators)
- extra time per order (sorting, staging, driver handoff)
- refund risk (platform policy + customer expectations)
- menu complexity risk (more mistakes)
- “visibility taxes” (promotions to stay visible)
- stress cost (peak chaos)

This is why a platform strategy must be built around:
- limited menu
- strong SOP
- hard caps
- stable packaging

# Part 11 — Platform product management (treat each dish like a product)

On platforms, dishes behave like products with performance metrics.

Each dish has:
- conversion (does it get ordered?)
- rating impact (does it trigger complaints?)
- margin (does it pay you?)
- production load (does it slow you down?)

A professional platform operator does this monthly:
- **kills** the worst dish
- **improves** the best dish
- **adds** one new dish carefully

This is product management.

---

## The monthly “menu kill list” (simple and powerful)

Once per month, answer:

1) Which 2 items create the most complaints?
2) Which 2 items have the lowest margin?
3) Which 2 items take the most time?

If an item is “bad” on 2 out of 3:
- remove it
- or redesign it as a kit/cold‑ready/finish‑at‑home

This one habit keeps ratings stable.

# Part 14 — Delivery reality: temperature, time, and driver variance

Before you list any dish:
- pack it

# Part 15 — Operations: how to handle drivers without stress

Driver handoff is a common pain point.

### Create a “driver protocol”
- a clear pickup spot
- orders staged by number/name
- final check before handoff
- cold items kept separate and cold
- hot items kept hot (within safe practice)

### Use short signals
- “Order ready”
- “Hold 2 minutes”
Short signals reduce chaos.

### Protect your flow
If you stop cooking to talk to drivers constantly, quality drops.
So keep your handoff process quick and repeatable.

# Part 21 — Profit protection: the “platform buffer” rule (simple)

Here’s a simple rule that keeps many small kitchens safe:

**If you can’t keep a buffer, you can’t scale a platform.**

Your platform buffer is:
- extra margin to absorb refunds, delays, packaging upgrades, and mistakes

### A practical buffer method
For every platform week, set aside a small percentage of platform revenue as:
- “platform risk buffer”

Why?
Because platforms have higher variance:
- more refunds
- more delivery complaints
- more packaging issues
- more sudden spikes

If you keep a buffer, you don’t panic when something goes wrong.
You fix it calmly.
