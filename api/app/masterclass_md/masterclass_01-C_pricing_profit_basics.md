# Masterclass 01 — Pricing & Profit Basics (For Home Kitchens)


**What you will learn (simple):**
- How to price so you **don’t lose money** (even if you cook at home, part-time).
- How to calculate a **fair price** in 10 minutes (without spreadsheets).
- How to increase profit using **bundles, add-ons, and smart portioning** (without feeling “pushy”).
- How to avoid the most common pricing mistakes home kitchens make.

**Who this is for:**
- You are a home kitchen seller (part-time or small batches).
- You want more stable income and less stress.
- You want to be fair — but also **earn something** for your time.

**Time:** 30–60 minutes to read + 10 minutes to apply.

---

## Big idea (1 minute)

If you price too low, you feel busy but you stay broke.  
If you price too high without value, customers hesitate.

Good pricing is simple:

**A good price = (food cost) + (packaging) + (platform fees) + (your time) + (a small profit buffer).**

That’s it.

You don’t need an MBA. You just need a **simple method** you can repeat.

---

## Why this matters (simple explanation)

Many home kitchens make these problems:

1) **They price like a friend, not like a business.**  
   “I don’t want to be expensive.”  
   Result: you work hard, but the money is not worth it.

2) **They forget hidden costs.**  
   Oil, spices, sauce, rice, gas/electricity, kitchen paper, containers, delivery fuel, payment fees.  
   Result: your “profit” disappears.

3) **They try to compete with cheap fast food.**  
   But you are not a big chain. You are small-batch, homemade, and personal.  
   Result: wrong game, wrong pricing.

4) **They don’t pay themselves.**  
   Time is a real cost. Even part-time sellers need to earn for time.

**Pricing is not just numbers. Pricing is your energy.**  
When the price is right, you can cook with joy, stay consistent, and customers get better food.

---

## How customers think about price (very practical)

Customers do not calculate your ingredient cost. They ask:

- “Does it look delicious?”
- “Does it feel safe and clean?”
- “Is it worth it compared to ordering elsewhere?”
- “Is it easy to order and pick up?”

So pricing becomes easier when you increase **value signals**:

- clear portion photo (shows value)
- clean packaging (trust)
- consistent menu (confidence)
- add-ons/bundles (feels like a full meal)

**You don’t always need discounts. You need clarity and trust.**

---

## The simple framework: Price in 4 layers

Think of your price in 4 layers:

### Layer 1) Hard costs (money you pay)
- ingredients
- packaging/containers
- platform/payment fees (if any)
- delivery fuel (if you do delivery)

### Layer 2) Soft costs (easy to forget)
- oil, spices, sauces, rice
- electricity/gas
- cleaning supplies
- paper towels, gloves (optional)
- small waste (food that gets thrown away)

### Layer 3) Your time
- shopping
- prep
- cooking
- packing
- messaging customers
- cleanup

### Layer 4) Profit buffer
- unexpected cost
- last-minute price changes
- your business growth

A healthy home kitchen price includes all 4 layers.

## Step-by-step: The 10-minute pricing method (no spreadsheet)

You will calculate a **baseline price** for one dish in 10 minutes.

### Step 1 — Choose one dish to price
Pick your hero dish (the one you sell most):
- adobo, kare-kare, sinigang
- pad thai, green curry, basil chicken
- pho, bun cha, spring rolls
- fried rice, dumplings, noodle bowls

Write it down: **Dish = [Dish]**

### Step 2 — Estimate your food cost per portion (simple)
You have two easy ways:

#### Option A (fast): “Batch cost ÷ portions”
1) Think of a normal batch you cook (example: 10 portions).
2) Add the cost of the main ingredients for the whole batch.
3) Divide by number of portions.

Example:
- Chicken 220
- Vegetables 60
- Coconut milk 40
- Rice 30
- Total batch ingredients: 350
- Portions: 10  
**Food cost per portion = 350 ÷ 10 = 35**

#### Option B (super fast): “Grocery receipt method”
1) Look at your last grocery receipt.
2) Circle what you used for the dish.
3) Estimate how many portions it made.
4) Divide.

This is not perfect — it is **good enough**.

### Step 3 — Add packaging cost
Packaging is real cost:
- container + lid
- bag
- sauce cup
- napkin (optional)

Example:
- Container 4
- Sauce cup 1
- Bag 1  
**Packaging = 6 per portion**

### Step 4 — Add “extra cost” (for spices, oil, electricity)
This is the cost many people forget.
Add a simple buffer:
- **Small dish:** add 5–8
- **Normal dish:** add 8–15
- **Meat/seafood dish:** add 10–20

Example: add **10**

### Step 5 — Add platform/payment fees (if any)
If you have Stripe or other fees, add it here.  
Simple method:
- add **3–5%** of price (later)
Or
- add a fixed number per portion (example 3–6)

If you are not sure, you can skip this in the first calculation and add it later.

### Step 6 — Add “time pay” (simple rule)
You need to pay your time. But keep it simple.

Pick a “time pay” per portion:
- **Beginner rule:** 10–25 per portion  
- **If cooking is heavy/time-consuming:** 25–50 per portion  
- **If you sell very small volume:** choose higher

How to choose:
- If you cook 10 portions and you spend 3 hours total (shopping+prep+cook+pack+cleanup),
  and you want 150 per hour, your time cost total is 450.
  450 ÷ 10 portions = **45** per portion.

If that feels too high, reduce by improving efficiency or raising price gradually.

### Step 7 — Add profit buffer
Add a small buffer:
- **5–15 per portion**, depending on dish.

This covers small mistakes and helps you grow.

## Example: A complete baseline price (easy)

Let’s say:
- Food cost: 35
- Packaging: 6
- Extra cost: 10
- Time pay: 30
- Profit buffer: 10

Baseline = 35 + 6 + 10 + 30 + 10 = **91**

Now you pick a customer-friendly price point. For example:
- 89
- 95
- 99
- 109

If your market is Norway and costs are higher, your baseline might be higher.  
That is normal.

**The goal is not “cheap.” The goal is “worth it.”**

## The “price confidence test” (very important)

After you choose a price, ask:

1) If I sell 10 portions, will I be happy with the money after costs?  
2) If I sell 20 portions, will I still have energy next week?  
3) Do I feel proud to sell this for this price?

If you feel uncomfortable because it’s “too expensive,” remember:

- Restaurants charge for rent and staff.
- You charge for **time, skill, and small batches**.
- Customers pay for **experience** and **convenience**.

- clear portion photo

## Pricing tactics that increase profit (without feeling pushy)

### 1) Add-ons (simple upsell)
One add-on can increase order value a lot:
- extra rice
- spring rolls / lumpia
- sauce cup
- dessert
- drink

**Rule:** spotlight only 1–2 add-ons, not 10.

**Pricing add-ons:**
- Add-on price should feel easy to add.
- Many add-ons have high margin if they use simple ingredients.

**Example add-on menu (simple):**
- Extra rice: +15
- Spring roll (2 pcs): +35
- Extra sauce: +10

### 2) Bundles / share packs
Bundles increase profit because:
- less messaging per portion
- less packing time per portion
- customers buy more at once

**Simple bundle examples:**
- 2 portions: small discount (or no discount, just convenience)
- 4 portions: “family pack”
- “main + side” combo

**Bundle pricing rule:**  
Do not discount too hard. You are not a big chain.
Use “value framing”:
- “Family pack”
- “Order together”
- “Easy dinner”

### 3) Portion strategy (value + profit)
Portion size is both marketing and profit.

- If portions are too big: profit disappears.
- If too small: customers complain.

**Simple solution:**
- Decide your portion size and keep it consistent.
- Take a “portion photo” every time you change it.

**Portion proof = higher trust and fewer price complaints.**

### 4) One “hero dish” price anchor
Have one main dish that becomes your “known price”.
Customers learn it. They stop asking.

Then:
- Add-ons are easy.
- Bundles feel simple.

This is why “signature dish” campaigns work well.

## How to price for different situations

### A) One dish, small batch (like 6–10 portions)
Your time cost per portion is higher.  
So your price should be higher.

**Solution options:**
- Raise price slightly
- Use bundles to sell more per customer
- Use add-ons to increase order value

### B) High volume day (like 20–40 portions)
Your time cost per portion becomes lower.  
You can make more profit and feel less stress per portion.

**Solution:**
- Encourage group orders (family/office)
- Use a single pickup window (less messaging)

### C) Catering / office trays
Different thinking:
- Customers value convenience and “feeding many people”.
- Trays can be profitable if you simplify menu.

**Basic catering price rule:**
- Create 1–2 tray options only
- Price by portions (10, 20)
- Add a clear pickup time

## What to do when customers say: “Too expensive”

First: stay calm. You don’t need to argue.

A simple reply:
> “Totally understand 😊 We make small batches at home with good ingredients.  
> If you want to try once, DM ORDER and I’ll suggest the best option.”

You can also offer:
- smaller portion option (if possible)
- simple add-on instead of discount

**Discounts train customers to wait. Value trains customers to buy.**

## Common mistakes (and fixes)

### Mistake 1: Pricing only by ingredient cost
**Fix:** always add packaging + time + buffer.

### Mistake 2: “I feel bad charging for my time”
**Fix:** your time is real. Without time pay, you will quit.

### Mistake 3: Too many menu items
**Fix:** keep 1–3 hero items. Simpler = more profit.

### Mistake 4: Big discounts to get orders
**Fix:** do bundles/add-ons instead. Keep discounts small and rare.

### Mistake 5: Changing price every week
**Fix:** keep your hero dish stable. Adjust slowly if needed.

### Mistake 6: No portion proof
**Fix:** post a portion/box photo. It reduces price questions.

### Mistake 7: Too much messaging time
**Fix:** use templates + one pickup window + bundles.

## “Are my prices too low?” quick signs

If 2–3 of these are true, your price is likely too low:

- You feel tired, but you don’t feel rewarded.
- You avoid calculating costs because it makes you sad.
- You sell out but you still don’t see money.
- Customers ask for discounts often (you attract bargain hunters).
- You cannot afford better packaging.
- You skip marketing because you feel “what’s the point”.

A healthy price gives you:
- energy to cook next week
- money to improve photos/packaging
- room to grow

## How to raise prices without losing customers (gentle method)

Many home kitchens are afraid to increase prices. Here is a safe way:

### Step 1: Improve “value signals” first (same week)
Before you raise prices:
- post a **portion photo** (shows value)
- post a **packing photo** (shows trust)
- post a short **cooking clip** (shows fresh)

This makes the higher price feel normal.

### Step 2: Raise only one dish first
Don’t change everything. Choose your hero dish.

Example:
- Old price: 95
- New price: 105

### Step 3: Use a calm explanation (optional)
You can say:
“Hi 😊 We updated prices a little because ingredients and packaging are more expensive.  
We still cook fresh at home with care ❤️”

You don’t need long explanations.

### Step 4: Keep the price stable for 1–2 months
Stability builds trust. Customers learn your price.

## How to handle “cheap competitor” thinking

Sometimes customers compare you to cheap takeout or frozen food.

Your best response is not arguing. Your best response is **positioning**:

- “Homemade”
- “Small batch”
- “Made fresh”
- “Local pickup”
- “Real comfort food”
- “Clean packing”

You can also use a simple “quality line”:
“Made with good ingredients — cooked like we cook for family.”

People who want the cheapest will not be your best customers.  
Your goal is to attract people who value taste and trust.

## A practical guide to bundles (so you don’t lose money)

Bundles can increase sales, but only if priced correctly.

### Bundle rule 1: Bundle should save YOU time
A bundle is good when:
- one customer buys 2–4 portions
- fewer messages
- fewer pick-ups
- less stress

### Bundle rule 2: Do not give big discounts
Instead of discounting, use “value framing”.

**Bad bundle example:** 20% off (too much)  
**Good bundle example:** small discount or none + better wording

### Bundle rule 3: Make bundles simple
Offer 1–2 bundle choices only:
- “2-portion pack”
- “4-portion family pack”

### Example bundle pricing
If single portion price is 109:
- 2 pack: 209 (small saving)
- 4 pack: 399 (small saving + feels like a deal)

Or no discount:
- 2 pack: 218 (same price, just easier)
- 4 pack: 436

Both can work. Many customers choose bundle just because it’s easy.

## Add-on strategy (the most underrated profit tool)

Add-ons work because they are “small yes”.

Customers think:
“I’m already ordering. Why not add rice/sauce/rolls?”

### Best add-ons for home kitchens
- rice (very profitable)
- sauce cup (very profitable)
- spring rolls / lumpia
- small dessert
- extra meat (higher price)

### Add-on pricing tips
- Keep add-on prices simple and round.
- Do not offer too many options.
- Always show add-on in a photo sometimes (it sells better).

**Example:**  
“Add-on this week: crispy spring rolls (2 pcs) +35”

## Real mini-cases (simple and realistic)

### Case A — “Busy but broke”
Maria sells 10 portions every weekend at 85 each.
She thinks: “I’m selling out, so I’m successful.”

But after costs:
- food + packaging + extras = 55
Profit before time = 30 per portion
10 portions = 300 total

She spent:
- 4 hours shopping + cooking + packing + cleanup
300 ÷ 4 hours = 75 per hour (before any unexpected cost)

She feels tired and stops after 2 months.

**Fix:** She raises price to 105, adds a 4-portion family pack, and adds “extra rice +15”.
Now her profit supports her time and she continues.

### Case B — “People say it’s expensive”
A Thai kitchen sells green curry for 139.
Some customers comment: “Too expensive.”

But the kitchen shows:
- clean packing
- big portion
- fresh cooking clip
They keep the message short: “Made fresh at home.”

They keep the price stable for 2 months.
The price complaints stop because new customers understand the value.

### Case C — “Office orders change everything”
A Vietnamese kitchen tries one “Office Lunch Week”.
One coworker orders for 8 people.
That is 8 portions in one pickup and one message thread.

The kitchen realizes:
Office orders reduce stress and increase income.

**Lesson:** pricing is not only numbers — it is also choosing a better customer type.

## FAQ (quick answers)

### “Should I show prices publicly?”
Both can work.

If you get many price questions, it can help to show:
- a simple “starting from” price
- or a short menu photo with prices

If you prefer DM:
That’s OK too — just reply fast with a template.

### “Should I charge extra for delivery?”
Yes, if delivery costs you time and fuel.
Keep it simple:
- delivery fee by area OR
- delivery only on one day/time window

### “How do I price when I don’t know exact costs?”
Start with estimates. Improve later.
The goal is direction, not perfection.

### “What if I only cook sometimes?”
Your time cost is higher. Your price must reflect that.
Part-time does not mean cheap. Part-time means limited supply.
