# Business Coach #32-A — Profit Per Dish Thinking (Contribution Margin Without Spreadsheets)
## The owner skill that protects net: knowing which dishes actually pay you back

A lot of home kitchen owners focus on sales:
- “How many orders did we get?”
- “How much did we sell?”

But stable businesses also focus on something more important:

**Profit per dish.**

Because two dishes can sell for the same price…  
and produce very different net profit.

When you understand profit per dish, your decisions get easier:
- which dish should be an anchor
- which dish should be rotation only
- which dish needs redesign (portion, packaging, ingredients)
- where you can invest in quality without losing net
- how to compete without underpricing

This month is about a simple skill:
**contribution margin thinking**—without spreadsheets.

Contribution margin is simply:
> “After the direct costs of serving this dish, how much is left to support the business and pay you?”

You can do this with calm, simple estimates.

---

## 1) Why profit per dish matters more as you grow

As your kitchen grows:
- packaging costs rise
- ingredient costs fluctuate
- transport and time pressure rises
- mistakes become more expensive
- waste becomes more visible

If you only track total sales, you can become:
busy but not rewarded.

Profit per dish protects you from that.

It helps you focus on winners that are:
- loved by customers
- repeatable
- profitable
- sustainable to produce

That is the true “anchor dish” definition.

---

## 2) The simple profit-per-dish model (no heavy math)

You don’t need perfect accuracy.
You need useful clarity.

Think in three layers:

### Layer 1: Price
What the customer pays.

### Layer 2: Direct costs
Costs directly tied to the dish:
- ingredients (main)
- packaging (container, cups, bag)
- add-on packaging (if needed)

### Layer 3: What remains
What remains supports:
- your time and energy
- your fixed costs (small equipment, supplies)
- transport and running costs
- buffer and reinvestment
- your profit

This is your “dish contribution.”

---

This system becomes stronger when your ledger is alive.

If you log expenses with categories:
- Ingredients
- Packaging
- Transport

…and keep your dish naming consistent in income entries (dish names or dish lines), you can *learn from reality* over time.

You don’t need perfect dish costing inside the app.
You need:
- stable categories
- stable dish naming
- periodic comparisons (Last 30 vs Prev 30)
- your own calm estimates

That’s enough.

## 4) The three “profit killers” that hide inside dishes

Here are common reasons a dish has weak profit:

### Profit killer 1: Ingredient intensity
The dish requires expensive ingredients or too many unique items.

Owner fix:
- build ingredient overlap with anchors
- simplify variations
- redesign the dish around a strong base

### Profit killer 2: Packaging intensity
The dish needs extra cups, special containers, or leak-proof solutions.

Owner fix:
- redesign the dish (separate what must be separate)
- standardize packaging per dish type
- tighten sauce portioning to reduce cups and mess

### Profit killer 3: Time intensity
The dish takes much longer to produce or requires many steps.

Owner fix:
- simplify prep steps
- batch components
- keep the dish as rotation, not anchor
- move the dish into “special order” only (if you choose)

Time is a cost even if you don’t “pay yourself hourly.”
Time intensity drains the business.

---

## 5) Anchor dish decision rule: love + repeatability + profit

A dish should be an anchor when it checks three boxes:

1) Customers love it and repeat it  
2) It is repeatable in quality and packaging  
3) It protects net profit (not thin margins)

If a dish is loved but has weak profit, don’t remove it instantly.
Redesign it.

Owners don’t panic.
Owners redesign.

---

## 6) The “redesign instead of remove” mindset

When a dish is popular but not profitable, use redesign levers:

### Lever A: Portion control
- standard portion method (scale/scoop/container fill)
- reduce drift and protect net
- keep customer experience consistent

### Lever B: Packaging redesign
- separate wet and crisp
- seal liquids
- reduce spills and complaints
- simplify packaging types

### Lever C: Ingredient overlap
- reuse base ingredients across dishes
- reduce unique items
- reduce waste and emergency purchases

### Lever D: Add-on structure
Sometimes the dish is fine, but the order value is too low.
Bundles and add-ons can raise value without raising chaos.

---

## 7) A practical way to rank your dishes (simple, owner-friendly)

You can rank dishes using three scores (1–5):
- Repeatability (quality + packaging)
- Cost intensity (ingredients + packaging)
- Time intensity (steps + prep)

You don’t need a spreadsheet.
You just need a clear feeling:
- which dish is easiest to repeat and protect net?
- which dish needs redesign?
- which dish should be rotation only?

This ranking removes emotional decision-making.

## 10) A “good enough” costing routine you can run in your head

You don’t need exact cents.
You need a repeatable routine that gives you direction.

A calm routine:
1) Estimate ingredient cost per portion (rough, honest)  
2) Add packaging cost per order (container + cups + bag)  
3) Notice time intensity (easy / medium / heavy)  
4) Ask: “Does this dish pay me back for the work?”  

If the answer is “not really,” you don’t panic.
You redesign the dish or change its role:
- rotation only
- limited time special
- premium version
- bundle-only (to increase order value)

This is how owners keep creativity without sacrificing net.

---

## 11) The “dish role” system (anchors, rotation, premium, special order)

Profit-per-dish thinking becomes very easy when every dish has a role.

### Role 1: Anchor dish
- most repeatable
- strong profit protection
- stable packaging
- stable prep rhythm

### Role 2: Rotation dish
- creative
- seasonal
- still repeatable, but not always available
- protects ingredient waste by being limited

### Role 3: Premium dish
- higher ingredient intensity OR higher time intensity
- priced accordingly
- not meant to be the “cheap option”
- protects your craft value

### Role 4: Special-order dish
- only when requested
- protects your week from chaos
- protects margin because it’s not constant

If you assign roles, you stop arguing with your menu.
You lead it.

---

## 12) Portion drift is the silent profit leak (and the easiest to fix)

Many owners lose profit not because prices are wrong—  
but because portions drift upward slowly.

Portion drift happens when:
- you “just add a little more” repeatedly
- different days produce different serving sizes
- helpers portion differently than you

The fix is not harshness.
The fix is a standard:
- one scoop
- one scale weight
- one container fill line

Portion standard protects:
- taste balance
- customer expectation
- profit margin
- fairness perception

It also makes your business feel more professional.

---

## 13) Packaging cost per dish (a quick owner method)

You don’t need to cost every item perfectly.
But you should know which dishes are “packaging heavy.”

Packaging heavy dishes often include:
- sauces that require multiple cups
- crispy items requiring separation + venting
- multi-component bowls with many toppings

Owner method:
- label dishes as Low / Medium / High packaging intensity

Then choose a strategy:
- redesign to reduce cups
- standardize a better-value packaging set
- price the dish to match the packaging reality

Packaging is part of the product.
It must be funded by the product.

---

## 14) Use Top dishes + your role system to simplify the menu

Top dishes tells you what is proven.

Combine Top dishes with roles:
- top repeat dish + good margin → anchor
- top dish + weak margin → redesign
- medium demand + high cost/time → rotation or premium
- low demand + unique ingredients → remove or special-order only

This is not about being “strict.”
This is about building a menu you can sustain.
