# Business Coach #44-A — Local Market Intelligence Sprint (Find Gaps, Win Positioning)
## A practical owner skill: read your local market fast—then build a smarter menu position without copying

If you want something truly “new” (not another post about routines), this is it:

**Market intelligence.**

Not corporate research.
Not expensive reports.

Just a sharp owner ability:
- understand what customers are used to seeing
- spot the gaps competitors are not filling
- choose a position that is easier to win
- build offers that fit local demand

When you do this well, you stop guessing.
You stop copying.
You start leading.

This month is a simple Market Intelligence Sprint you can repeat anytime.

---

## 1) The goal: find “gaps,” not competitors

Many owners look at competitors and feel pressure:
- “They have more items.”
- “They are cheaper.”
- “They are louder.”

That’s the wrong focus.

The goal is not to match them.
The goal is to find gaps:
- unmet needs
- underserved segments
- weak experiences
- boring menus
- missing cultural depth
- missing trustworthy options

Gaps are where small businesses win.

---

## 2) The 4-part market map (fast and useful)

A strong local market map can be built using four buckets:

### Bucket A: Everyday takeaway (habit competitor)
- the “default” places people order from
- often generic, familiar
- wins on habit and speed

### Bucket B: Food trucks / event food (novelty competitor)
- wins on fun
- wins on limited menu mastery
- often premium priced at events

### Bucket C: Delivery app menus (comparison competitor)
- wins on convenience and discovery
- prices often higher due to fees
- strong photo-driven choices

### Bucket D: Grocery ready meals (lazy competitor)
- wins on price and convenience
- often lower taste/authenticity

You don’t need to hate these competitors.
You just need to understand what they train customers to expect.

---

## 3) What to observe (the real intelligence points)

The most useful intelligence points are not “what is their logo.”

They are:

### A) Formats that dominate
- bowls?
- wraps?
- burgers?
- pizza?
- salads?
- meal deals?

Formats tell you what customers already understand.

### B) Price bands
Customers are trained by local price ranges.
You don’t copy them—you understand them.

Observe:
- typical main dish range
- typical combo range
- typical drink/dessert range
- premium range (what “expensive but accepted” looks like)

### C) Menu structure
- how many “anchors” do they really have?
- how many add-ons?
- do they push combos?

Structure reveals what converts.

### D) Experience weak spots
Look for weakness:
- messy packaging
- unclear pickup
- inconsistent reviews
- “tastes different every time”
- long waits or late delivery

Weak spots are your opportunity.

---

## 4) The “gap finder” questions (the easiest way to win)

If you want one powerful method, ask these questions:

1) **What do customers want that competitors don’t deliver consistently?**  
2) **Which segment is underserved (lunch, office, family bundles, premium treat)?**  
3) **What experience detail is weak locally (clean eating, texture, clarity)?**  
4) **Which cultural flavor is missing or watered down?**  
5) **What format is missing that feels “easy to buy” (wraps/baguettes/milkshake lane)?**  

Your business wins when you choose one gap and fill it repeatedly.

---

## 5) The gap-to-position method (turn observations into strategy)

Once you identify a gap, you turn it into a position.

Examples of positions (concept level):
- “weekday lunch favorite that is clean and fast”
- “authentic Asian comfort with a signature sauce identity”
- “premium treat lane: one extraordinary edge item”
- “family packs that feel like real value”
- “office solution: reliable timing and labeled portions”

A position is not a slogan.
A position is what people experience repeatedly.

---

## 6) The “don’t copy—differentiate” rule

Market intelligence is not permission to clone menus.

The rule:
- copy the structure (clarity)
- not the dish

Examples:
- they sell wraps → you sell a signature cultural wrap with your flavor lane
- they sell bowls → you sell bowls with a signature finish and better depth
- they sell combos → you sell a simpler, cleaner offer stack

You borrow what customers understand, then you make it yours.

That is strategic.

---

## 7) A fresh “market gap” play (wraps/baguettes/drinks as a lane)

Here’s a very common gap in many places:

**Portable lunch formats with real flavor depth.**

Takeaway often has wraps, but they are generic.
Food trucks often have signature items, but not repeatable daily.

So you can build a lane:
- 1–2 signature wraps/baguettes (portable, clean)
- 1 signature drink or dessert add-on
- one simple combo

This is not about doing more.
It’s about doing one format extremely well.

---

## 8) What to do after the sprint (the one decision)

Market intelligence becomes valuable only when you decide one move:

- choose one gap
- choose one segment
- choose one offer structure
- commit for a period

Then measure results.

A small business wins by going deeper, not wider.

## 9) A “gap” example library (use as inspiration)

Here are gap types that show up often:

- Clean, portable lunch formats with real flavor depth
- Better texture protection than local takeaway
- Clear, small office packs (timing + labeling)
- Family bundles that feel like obvious value
- One signature drink/dessert lane that makes meals feel complete
- Authentic cultural flavor that isn’t watered down
- A premium “trusted favorite” option in a market full of inconsistency

You don’t need to fill all gaps.
Fill one gap repeatedly.

That’s how small businesses win.

## 10) Final reminder

Market intelligence is not a one-time thing.
It’s a habit: observe → choose a gap → commit for a period → measure.

---

## 11) Market “white space” you can own (positions that are easier to win)

A gap becomes a winning position when it is:
- valuable to customers
- hard for competitors to deliver consistently
- easy for you to repeat with pride

Examples of white space positions:

### White space A: “Clean portable lunch with real flavor”
Many places have lunch options, but they’re often:
- boring
- messy
- inconsistent
If you deliver clean portable flavor with a signature system, you win.

### White space B: “Authentic comfort—consistent”
Some markets have “Asian” options, but they’re watered down or inconsistent.
Authentic + consistent becomes a premium position.

### White space C: “Family value without confusion”
Families want value and ease.
If your structure makes ordering for 3–5 people obvious, you win.

### White space D: “Office solution that repeats”
Offices want timing and predictability.
If your offer is clear and repeatable, you win.

White space is not about being different for fun.
It’s about being different in a way customers care about.

---

## 12) A simple “market scan” checklist (what to notice in 15 minutes)

When you scan the market (even casually), notice:

- What are the top 3 most common formats? (bowls, wraps, pizza, etc.)
- What is the local “safe price” for a main dish?
- What is the accepted premium price?
- What add-ons show up everywhere? (drinks, fries, dessert)
- What is the most common weakness? (messy, slow, inconsistent)
- What is missing that customers would love?

This is enough to generate strategy.

---

## 13) Use market insight to build a “clean choice ladder”

Once you understand price bands and formats, build a simple ladder:
- Standard (safe favorite)
- Combo (complete meal)
- Premium (best version)

This ladder matches how customers already think.
You’re not forcing behavior.
You’re making decisions easier.

---

## 14) The “gap must be repeatable” rule

A gap is only useful if you can fill it repeatedly.

If the gap requires:
- rare ingredients
- fragile packaging
- too much time per order

…then it’s not a gap you can own sustainably.

Choose gaps that fit your reality and your lane.

---

## 15) The one decision that turns intelligence into growth

End every market sprint with one sentence:

**“We will own ______ for the next 30 days.”**

Then align:
- menu structure
- offer stack
- messaging

This is how intelligence becomes results.
