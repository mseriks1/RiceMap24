# Business Coach #43-A — Pricing Psychology (Anchors, Decoys, Premium Option) Simple And Ethical
## How to price in a way that feels fair and increases profit—without tricks or pressure

Pricing is not only math.
Pricing is also psychology.

That doesn’t mean manipulation.
It means understanding how customers make choices.

Customers rarely know what a dish “should” cost.
They decide based on:
- perceived value
- trust
- comparison points on your menu
- how easy it is to choose
- whether the price feels fair for what they get

This month is about simple, ethical pricing psychology—especially useful for home kitchens competing with takeaway and food trucks.

We will cover three practical concepts:
- **Anchor pricing**
- **Premium option**
- **Decoy option** (ethical use)

You will use these to make your menu easier to choose and more profitable—without becoming salesy.

And remember:
**Never remove what sells well. Protect anchors.**
Pricing psychology should strengthen anchors, not destroy them.

---

## 1) Pricing psychology is about clarity, not tricks

When pricing is clear, customers feel safe.

When customers feel safe, they buy and repeat.

So the goal is:
- fewer doubts
- fewer questions
- fewer negotiations
- more confident buying

Clarity is the real pricing advantage.

---

## 2) Anchor pricing (the main reference point)

An anchor price is the price customers use as a reference.

Usually, your anchor dish becomes your price reference:
- it’s the best seller
- it’s familiar
- it’s what customers compare everything else to

So anchor pricing means:
- your anchor dish price should be sustainable
- your anchor should be consistent in portion and experience
- your anchor sets “what your kitchen costs”

If your anchor is underpriced, your whole menu feels undervalued.
If your anchor is priced sustainably, the menu feels normal.

Anchors protect value.

---

## 3) The premium option (a calm way to increase order value)

A premium option is not a different dish.
It’s a higher-value version of a dish or offer.

Examples (concept level):
- extra protein
- premium topping
- upgraded side
- signature add-on bundle
- “premium pack” version

Why premium options work:
- some customers want “more”
- some customers want “best”
- some customers want higher portion or higher quality signals

Premium options allow you to serve those customers without changing the whole menu.

Premium options can also protect profitability when ingredient costs rise.

---

## 4) The decoy option (ethical use)

A decoy option is a choice that helps customers choose more easily.

Used ethically, it’s not a trick.
It’s menu design.

Example concept:
- a small option
- a standard option
- a premium option

Customers often choose the middle option when:
- it feels balanced
- it feels safe
- it feels fair

The decoy’s job is to create clarity:
- “Oh, the standard option is the right fit.”

Ethical decoys:
- are real options
- have real value
- do not hide information
- do not pressure customers

The goal is simply to reduce confusion.

---

## 5) Why “three options” often sells better than “many options”

Too many options creates decision fatigue.

Three options creates clarity.

Examples of ethical three-option structures:
- Small / Standard / Premium
- Lunch / Combo / Premium Combo
- Single / Family / Office

This is not manipulation.
This is helping people decide.

---

## 6) Pricing psychology must be supported by value signals

No psychological structure can save a weak experience.

Value signals matter:
- clean packaging
- sealed sauces
- consistent portion
- smooth pickup
- taste consistency

When value signals are strong, pricing feels fair.

When value signals are weak, any price feels “too high.”

So pricing psychology is a layer on top of trust.

Trust comes first.

---

## 7) A simple ethical pricing structure you can use

Here is a simple structure that fits many home kitchens:

- Anchor dish (standard)  
- Combo version (anchor + add-on)  
- Premium version (combo + premium add-on)  

This creates:
- easy choice
- higher AOV
- premium option for customers who want it

And it keeps the menu clean.

---

## 8) The owner mindset: price to deliver the promise

Your price is what allows you to deliver:
- consistency
- cleanliness
- reliability
- cultural quality
- repeatable excellence

Pricing is not “charging more.”
It is funding the promise.

That is ethical.

## 9) Practical examples (concept level, not exact prices)

### Example A: The anchor + combo + premium ladder
- Anchor: main dish
- Combo: main + drink or side
- Premium: combo + premium topping or extra protein

Customers choose based on appetite and value.
You protect net across different customer needs.

### Example B: The “decoy” that creates clarity
- Small: for light eaters
- Standard: best value
- Premium: for bigger appetite

Most customers choose standard.
Premium catches customers who want more.
Small reduces fear for first-time buyers.

The point is not to push.
The point is to let customers self-select easily.

---

## 10) A warning: don’t use psychology to hide weak value

If the experience is messy or inconsistent, customers will feel tricked.

So the ethical rule is:
- improve value signals first
- then structure choices

When value is real, structure is helpful.

---

## 11) Your best psychological tool is consistency

The strongest “pricing psychology” is not menu tricks.
It is repeatable excellence.

When customers know you are consistent, price pressure drops.

Consistency is the ultimate value anchor.

---

## Final reminder

Pricing psychology is simply designing choice with respect.
Respect for customer clarity.
Respect for your craft.

---

## 12) The “fairness test” (how to keep pricing ethical)

If you want to stay fully ethical and customer-friendly, use a fairness test:

- Is the customer clearly told what they get?
- Is the portion consistent across orders?
- Is the price consistent (no surprise fees)?
- Does the premium option add real value (not just a new name)?
- Can a first-time customer choose a safe option easily?

If yes, your structure is ethical.

Ethical pricing is not about being cheap.
It is about being clear, consistent, and respectful.

---

## 13) When to create a premium option vs when to raise the base price

A premium option is best when:
- some customers want more (bigger appetite)
- ingredient costs are rising but you don’t want to shift all pricing at once
- you want to increase AOV without pressuring everyone
- you want a “best” version that feels special

Raising base price is best when:
- costs have permanently changed across the board
- the dish is consistently underpriced
- the experience has improved and value signals are stronger
- you want to protect net for every order

Both are ethical.
Choose the tool that fits the reality.

---

## 14) Decoy design that is respectful (how to do it right)

Decoys become unethical only when they are deceptive.

Respectful decoy design:
- all options are real and deliver value
- the small option is truly smaller
- the premium option is truly better
- the standard option is the best “balanced” fit

You are not pushing customers.
You are giving them a clear ladder.

A clear ladder reduces “Should I?” stress.

---

## 15) The “menu architecture” that makes pricing feel normal

Pricing feels normal when the menu has structure:

- A few anchors
- A few add-ons
- One bundle
- One premium upgrade

Without structure, prices look random.

With structure, prices feel logical.

Logic reduces price resistance.

---

## 16) How pricing psychology helps you compete (without underpricing)

Takeaway often wins on habit.
Food trucks win on novelty.

You win by creating:
- clarity
- trust
- a clean ladder of choices

A ladder of choices makes customers feel:
“I can choose what fits me.”

That is customer respect.
That respect creates loyalty.

---

## 17) “First-time buyer” design (pricing should reduce fear)

Your structure should help first-time buyers feel safe:

- a clear standard option (“most popular”)
- a smaller option for light eaters (reduces fear)
- a premium option for big appetite or “best” seekers

When first-time buyers feel safe, they become repeat customers.

---

## 18) The owner win: better profit without more complexity

The goal of pricing psychology is not to sell more items.
It is to:
- increase order value naturally
- protect net profit
- keep the menu clean

Ethical structure is one of the simplest ways to do that.
