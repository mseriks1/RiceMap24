# Business Coach #21-A — Year Review And Year Plan (Repeat, Simplify, Invest)
## How to use a full-year lens to build a calmer, stronger business next year

A month is a moment.  
A quarter is direction.  
A year is identity.

A full-year review is where you stop thinking:
“Did I have a good week?”

…and start thinking:
**“What kind of business am I building?”**

This month is about doing a calm, owner-level Year Review and turning it into a Year Plan.

Not a complicated strategy document.
A clear plan built from your real data:
- Income / Expenses / Net
- period comparisons (Now vs Prev)
- top dishes and top customers (if linked)
- category trends (if used)
- exports and documentation
- your own capacity and quality standards

The goal is three words:
**Repeat. Simplify. Invest.**

Repeat what works.  
Simplify what creates drift.  
Invest where it makes next year easier.

---

## 1) The Year Review headline (the simplest starting point)

Start with the headline totals for the year:

- Total Income
- Total Expenses
- Total Net

This gives you the clean truth:
- what the business produced
- what it cost
- what stayed

Net is not a judgement.
Net is direction.

---

## 2) Break the year into “seasons” (quarter or half-year lenses)

A year can be noisy.
Breaking it into periods makes it readable.

Two easy options:
- Quarterly (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4)
- Half-year (H1 vs H2)

Inside Accounting & Insights:
- use “This year” where available
- use Custom ranges if needed
- use comparisons (Now vs Prev)

Then ask:
- Which period was strongest—and why?
- Which period had drift—and why?
- Which period felt most sustainable—and why?

This is the year becoming understandable.

---

## 3) Year patterns: what owners should look for

A full-year lens reveals four useful patterns:

### Pattern 1: Demand pattern
- Does demand spike at specific times?
- Does lunch become stronger in some seasons?
- Do weekends dominate?

### Pattern 2: Cost pattern
- Did packaging rise slowly?
- Did ingredients rise because of waste or price changes?
- Did transport become chaotic?

### Pattern 3: Product pattern
- Which dish stayed in the top list all year?
- Which dishes were seasonal spikes?
- Which dish became the true anchor?

### Pattern 4: Customer pattern
- Did top customers become stable?
- Did the repeat base grow?
- Did office customers become a segment?

Patterns are power.
Power creates calm decisions.

---

## 4) The Year Review method (clean and repeatable)

Use this method:

### Step A: Read the headline (Income/Expenses/Net)
Then compare:
- Q4 vs Q3
- H2 vs H1
- Last 30 days vs previous 30 days (for current momentum)

### Step B: Identify the “best stability engine”
Pick one:
- anchor dish
- lunch lane
- repeat customer base

### Step C: Identify the “biggest drift”
Usually one of:
- packaging drift
- ingredient waste drift
- transport drift
- too much variety drift

### Step D: Choose one simplification
Example:
- “One packaging standard per dish type.”
- “Anchors stay stable; only one rotating slot.”
- “One supply run rhythm per week.”

### Step E: Choose one investment (only if it reduces friction)
Examples:
- better sealing to prevent spills
- storage upgrade to reduce waste
- scale/portion system to protect consistency
- packaging station setup

This is the whole plan.
It’s not complicated.
It’s real.

---

## 5) Repeat: what to repeat next year (the best part)

Repeat means:
- protect what already works
- stop “reinventing” what doesn’t need change
- build mastery

Things worth repeating:
- anchor dish consistency
- sauce system identity
- lunch lane rhythm if it creates stability
- warm boundary scripts that reduce chaos
- logging habits that keep clarity strong

Repeat is how you become a brand.
Brands are simply promises customers trust.

---

## 6) Simplify: what to remove or reduce next year

Owners grow by subtraction as much as by addition.

Simplify is often:
- fewer packaging options
- fewer dish variations
- fewer special requests that destroy rhythm
- fewer unplanned supply runs
- fewer last-minute changes

Simplification protects:
- net profit
- quality
- time
- energy

Simplification is professionalism.

---

## 7) Invest: where to invest for next year (purpose only)

Invest is not “buy more.”
Invest is “reduce friction.”

A good investment does at least one of these:
- protects texture and reputation
- reduces waste
- improves portion consistency
- prevents spills
- makes packaging faster
- makes storage safer
- makes the business easier next month

If it doesn’t reduce friction, it’s not an investment.
It’s noise.

---

## 8) Make the Year Plan measurable (without pressure)

A strong plan has one measurable focus.

Choose one:
- stabilize net profit
- reduce packaging drift
- grow lunch lane
- strengthen repeat base
- improve product consistency for the anchor dish

Then use your tools:
- comparisons
- categories
- top lists
- exports

You don’t need daily tracking.
You need monthly clarity and quarterly review.

---

## Your Owner Standard for this month

**“I review my year to repeat what works, simplify what drifts, and invest with purpose.”**

That’s how next year becomes calmer and stronger.


---

## A year plan that respects your capacity (the hidden success factor)

A great plan is not the biggest plan.
It is the plan you can sustain.

So include your capacity truth:
- What level of volume feels healthy?
- What level creates stress?
- Which days are best for production?
- Which lane (lunch/dinner/weekend) fits your life?

Businesses last when they fit the owner’s real life.

Capacity planning is not weakness.
It is wisdom.

---

## Your final reminder

Year planning is not about predicting everything.
It is about building a system that handles reality.

Repeat. Simplify. Invest.
That is enough to build a strong year.

---

## 9) Build a “12-month dashboard” mindset (without extra work)

A year plan becomes easier when you think in a simple dashboard:

- **Monthly:** check Income/Expenses/Net (headline only)
- **Quarterly:** compare periods and pick one lever
- **Half-year:** check stability and drift in bigger picture
- **Yearly:** repeat/simplify/invest decisions

This is not a lot of work.
It is the rhythm that keeps owners calm.

---

## 10) Year Review: the “repeat list” (write it once)

Your repeat list is the heart of next year.

Examples of repeat items:
- your anchor dish stays stable
- your signature sauce stays consistent
- your packaging standard stays locked
- your warm boundary scripts stay consistent
- your supply run rhythm stays planned
- your income and expense logging stays consistent

Repeat list means:
the business stops being reinvented every month.

That is how brands are built.

---

## 11) Year Review: the “stop list” (where profit and calm come from)

Your stop list is the hidden profit plan.

Examples:
- stop buying random packaging types
- stop adding dish variations that increase waste
- stop accepting last-minute changes after confirmation
- stop unplanned supply trips caused by poor prep
- stop “special ingredients” that don’t pay back

Stop list is not negativity.
Stop list is professionalism.

---

## 12) Year Review: the “upgrade list” (investments that reduce friction)

Pick 1–3 upgrades max for the year.

Good upgrades:
- sealing system that eliminates spills
- storage that reduces spoilage
- scale/portion system for consistency
- packaging station setup (speed and calm)
- labels and organization that reduce mistakes

Upgrades should make next month easier.
If they don’t, they are not upgrades.

---

## 13) Turn your plan into one sentence (so it stays real)

A great year plan can be said in one sentence:

**“Next year we protect our anchor, reduce drift, and build stability through repeatable systems.”**

If you can say it, you can lead it.

---

## 14) The “year plan check-in” question

When you’re unsure what to do, ask:

**“Does this decision help our repeat list, stop list, or upgrade list?”**

If yes, it belongs.
If no, it’s noise.

---

## Your final Year Plan line

**“Next year will be built by repetition, simplification, and purpose—until the business feels stable.”**
