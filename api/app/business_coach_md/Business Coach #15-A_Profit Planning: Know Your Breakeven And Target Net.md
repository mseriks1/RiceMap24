# Business Coach #15-A — Profit Planning: Know Your Breakeven And Target Net
## A calm way to plan profit using your ledger—without complicated math

A lot of home kitchen owners work hard and still feel uncertain about money.

Not because they are doing something wrong.
But because they are missing one owner tool:

**Profit planning.**

Profit planning is not spreadsheets.
Profit planning is simply being able to answer two questions:

1) “What does it take for my kitchen to be worth it each month?”  
2) “What level of net profit am I aiming for?”

This month is about making that simple—using the data you already record:
- income
- expenses
- net

---

## 1) Breakeven: the minimum your kitchen must produce

Breakeven means:
**the point where the business covers its costs.**

When you understand breakeven, you stop guessing.

You can look at a month and say:
- “We covered costs.”
- “We are above breakeven.”
- “We need one improvement.”

### What counts as costs?
In a home kitchen business, your costs usually include:
- ingredients
- packaging
- transport
- small equipment and supplies
- any business-related services (where relevant)

This is why expense categories matter.
They make breakeven visible.

---

## 2) Target net: what you want to keep

Net is what stays after expenses.

A target net is simply a decision:
**“This is what I want to keep per month.”**

The point is not to set a perfect number.
The point is to set a direction.

A direction helps you choose:
- how focused your menu should be
- how efficient your prep must be
- what level of orders you need
- what investments are realistic

---

## 3) Use your real numbers (the calm method)

Instead of guessing costs, use the ledger.

Look at your last 30 days or last month:
- total income
- total expenses
- net

Then ask:
- “What expenses repeat every month?”
- “Which expenses were special (one-time)?”
- “What is my typical net when I run normally?”

This gives you a real baseline.

Baseline is power.

---

## 4) Simple target planning (no heavy formulas)

Here is a clean way to think:

**Target income = expenses + target net**

Example (simple):
- If monthly expenses average 10,000
- and you want 15,000 net
- then your target income is 25,000

This is not a promise.
It’s a direction.

Then you can ask:
- “What dishes make this easier?”
- “What order rhythm supports this?”
- “Where can I reduce waste?”
- “Which costs are rising?”

Now your business becomes manageable.

---

## 5) Where profit planning connects to Top dishes and Top customers

This is where your system becomes powerful:

- Top dishes helps you focus on what produces income reliably.
- Top customers helps you protect stability (repeat behavior).
- Expense categories help you see where net is leaking.

When you combine these three, you get a strong profit planning loop:

1) Focus on dishes that sell and package well  
2) Protect the customers who repeat  
3) Control cost drivers (ingredients/packaging/transport)  
4) Net becomes stronger over time  

That’s real ownership.

---

## 6) A practical “breakeven” way to think (without pressure)

Breakeven is not a fear.
Breakeven is clarity.

If you know:
- “My normal monthly expenses are about X”

…then you know what must be covered before the business feels stable.

Then everything above that becomes:
- reinvestment
- upgrades
- savings
- growth
- freedom

That’s why profit planning is motivating.
It gives you a path.

## Use the Ledger to build a simple “cost reality” picture

Breakeven is easiest when you stop guessing.

Open your Ledger and look at a realistic period (Last 30 days or Last month). Then ask:

### Which expenses repeat?
These are your “business must-pay” costs:
- ingredients purchases
- packaging restocks
- transport (fuel/runs)
- small supplies that happen every month

### Which expenses were one-time?
These are investments or exceptions:
- a new scale
- a one-time equipment purchase
- a larger packaging buy to stock up
- a special event purchase

This separation matters because:
- repeating costs determine your real breakeven
- one-time costs shouldn’t scare you if they’re strategic

---

## A simple “breakeven method” that works for most owners

If you want a calm method, do this:

1) Choose a month you consider “normal” (not extreme).  
2) Read total **Expenses** for that month.  
3) That total is your **breakeven baseline**.

Now you can lead:
- If income is below baseline → you know why it feels tight.
- If income is above baseline → you have room for net, upgrades, and savings.

No complex math needed.

---

## Target net: choose a number that supports your life

A good target net is connected to real life:
- how much time you are investing
- what your business should contribute
- whether you plan to reinvest or save
- your long-term plan (part-time vs full-time)

Your first target net doesn’t need to be big.
It needs to be meaningful.

Meaningful targets build direction.

---

## Convert monthly targets into weekly rhythm (so it feels doable)

If you like weekly thinking:

- Monthly expenses ÷ 4 ≈ weekly cost baseline  
- Monthly target net ÷ 4 ≈ weekly net goal  

Now you have a weekly picture without pressure:
- “This is the kind of week I’m building.”

Weekly rhythm reduces overwhelm.

## A small “order math” way to think (optional, but motivating)

If you ever want a simple way to visualize your plan:

- If your average order brings in about X in income
- and your target income is Y
- then you roughly need Y ÷ X orders in a month

This isn’t to pressure you.
It’s to give you a realistic picture of what your plan means in the real world.

Owners don’t fear numbers.
Owners use numbers to reduce uncertainty.
