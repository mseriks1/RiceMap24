# Business Coach #35-A — Fast Menu Decisions (What To Keep, Rotate, Or Remove) Using Your Data
## A calm owner method to keep the menu strong without emotional guesswork

Every growing kitchen reaches a moment where the menu needs decisions.

Not because anything is “wrong.”
But because menus drift.

- too many items
- too many variations
- too many ingredients
- too many packaging types
- too much time intensity

When menus drift, net profit usually drifts too.

The solution is not panic.
The solution is a calm menu decision system.

This month is about making fast, smart menu decisions using the data and signals you already have:
- Top dishes (if dish naming/lines are used)
- Top customers (repeat behavior)
- Income/Expenses/Net over time
- Category drift (ingredients/packaging/transport)
- Your own capacity truth (what you can sustain)

And remember the anchor rule:
**Never remove what sells well. Protect anchors.**

This method is not about cutting creativity.
It’s about protecting stability and profit.

---

## 1) Why menu decisions should be “system-based,” not mood-based

Mood-based menus sound like:
- “I’m tired of cooking this, so I’ll remove it.”
- “This new idea feels exciting, so I’ll add five things.”
- “Competitors have it, so I should too.”

System-based menus sound like:
- “This dish repeats and protects net, so it stays.”
- “This dish is loved but high-cost, so we redesign it.”
- “This dish doesn’t repeat and creates waste, so we rotate it or remove it.”

Systems remove emotional stress.
They also make your kitchen feel professional.

---

## 2) The three menu roles: Anchor, Rotation, Remove

Most menu decisions become easy when every dish has a role.

### Role 1: Anchor dish (always available)
Anchor dishes are:
- repeatable in taste and packaging
- loved and ordered repeatedly
- profitable enough to sustain

Anchors are your stability engine.

### Role 2: Rotation dish (limited, seasonal, controlled)
Rotation dishes are:
- creative and fun
- not always available
- designed to reuse core ingredients
- tested as “future anchors” sometimes

Rotation keeps the brand alive without creating chaos.

### Role 3: Remove (or special order only)
Remove decisions are not negative.
They are professional.

A dish should be removed or moved to special-order only when:
- it doesn’t repeat
- it requires unique ingredients that waste
- it is packaging-heavy and messy
- it is time-heavy and creates stress
- it creates complaints or inconsistency

Removal is often the fastest profit upgrade.

---

## 3) The “data first” menu decision flow (fast and simple)

Here is a calm flow you can repeat monthly or quarterly:

### Step 1: Look at Top dishes (Last 30 days / This month)
Ask:
- What repeats?
- What is rising?
- What disappeared?

### Step 2: Look at your Month overview (Income/Expenses/Net)
Ask:
- Is net improving or flat?
- Did expenses rise faster than income?

### Step 3: Check drift categories (Ingredients/Packaging/Transport)
Ask:
- which category is creeping?

### Step 4: Apply the role logic
- Keep anchors stable
- Limit rotation to one slot
- Remove or restrict dishes that create waste or chaos

This is enough to keep your menu profitable and calm.

---

## 4) The “never remove what sells” rule (and what to do instead)

Sometimes the dish you are tempted to remove is:
- your best seller
- your identity
- your anchor

If it sells and repeats, don’t remove it.

If it is stressful or low profit, redesign it:
- portion standard
- packaging redesign
- ingredient overlap
- time-saving prep steps
- add-on structure to increase order value

Anchors are worth redesigning.
They are not worth destroying.

---

## 5) The “Remove” decisions that make your business easier instantly

Here are common remove triggers:

- the dish requires a rare ingredient that spoils  
- the dish requires 4–5 packaging components  
- the dish causes leaks or soggy complaints repeatedly  
- the dish is hard to portion consistently  
- the dish is slow and disrupts production rhythm  
- the dish sells occasionally but not enough to justify complexity  

If you remove or restrict one dish like this, your whole week becomes easier.

Easier week = better quality.
Better quality = more repeat customers.
More repeat customers = better net.

---

## 6) Menu decisions should protect your identity (not only profit)

Identity matters.

You are not trying to become generic takeaway.

Your menu should reflect:
- your culture
- your flavor logic
- your signature elements (sauce, finishing style)
- your anchor dishes that customers love

So menu decisions should be:
- profitable
- repeatable
- culturally proud

This is how home kitchens become brands.

---

## 7) The “one change per period” discipline

To keep this calm:
make one menu change per period.

One change can be:
- promote one rotation dish to anchor
- remove one low-repeat dish
- redesign one stressful anchor

One change per period is enough.
It prevents chaos.
It creates measurable learning.

Menu discipline creates calmer weeks—and calmer weeks create better profit.

## 8) The “dish decision” scorecard (fast, owner-friendly)

To remove emotional guesswork, you can score each dish quickly (1–5):

### A) Repeat signal
How often does it repeat (based on your memory + Top dishes)?

### B) Complexity signal
How many unique ingredients and steps does it require?

### C) Packaging signal
Is it clean and repeatable, or messy and component-heavy?

### D) Profit signal
Does it feel like it pays you back (profit per dish thinking)?

### E) Identity signal
Does it represent your kitchen’s culture and signature style?

Then decide:
- High repeat + strong profit + strong identity → anchor
- Medium repeat + fun + manageable complexity → rotation
- Low repeat + high complexity/waste → remove or special-order only

This scorecard makes decisions calm and fast.

---

## 9) How to handle a dish that sells but causes stress

This happens a lot:
a dish sells, but it creates chaos.

Owner rule:
**If it sells, redesign first.**

Redesign levers:
- simplify steps (batch components)
- simplify ingredients (overlap system)
- simplify packaging (one standard)
- simplify add-ons (reduce components)
- standardize portions (profit protection)

If redesign doesn’t solve it, then you can move it to:
- rotation only
or
- special-order only

That protects both:
- customer trust
- owner sustainability

---

## 10) The “rotation discipline” that prevents menu drift

Rotation is powerful—but only if controlled.

A strong rotation discipline:
- only one rotation dish at a time
- rotation stays for a full period (customers learn it)
- rotation must reuse core ingredients
- rotation should be measurable (consistent dish name)

This creates:
- novelty without chaos
- learning without stress
- potential future anchors

---

## 11) Menu simplification usually increases quality

Owners fear simplification because they think:
“Customers want a big menu.”

Often customers want:
- a few strong winners
- consistency
- confidence

Simplification:
- reduces waste
- reduces mistakes
- improves speed
- improves portion consistency
- improves packaging reliability

That means:
better reviews, more repeats, stronger net.

---

## 12) One menu change can improve your whole quarter

This is why the “one change per period” rule works:
- promote one dish
- remove one dish
- redesign one dish

Small menu improvements compound into big stability.
